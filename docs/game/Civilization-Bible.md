# 1. Civilization Philosophy

## Why Civilizations Are the Backbone of the Game

Civilizations are not simply one category among many in Jolt Time — they are the **organizing principle** around which all historical content revolves. Every artifact belongs to a civilization. Every museum hall represents a civilization. Every expedition destination traces the path of a civilization through time.

Without civilizations, artifacts are isolated objects. With civilizations, artifacts become **meaningful narratives** — windows into how specific peoples lived, created, conquered, and contributed to the human story.

## Why History Is Told Through Civilizations

History is not a series of disconnected events. It is a **web of interconnected civilizations**, each influencing and being influenced by others. When a player collects an artifact from Ancient Egypt, they are not just acquiring an object — they are participating in a story that includes the Nile's annual floods, the construction of the pyramids, the trade routes to Mesopotamia, the conquests of Alexander, and the lasting legacy of Egyptian mathematics and medicine.

Civilizations provide the **context** that transforms collecting into education, and gameplay into understanding.

## The Three Pillars of Civilization Design

### Pillar 1: Authenticity
Every civilization in Jolt Time is represented with historical accuracy and respect. We do not invent, exaggerate, or distort. We present civilizations as they were — complex, flawed, innovative, and worthy of study.

### Pillar 2: Agency
Players do not passively observe civilizations — they **interact** with them. Expeditions take players to civilization sites. Artifacts reveal civilization secrets. Museums display civilization achievements. Every interaction deepens understanding.

### Pillar 3: Accessibility
Complex civilizations are made accessible without being oversimplified. A player should be able to enjoy Jolt Time casually while still absorbing genuine historical knowledge. Deep dives are available for those who seek them.

# 2. Civilization Hierarchy

The Civilization Hierarchy provides the **structural framework** through which all historical content is organized, discovered, and experienced.

```
WORLD
  │
  ▼
REGION
  │
  ▼
CIVILIZATION
  │
  ▼
ERA
  │
  ▼
DYNASTY
  │
  ▼
CULTURE
```

## Level 1: World

The **World** is the sum total of human history — all civilizations, all eras, all achievements, all connections. In Jolt Time, the World serves as the ultimate container and the player's ultimate goal: to collect and preserve the heritage of all human civilization.

## Level 2: Region

**Regions** are geographical and cultural zones that contain related civilizations. Regions provide high-level organization and help players navigate the vast landscape of human history.

| Region | Geographic Scope |
|--------|-----------------|
| Africa | Continental Africa, including Egypt, North Africa, Sub-Saharan Africa |
| Europe | Western, Central, Eastern, and Northern Europe |
| Asia | East Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, Central Asia |
| Middle East | The Levant, Arabian Peninsula, Persia, Anatolia |
| North America | Pre-Columbian North America, Colonial America, United States |
| South America | Pre-Columbian South America, Colonial South America |
| Oceania | Australia, Pacific Islands, New Zealand |
| Polar Regions | Arctic, Antarctic (human exploration and indigenous peoples) |

## Level 3: Civilization

A **Civilization** is a distinct human society with shared identity, territory, culture, and historical trajectory. Civilizations are the primary unit of organization in Jolt Time — the entity to which artifacts belong, the subject of museum halls, and the focus of expeditions.

Examples: Ancient Egypt, Roman Empire, Han Dynasty, Aztec Empire

## Level 4: Era

An **Era** is a defined period within a civilization's history, characterized by distinct cultural, political, or technological developments. Eras allow for fine-grained temporal organization within civilizations.

Examples: Old Kingdom Egypt, Imperial Rome, Tang Dynasty China

## Level 5: Dynasty

A **Dynasty** is a sequence of rulers from the same family or line within an era. Dynasties provide the most granular level of temporal organization and often correspond to distinct artistic, architectural, or cultural movements.

Examples: Eighteenth Dynasty Egypt (Thutmose, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun), Julio-Claudian Dynasty Rome

## Level 6: Culture

**Culture** refers to specific traditions, artistic movements, religious practices, or technological achievements within a dynasty or civilization. Culture provides thematic organization for artifacts and exhibits.

Examples: court culture, religious art, military tradition, maritime culture

# 4. Civilization Metadata

Each civilization in Jolt Time contains a comprehensive metadata profile that provides essential information and ensures accurate representation.

## Metadata Schema

```typescript
interface CivilizationMetadata {
  // Basic Identification
  historicalName: string;           // The name as historically used
  alternativeNames: string[];        // Common alternative names and translations
  region: Region;                    // Geographic region classification
  capital: string[];                 // Primary and secondary capitals
  
  // Temporal Data
  timeline: {
    startYear: number;               // Year civilization begins (negative for BCE)
    endYear: number;                 // Year civilization ends (negative for BCE)
    peakYear?: number;               // Year of maximum territorial/cultural influence
  };
  
  // Cultural Data
  languages: string[];               // Primary languages spoken
  religion: string[];                // Primary religious practices
  culture: string[];                 // Cultural characteristics
  
  // Historical Context
  achievements: string[];            // Major accomplishments
  legacy: string;                    // Long-term historical impact
  
  // Relationships
  predecessor?: string;              // Civilization this evolved from
  successor?: string;                // Civilization that evolved from this
  contemporaries: string[];          // Civilizations existing simultaneously
}
```

# 5. Civilization Identity

Each civilization possesses a distinct **Identity Profile** that defines its visual, cultural, and thematic presence in Jolt Time. This identity informs all visual design, artifact descriptions, museum layouts, and expedition environments.

## 5.1 Identity Dimensions

### Visual Identity

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Color Palette** | Civilization-specific color schemes drawn from historical pigments, materials, and artistic traditions |
| **Symbolic Motifs** | recurring patterns, glyphs, and designs that evoke the civilization |
| **Iconography** | Recognizable symbols (hieroglyphs, emblems, totems) |
| **Typography** | When applicable, period-appropriate writing styles |

# 6. Civilization Relationships

Civilizations do not exist in isolation. They interact, influence, and shape each other through complex webs of relationship. Jolt Time models these relationships to create a **living historical ecosystem**.

## 6.1 Relationship Types

### Trade Relationships

Trade relationships connect civilizations through commerce, resource exchange, and economic interdependence.

| Relationship | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Direct Trade** | Silk Road between Han Dynasty and Roman Empire |
| **Regional Trade** | Mediterranean trade network connecting Greece, Rome, Carthage, Phoenicia |
| **Luxury Trade** | Phoenician purple dye trade throughout the ancient world |
| **Essential Trade** | Salt trade in Ancient Egypt and Sub-Saharan Africa |

### Military Conflicts

Wars and conquests shape civilization trajectories and artifact distribution.

| Relationship | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Conquest** | Roman conquest of Greece, Carthage, Egypt |
| **Defensive** | Greek city-states vs. Persian Empire |
| **Expansion** | Mongol conquest across Asia and Europe |
| **Jihad/Crusade** | Islamic expansion, European Crusades |

Political and military alliances create bonds between civilizations.

| **Marriage Alliance** | Habsburg marriages shaping European dynasties |
| **Satellite State** | Roman client kingdoms |

### Cultural Influence

Ideas, art, religion, and customs spread between civilizations.

| Relationship | Example |

| **Artistic Influence** | Roman art influenced by Greek models |
| **Technological Transfer** | Papermaking from China to Islamic world to Europe |

### Knowledge Exchange

Scientific, mathematical, and philosophical knowledge crosses civilization boundaries.

| Relationship | Example |

# 7. Timeline Rules

The Timeline Rules establish how civilizations relate temporally, ensuring accurate representation of historical overlap, transition, and progression.

## Rule 1: Civilization Overlap

Multiple civilizations often exist simultaneously. Players can expedition to any active civilization at any given time.

| Time Period | Active Civilizations |
|-------------|---------------------|
| 500 BCE | Greece, Persia, Carthage, Egypt, China (Warring States), India (Mahajanapadas) |
| 100 CE | Rome, Han Dynasty, Parthia, Kush, Mesoamerica (Classic period) |
| 1200 CE | Song Dynasty, Ayyubid Dynasty, Kingdom of England, Inca, Crusader States |
| 1800 CE | British Empire, French Republic, Qing Dynasty, Ottoman Empire, United States |

## Rule 2: Era Continuity

Civilizations evolve through eras that maintain cultural continuity while enabling distinct identity shifts.

**Example: Rome**
```
Roman Kingdom (753–509 BCE)
  ↓
Roman Republic (509–27 BCE)
  ↓
Roman Empire (27 BCE–476 CE)
  ↓
Western Roman Empire (476–1453 CE) ← Byzantine Empire continues
```

**Example: China**
```
Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors (mythical)
  ↓
Xia Dynasty (c. 2070–1600 BCE)
  ↓
Shang Dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE)
  ↓
Zhou Dynasty (c. 1046–256 BCE)
  ↓
Qin Dynasty (221–206 BCE)
  ↓
Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE)
  ↓
[Interregnum periods]
  ↓
Sui Dynasty (581–618 CE)
  ↓
Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE)
  ↓
Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE)
```

## Rule 3: Transition Rules

Civilizations do not end abruptly. Transitions follow patterns:

| Transition Type | Definition | Example |
|----------------|-----------|---------|
| **Conquest** | Absorbed by another civilization | Persia → Alexander's Empire → Seleucid Empire |
| **Evolution** | Transforms into successor state | Roman Republic → Roman Empire |
| **Fragmentation** | Splits into multiple states | Alexander's Empire → Diadochi kingdoms |
| **Collapse** | Rapid decline without clear successor | Bronze Age Collapse |
| **Integration** | Absorbed into larger civilization | Celtic tribes → Roman Empire |

## Rule 4: Temporal Boundaries

Civilizations have defined start and end dates but context may extend presence.

| Field | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **Active Period** | Time when civilization exercises territorial/political control |
| **Cultural Legacy** | Time after political end when culture continues to influence |
| **Archaeological Period** | Time when civilization's material culture is present |

**Example: Ancient Egypt**
- Active Period: 3100 BCE – 30 BCE
- Cultural Legacy: Continues through Ptolemaic, Roman, Coptic periods
- Archaeological Period: Archaeological evidence extends through modern times

# 9. Artifact Ownership Rules

Artifacts are primary archaeological or historical objects that belonged to or were produced by a specific civilization. Ownership rules ensure accurate historical representation.

## Rule 1: Primary Ownership

Every artifact belongs primarily to one civilization — the civilization that created it, used it, or with which it is most associated.

| Artifact | Primary Owner | Rationale |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Rosetta Stone | Ancient Egypt | Created in Egypt, hieroglyphic text |
| Terracotta Army | Qin Dynasty | Created for Emperor Qin |
| Parthenon Marble | Ancient Greece | Created in Athens for the Parthenon |

## Rule 2: Shared History Documentation

When artifacts have complex histories involving multiple civilizations, ownership includes documented provenance.

**Example: The Silk Road Manuscripts**
```
Primary Owner: Tang Dynasty
Secondary Involvement: Persia (Sasanian Empire), India (Gupta)
Shared History:
- Created in Tang China (7th century CE)
- Traded along Silk Road
- Sasanian influence in design elements
- Acquired by Japanese monastery (9th century)
- Current location: Japan
```

## Rule 3: Conquest and Plunder

Artifacts obtained through conquest carry complex ownership implications.

**Example: Nebra Sky Disk**
```
Primary Owner: Bronze Age Central Europe
Conquest History: Possibly obtained through trade/raid by Únětice culture
Current Custody: Germany
Representation: Bronze Age Central Europe with provenance note
```

## Rule 4: Colonial Context

Artifacts with colonial acquisition histories require sensitive, accurate representation.

**Example: Benin Bronzes**
```
Primary Owner: Benin Kingdom
Colonial Context: Looted by British Empire (1897)
Ongoing Debate: Repatriation discussions
Museum Display: Benin Kingdom hall with colonial context
```

## Rule 5: Unknown Provenance

Artifacts without clear ownership documentation are temporarily assigned to most likely civilization with provenance noted.

# 10. Educational Philosophy

Every civilization in Jolt Time serves as a vehicle for education. Players learn history not through lectures but through **discovery, collection, and curation**.

## 10.1 Educational Dimensions

### History

Each civilization teaches its historical narrative:
- Key events and turning points
- Rise and fall patterns
- Major leaders and figures
- Conflict and resolution

### Culture

Civilizations reveal cultural richness:
- Art and artistic traditions
- Music and performance
- Food and cuisine
- Clothing and fashion
- Social customs and traditions

### Science

Scientific achievements demonstrate human innovation:
- Mathematical discoveries
- Astronomical knowledge
- Medical practices
- Engineering feats
- Agricultural innovations

### Technology

Technological artifacts showcase human ingenuity:
- Tool and instrument creation
- Material science (metallurgy, ceramics, textiles)
- Construction techniques
- Transportation and communication

### Art

Artistic achievements express civilization values:
- Visual arts (painting, sculpture, architecture)
- Literary arts (poetry, drama, philosophy)
- Decorative arts (metals, textiles, ceramics)

### Society

Civilizations reveal social organization:
- Government and law
- Family and kinship

# 14. Things We NEVER Do

These are inviolable principles that define Jolt Time's approach to civilization representation.

## We NEVER Create Fictional Civilizations

| ❌ Never | ✅ Always |
|---------|----------|
| Invent fictional empires | Use only historically documented civilizations |
| Create "generic fantasy" cultures | Research and accurately represent each civilization |
| Mix fictional elements with real | Maintain clear boundaries between game mechanics and historical content |

**Rationale:** Players trust Jolt Time to teach real history. Invented civilizations undermine educational value and player trust.

## We NEVER Present Mythology as History

| ❌ Never | ✅ Always |
|---------|----------|
| Present mythological events as historical fact | Clearly distinguish mythology from historical record |
| Depict gods as historical figures | Show mythological narratives in their proper context |
| Confuse religious texts with historical evidence | Acknowledge what is known, debated, and legendary |

**Rationale:** Mythology is valuable cultural content, but it must be presented as mythology, not history. Players deserve to understand the distinction.

## We NEVER Produce Political Propaganda

| ❌ Never | ✅ Always |
|---------|----------|
| Glorify colonialism or imperialism | Present conquest with appropriate context |
| Demonize civilizations for political purposes | Show complexity, nuance, and multiple perspectives |
| Rewrite history to suit modern political agendas | Present history as it was understood at the time |

**Rationale:** Jolt Time educates, not indoctrinates. Players form their own opinions based on accurate historical presentation.

## We NEVER Engage in Historical Revisionism

| ❌ Never | ✅ Always |
|---------|----------|
| Downplay atrocities | Present uncomfortable truths with appropriate gravity |
| Exaggerate accomplishments | Acknowledge both achievements and failures honestly |
| Ignore documented historical consensus | Follow scholarly consensus except where debate is explicitly noted |

**Rationale:** Historical accuracy is non-negotiable. Revisionism — regardless of motivation — betrays the educational mission.

## We NEVER Appropriate or Co-opt Sacred Content

| ❌ Never | ✅ Always |
|---------|----------|
| Use sacred objects frivolously | Treat religious artifacts with respect |
| Mock or trivialize religious practices | Show religious traditions in their proper context |
| Claim ownership of cultural heritage inappropriately | Acknowledge living cultural connections |

**Rationale:** Many civilizations have living cultural descendants who deserve respect. Sacred content requires special care.

# 15. Civilization Manifesto

## Why Preserving Every Civilization Matters

Every civilization that has ever existed represents a unique experiment in human organization, creativity, and survival. The Sumerians invented writing. The Egyptians built monuments that still inspire awe. The Greeks developed democracy and philosophy. The Chinese invented paper and gunpowder. The Islamic world preserved and advanced classical knowledge during Europe's Dark Ages. The Vikings explored continents before Columbus. The list is endless — and every entry on that list matters.

When a civilization disappears, it does not simply vanish. It leaves behind artifacts, ideas, technologies, and genetic legacies that persist for millennia. Modern humanity is the product of every civilization that came before — every innovation, every trade route, every war, every marriage alliance, every cultural exchange.

Jolt Time exists because we believe **no civilization should be forgotten**. We believe that understanding where we came from helps us understand where we are going. We believe that the Phoenician alphabet's descendants should know their debt to Tyre and Sidon. We believe that the scientific method's heirs should know Al-Khwarizmi and Ibn al-Haytham. We believe that every child who plays this game should emerge knowing that humanity's story is richer, more interconnected, and more inspiring than they imagined.

## Why Every Culture Deserves Representation

Every culture that has ever existed represents a unique answer to the question: *What does it mean to be human?*

The Māori answer differs from the Roman answer. The Han Chinese answer differs from the Aztec answer. These answers are not better or worse — they are different, and the differences teach us about human potential.

Jolt Time does not rank civilizations. We do not declare some "great" and others "minor." Every civilization in this game receives the same care, the same respect, the same dedication to accuracy. The Benin Kingdom's brasswork receives the same treatment as Rome's architecture. The Mississippian culture's mound-building receives the same treatment as Egypt's pyramids.

This is not relativism. It is **historical justice**. It is the recognition that for thousands of years, human beings looked at the night sky and wondered, and built temples and calendars and cities and poems, and every single one of those acts of creation deserves to be remembered.

## Our Commitment

Jolt Time is more than a game. It is a **living archive of human achievement**.

We commit to:
- Representing every civilization with accuracy and respect
- Teaching history as it was, not as we wish it had been
- Honoring both the triumphs and the tragedies
- Connecting civilizations through their shared humanity
- Inspiring curiosity about the peoples who came before us
- Building a museum without walls, where every player becomes a curator of human heritage
