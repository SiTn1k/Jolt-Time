# 1. Artifact Creation Philosophy

## Artifacts Are Historical

Every artifact in Jolt Time represents something that actually existed. We do not create fictional objects. We rediscover real ones.

**The Standard:**
An artifact must have existed in physical reality. It must have been created by real people, for real purposes, in a real time and place.

**The Promise:**
When a player holds an artifact in Jolt Time, they hold a piece of history. Not a fantasy. Not a approximation. Real history.

## Artifacts Are Educational

Every artifact teaches something true about the past.

**The Standard:**
The primary purpose of every artifact is education. Players should finish reading an artifact's description knowing something they did not know before.

**The Promise:**
Jolt Time is an educational entertainment product. Every artifact fulfills this mission.

## Artifacts Are Authentic

Every artifact has been verified by historical sources.

**The Standard:**
Claims about artifacts must be supported by credible sources. Museum records, academic publications, archaeological reports — these are our authorities.

**The Promise:**
We do not repeat unverified claims. We do not spread historical misinformation. Every fact we present can be traced to a reliable source.

## Artifacts Are Meaningful

Every artifact connects to something larger.

**The Standard:**
An artifact is not an isolated object. It belongs to a civilization, an era, a collection, a story. Every artifact we create connects players to the greater narrative of human history.

**The Promise:**
No artifact exists without context. No artifact is included without purpose.

## Artifacts Are Never Random Collectibles

We do not create random drops. We curate historical discoveries.

**The Standard:**
Every artifact enters the game because it has historical significance. Rarity reflects real-world scarcity and museum importance — not arbitrary game mechanics.

**The Promise:**
The Roman Gladius is rare because Roman weapons rarely survive. The Clay Tablet is rare because most have crumbled to dust. Real scarcity, real significance.

## Stage 1: Research

**Objective:** Identify candidates and gather information.

**Activities:**
- Identify artifact based on civilization, era, collection gaps
- Research preliminary information from secondary sources
- Assess whether sufficient information exists for creation
- Flag if additional expert consultation needed

**Deliverable:** Research brief with source list and key facts.

## Stage 2: Verification

**Objective:** Confirm the artifact's historical existence.

**Activities:**
- Verify museum records or archaeological documentation
- Confirm the artifact exists in physical form somewhere
- Check for any disputed authenticity issues
- Identify any replicas or forgeries to distinguish from

**Deliverable:** Verification confirmation with source citations.

## Stage 3: Historical Validation

**Objective:** Ensure all claims are accurate.

**Activities:**
- Expert review of all factual claims
- Source verification for every piece of information
- Cross-reference multiple sources where possible
- Document any areas of historical uncertainty

**Deliverable:** Validated fact sheet with source documentation.

## Stage 4: Writing

**Objective:** Create engaging, educational content.

**Activities:**
- Draft official name and display name
- Write historical description (2-3 paragraphs)
- Write educational summary (1 paragraph)
- Write historical importance section
- Write discovery story if applicable
- List interesting facts (3-5 items)
- Develop cross-collection connections

**Deliverable:** Complete artifact content package.

## Stage 5: Review

**Objective:** Quality assurance before publication.

**Activities:**
- Editorial review for grammar and style
- Historical accuracy final check
- Educational value assessment
- Age-appropriateness verification
- Localization readiness check

**Deliverable:** Approved content package.

## Stage 6: Museum Assignment

**Objective:** Place artifact in appropriate museum.

**Activities:**
- Select primary museum
- Verify museum theme match
- Confirm museum collection compatibility
- Assign wing and hall if applicable

**Deliverable:** Museum assignment confirmation.

## Stage 7: Collection Assignment

**Objective:** Place artifact in appropriate collection.

**Activities:**
- Select primary collection
- Verify collection narrative fit
- Confirm artifact relationships within collection
- Assign to artifact set (Core/Supporting/Bonus)

**Deliverable:** Collection assignment confirmation.

## Stage 8: Publication

**Objective:** Release artifact to game.

**Activities:**
- Final quality check
- Database entry creation
- Asset integration (images, 3D models)
- Metadata population
- Game release

**Deliverable:** Live artifact in game.

# 4. Historical Authenticity Rules

## The Foundation of Trust

Historical authenticity is not a feature — it is a requirement. Every artifact in Jolt Time must meet these standards.

## Rule 1: Artifacts Must Exist in Reality

**The artifact must have existed in physical reality.**

- It was created by human hands
- It served a real purpose
- It existed at a specific time and place

**Exceptions:** None. No fictional artifacts under any circumstances.

## Rule 2: Artifacts Must Be Historically Documented

**The artifact must have reliable historical documentation.**

Acceptable documentation:
- Museum records and catalogs
- Archaeological excavation reports
- Peer-reviewed academic publications
- Academic textbooks
- Primary source documents

**Unacceptable:** Wikipedia alone, fan sites, speculative blogs, unverifiable claims.

## Rule 3: Artifacts Must Be Verifiable

**Claims about artifacts must be independently verifiable.**

- If a museum claims an artifact exists, verify with the museum
- If an academic paper describes an artifact, verify with the publication
- If an artifact is disputed, note the dispute and represent fairly

**Rule:** When in doubt, don't include. Omission is safer than misinformation.

## Rule 4: Artifacts Are Never Fictional

**We never create fictional artifacts, even if they seem plausible.**

The following are **NEVER** created:
- Composite artifacts (mixing features of multiple real artifacts)
- Representative artifacts (creating a "typical" example)
- Placeholder artifacts (filling gaps with hypothetical objects)
- Upgraded artifacts (creating a "better version" of a known item)

**Exception:** Damaged or destroyed artifacts may be described based on reliable historical records even if no physical example survives.

# 5. Writing Rules

## Content Creation Standards

Every piece of text in Jolt Time meets these standards.

## Descriptions Must Be Engaging

**Players read because they want to, not because they must.**

- Use active voice
- Create visual imagery
- Tell a story, not just facts
- Make the reader feel present in history

**Example:**
> ❌ "The gladius is a sword used by Roman infantry."
> ✅ "In the hands of a legionary, the gladius was an extension of Roman power. Short enough to use in tight formation, sharp enough to end a battle with a single thrust, this blade conquered the Mediterranean world."

## Descriptions Must Be Easy to Understand

**Complex history is accessible history.**

- Avoid jargon without explanation
- Define technical terms inline
- Use concrete examples
- Break complex ideas into digestible pieces

**Example:**
> ❌ "The Lorica segmentata provided weighted thoracic protection."
> ✅ "The lorica segmentata was armor made of iron strips joined together. It protected the soldier's chest and back while allowing enough flexibility to fight."

## Descriptions Must Be Historically Correct

**Accuracy is non-negotiable.**

- Every fact is verified
- Every claim is sourced
- Uncertainty is acknowledged
- Debates are represented fairly

**Example:**
> ❌ "The Great Pyramid was built by slaves."
> ✅ "Recent archaeological evidence suggests the Great Pyramid was built by paid workers, not slaves. These workers were organized into teams and received regular food rations."

## Descriptions Must Be Suitable for All Ages

**Jolt Time is for everyone.**

- No graphic violence without historical context
- No sexual content
- No religious offense
- No political controversy without educational purpose

# 6. Educational Structure

## Every Artifact Answers

Each artifact is designed to teach through discovery.

## What Is It?

**The fundamental identification.**

> "This is a Roman gladius — a short sword used by the infantry of the Roman Republic and Empire."

## Who Created It?

**The human element.**

> "Roman smiths forged these swords in specialized workshops. Each blade bore the mark of its maker, a point of pride and accountability."

## When?

**The temporal context.**

> "The gladius evolved during the 3rd century BCE and remained standard until the 3rd century CE — a weapon that lasted longer than many empires."

## Where?

**The geographical context.**

> "Gladius blades have been found across the Roman world, from the forts of Britain to the sands of North Africa."

## Why?

**The purpose and meaning.**

> "The gladius was designed for close combat. In the tight formations of the legion, a long sword was useless. The gladius was Rome's answer to the question: how do you conquer the world?"

## How Was It Used?

**The practical application.**

> "Soldiers trained for years to master the gladius. The thrust was the primary attack — quick, deadly, and suited to formation fighting."

## Why Does It Matter Today?

**The lasting legacy.**

> "The Roman legionary system shaped military organization for two millennia. Modern armies still use the legion's principles of training, discipline, and engineering support."

# 7. Source Requirements

## Credible Sources Only

We cite only sources that meet these standards.

## Preferred Sources

### Museums

- Major national museums (Smithsonian, British Museum, Louvre)
- Archaeological museums at excavation sites
- University museums with academic oversight
- Recognized museum catalogs and publications

### Universities

- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- University press publications
- Academic conference proceedings
- University excavation reports

### Archaeological Publications

- Journal of Archaeological Science
- American Journal of Archaeology
- Antiquity
- Cambridge Archaeological Journal

### Recognized Historical Institutions

- National historical societies
- UNESCO World Heritage documentation
- Smithsonian Institution publications
- National Geographic historical content

## Acceptable Sources

- Well-sourced academic textbooks
- Encyclopedia Britannica (for basic facts)
- Major documentary productions (BBC, PBS, NOVA)
- Primary source translations with academic annotation

## Unacceptable Sources

- Wikipedia as primary source (acceptable as starting point only)
- Popular history blogs without academic credentials
- Speculative or fringe theories
- Unverified online claims
- Commercial websites without academic backing

# 8. Naming Rules

## Consistent Identification

Every artifact has multiple names for different purposes.

## Historical Name

The name used historically for the artifact.

**Purpose:** Accuracy and authenticity.

**Example:** "Lorica segmentata"

**Rule:** Use the historically documented name, even if it differs from common usage.

## Display Name

The name shown to players in the game.

**Purpose:** Clarity and engagement.

**Example:** "Roman Lorica Segmentata"

**Rule:** Should be immediately understandable to general audiences while preserving historical accuracy.

## Original Language Name

The name in the original language (if applicable).

**Purpose:** Cultural authenticity and educational value.

**Example:** "盔甲" (Chinese for armor)

**Rule:** Always include when the original language differs from English.

## Internal Identifier

The unique system identifier for the artifact.

**Purpose:** Technical tracking and database management.

**Example:** "ROM_MIL_001"

**Rule:** Consistent format across all artifacts. Format: [CIVILIZATION]_[CATEGORY]_[NUMBER]

# 10. Forbidden Content

## Content We Never Create

The following are absolutely prohibited.

## ❌ Fictional Artifacts

We never create artifacts that did not exist.

**Examples of Forbidden Content:**
- "The Dragon Sword of Genghis Khan" (no such specific sword exists)
- "The Magical Amulet of Cleopatra" (magical claims unverifiable)
- "The Ultimate Roman Gladius" (composite/hypothetical)

## ❌ Myths Presented as Facts

We never present mythological content as historical fact.

**Examples of Forbidden Content:**
- Presenting King Arthur's sword as a documented historical artifact
- Claiming the Trojan Horse was a real wooden horse
- Stating the Bible's Flood story as literal historical event

**Note:** Myths can be included if clearly labeled as mythology and within educational context.

## ❌ Conspiracy Theories

We never include content based on conspiracy theories or fringe claims.

**Examples of Forbidden Content:**
- Moon landing denial artifacts
- Ancient astronaut "evidence"
- Holocaust denial materials
- Flat earth claims

## ❌ Propaganda

We never create content that spreads political propaganda or hateful ideologies.

**Examples of Forbidden Content:**
- Pure Nazi propaganda artifacts without educational context
- Pure Soviet propaganda artifacts without critical framing
- Content promoting slavery or genocide

**Note:** Historical artifacts associated with problematic periods can be included with proper educational framing.

## ❌ Unverifiable Claims

We never include claims that cannot be verified by credible sources.

**Examples of Forbidden Content:**
- Specific details without source documentation
- Quotes that cannot be attributed
- "Lost civilization" claims without archaeological support
- Alien contact evidence

# 11. Future Scalability

## Framework Must Support

The artifact creation framework is designed for indefinite expansion.

## Scale Targets

| Dimension | Year 1 | Year 3 | Year 5 |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|
| **Civilizations** | 20 | 50 | 100 |
| **Museums** | 10 | 50 | 100 |
| **Collections** | 100 | 500 | 1,000 |
| **Artifacts** | 500 | 3,000 | 10,000+ |

## Scalability Principles

### Standardization Without Constraint

All artifacts follow the same field structure regardless of civilization or era. This enables:
- Consistent database queries
- Uniform quality standards
- Efficient content pipelines

### Extensible Metadata

New fields can be added to artifacts without breaking existing data:
- Backward-compatible schema changes
- Optional fields for specialized content
- Version tracking for field evolution

### Modular Collections

Collections are containers that scale infinitely:
- No hard limits on collections per museum
- No hard limits on artifacts per collection
- No hard limits on museums in the game

### Pipeline Parallelization

The creation pipeline supports parallel workflows:
- Multiple researchers working simultaneously
- Multiple editors reviewing simultaneously
- Multiple artifacts in pipeline simultaneously

# 12. Localization Rules

## Global Accessibility

Artifact content must be created for global audiences.

## Multi-Language Support

### Content Design

All artifact content is written with localization in mind:

**Do:**
- Use simple sentence structures that translate well
- Avoid idioms and cultural references that don't translate
- Write clear, unambiguous prose
- Use terms that have established translations in historical contexts

**Don't:**
- Use humor that depends on cultural context
- Create puns or wordplay
- Reference local customs without explanation
- Assume reading level equivalence across languages

### Field Handling

| Field | Localization Approach |
|-------|---------------------|
| Official Name | Translate with academic consultation |
| Display Name | Full localization |
| Original Name | Keep original language, add transliteration |
| Historical Description | Full localization |
| Educational Summary | Full localization |
| Sources | Maintain original language citations |

### Quality Assurance

Localized content must preserve:
- Historical accuracy (facts don't change)
- Educational value (teaching points maintained)
- Engagement quality (writing remains compelling)
- Cultural sensitivity (localization doesn't introduce offense)

# 13. AI Writing Guidelines

## Future AI-Generated Content

As AI writing tools improve, they may assist with artifact content creation. All AI-generated content must meet the same standards.

## Required Human Oversight

AI-generated content is **never** published without human review.

**Required Process:**
1. AI generates draft content
2. Human reviews for accuracy
3. Human verifies all claims
4. Human approves for publication

## Standards Apply Equally

AI-generated content must meet **all** existing standards:

- Historical accuracy (verified by human)
- Educational value (assessed by human)
- Source documentation (verified by human)
- Quality checklist (completed by human)
- Forbidden content rules (enforced by human)

## Prompt Engineering Requirements

When using AI for drafting:

**The prompt must include:**
- Full artifact metadata
- Source materials to reference
- Writing style guidelines
- Quality standards
- Forbidden content restrictions

**Example Prompt Structure:**
```
Create artifact content for:
- Artifact: [full metadata]
- Sources: [source list]
- Style: [guidelines]
- Standards: [quality checklist]
- Restrictions: [forbidden content]
```

## Continuous Improvement

AI tools improve through:
- Tracking AI content that required significant revision
- Refining prompts based on revision history
- Identifying patterns in successful AI-assisted content
- Maintaining human expertise as the quality benchmark

# 14. Things We NEVER Do

## Inviolable Creation Standards

### ❌ Duplicate Artifacts

Every artifact is unique. We never create two artifacts representing the same historical object.

**Rule:** If an artifact exists in the game, no second artifact with the same identity is created.

### ❌ Invent Historical Facts

We never make up facts, even small ones.

**Rule:** Every piece of information in every artifact description must come from a credible source.

### ❌ Simplify History Incorrectly

We never oversimplify to the point of misrepresentation.

**Rule:** Simplification must preserve essential truth. If a complex topic cannot be simplified accurately, it must be marked for advanced content only.

### ❌ Sacrifice Accuracy for Gameplay

Gameplay considerations never override historical accuracy.

**Rule:** If gameplay requirements conflict with historical accuracy, accuracy wins. Gameplay is adjusted.

### ❌ Include Unverified Content

We never include content we aren't certain about.

**Rule:** When in doubt, leave it out. Omission is safer than misinformation.

### ❌ Skip the Pipeline

Every artifact follows the full creation pipeline.

**Rule:** No exceptions. No shortcuts. Every artifact goes through every stage.

### ❌ Ignore Corrections

When errors are identified, they are corrected.

**Rule:** Historical accuracy is more important than ego. Errors are fixed immediately.

# 15. Artifact Creation Manifesto

## Why Every Artifact Inside Jolt Time Should Feel Like Discovering a Real Piece of Human History

---

**A museum is only as good as its artifacts. And an artifact is only as good as its truth.**

When a player discovers the Rosetta Stone in Jolt Time, they should feel what Jean-François Champollion felt when he finally decoded its hieroglyphs — the thrill of understanding something that humanity had lost. When a player holds a piece of the Antikythera Mechanism, they should sense what the ancient Greek sailors felt when they looked up at the stars, calculating their position with bronze and ingenuity.

This is not a game about collecting digital objects. This is a game about touching history.

**Every artifact is a survivor.**

Most things made by humans are destroyed. Buildings collapse. Books burn. Cloth rots. Metal rusts. The vast majority of everything humanity has ever created is gone — recycled by entropy back into the earth.

But sometimes, rarely, something survives. A pot shard in the dirt. A coin in the mud. A carving on a temple wall. These fragments are all that remain of worlds that no longer exist.

When we add an artifact to Jolt Time, we honor its survival. We say: you endured. You made it through millennia to reach us. We will not let you be forgotten.

**Every artifact is a teacher.**

These objects were made by people who had no idea that someone like you would be holding them four thousand years later. The potter who shaped the clay had no idea his pot would teach schoolchildren about ancient trade routes. The scribe who pressed his stylus into wet clay had no idea his words would reveal how people worshipped, how they loved, how they lived.

When we create an artifact entry, we become translators. We take the mute testimony of objects and give them voices. We say: this is what you were. This is what you meant. This is why you matter.

**Every artifact is a connection.**

You cannot hold the Rosetta Stone without thinking of Napoleon. You cannot see the Antikythera Mechanism without wondering about the ship that carried it. You cannot touch an Egyptian mummy without feeling the weight of four thousand years of human belief in the afterlife.

This is what makes artifacts different from any other kind of collectible. They are not just rare. They are not just valuable. They are connected to everything else. They are nodes in the web of human experience.

When we add an artifact to Jolt Time, we add it to that web. We show the connections. We make the web visible.

**Every artifact is a promise.**

We promise players: this is real. This existed. This mattered. We have checked. We have verified. We would not show it to you if we weren't sure.

We promise the past: you will not be forgotten. Your achievements will be remembered. Your creations will be understood.

We promise the future: this knowledge will survive. These stories will be told. This understanding will be passed on.

**This is why we create artifacts.**

Not for the artifact count. Not for the collection completion metrics. Not for the gameplay engagement numbers.

We create artifacts because history deserves to be preserved. Because civilizations deserve to be remembered. Because every human achievement, no matter how small, is part of the story of us.

And because somewhere, four thousand years ago, a potter shaped clay into beauty, never imagining that someone like you would be admiring his work across the abyss of time.

That potter deserves to be remembered.

That potter deserves a place in Jolt Time.

And we will make sure he gets one.

**Every artifact a teacher speaking.**

**Every artifact a connection revealed.**

**Every artifact a promise kept.**
