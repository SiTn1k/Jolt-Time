# Ancient Celts - Daily Life & Society Pack IV

## Artifacts

### 1. **Bronze Cauldron from Hochdorf**
- **Original Celtic Name**: Unknown
- **Civilization**: Ancient Celts
- **Historical Period**: Hallstatt Culture
- **Approximate Date**: 6th century BCE
- **Current Museum**: Landesmuseum Württemberg, Germany
- **Discovery Site**: Hochdorf Chieftain's Grave, Germany
- **Category**: Bronze Cauldrons
- **Material**: Bronze
- **Dimensions**: Height: 80 cm, Diameter: 100 cm
- **Historical Description**: A large bronze cauldron decorated with intricate designs, used for communal feasting.
- **Social Context**: Reflects the importance of communal gatherings and the social hierarchy of the Celtic chieftain class.
- **Historical Importance**: One of the most significant finds from the Hallstatt period, showing advanced metalworking skills.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in a chieftain's burial mound, intact and richly decorated.
- **Interesting Facts**: The cauldron was lined with gold leaf inside.
- **Educational Summary**: Demonstrates the social and ceremonial roles of feasting in Celtic society.
- **References**: Hochdorf excavation reports, Landesmuseum Württemberg catalog.

### 2. **Iron Sickle from La Tène**
- **Original Celtic Name**: Unknown
- **Civilization**: Ancient Celts
- **Historical Period**: La Tène Culture
- **Approximate Date**: 3rd century BCE
- **Current Museum**: Laténium, Switzerland
- **Discovery Site**: La Tène archaeological site, Switzerland
- **Category**: Agricultural Tools
- **Material**: Iron
- **Dimensions**: Length: 25 cm
- **Historical Description**: A well-preserved iron sickle used for harvesting crops.
- **Social Context**: Indicates the agricultural practices and self-sufficiency of Celtic communities.
- **Historical Importance**: Highlights the transition to iron tools in Celtic agriculture.
- **Discovery Story**: Found among other farming tools in a settlement site.
- **Interesting Facts**: The design remained largely unchanged for centuries.
- **Educational Summary**: Shows the technological advancements in Celtic farming.
- **References**: La Tène excavation reports, Laténium museum catalog.

### 4. **Wooden Bucket from Manching**
- **Original Celtic Name**: Unknown
- **Civilization**: Ancient Celts
- **Historical Period**: Late La Tène Culture
- **Approximate Date**: 2nd century BCE
- **Current Museum**: Celtic and Roman Museum, Manching, Germany
- **Discovery Site**: Oppidum of Manching, Germany
- **Category**: Wooden Buckets
- **Material**: Wood, Bronze fittings
- **Dimensions**: Height: 30 cm, Diameter: 25 cm
- **Historical Description**: A staved wooden bucket with bronze hoops, used for storing liquids.
- **Social Context**: Shows the everyday storage solutions and household items of Celtic settlements.
- **Historical Importance**: Rare survival of organic material from the Celtic period.
- **Discovery Story**: Preserved in waterlogged conditions at the oppidum site.
- **Interesting Facts**: The bucket's construction technique is similar to later medieval methods.
- **Educational Summary**: Highlights Celtic woodworking skills and domestic life.
- **References**: Manching excavation reports, Celtic and Roman Museum catalog.

### 5. **Bone Flute from Dunadd**
- **Original Celtic Name**: Unknown
- **Civilization**: Ancient Celts
- **Historical Period**: Insular Celtic Kingdoms
- **Approximate Date**: 1st century CE
- **Current Museum**: National Museum of Scotland
- **Discovery Site**: Dunadd Fort, Scotland
- **Category**: Musical Instruments
- **Material**: Bone (likely sheep or deer)
- **Dimensions**: Length: 15 cm
- **Historical Description**: A simple flute carved from bone, used for music and ceremonies.
- **Social Context**: Indicates the role of music in Celtic rituals and daily life.
- **Historical Importance**: One of the few surviving Celtic musical instruments.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in a refuse pit within the fort.
- **Interesting Facts**: The finger holes are precisely spaced for musical scales.
- **Educational Summary**: Demonstrates the cultural and artistic expressions of the Celts.
- **References**: Dunadd excavation reports, National Museum of Scotland catalog.
