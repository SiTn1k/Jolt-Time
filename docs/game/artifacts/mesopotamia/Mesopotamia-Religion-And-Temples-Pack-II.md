## Collection Philosophy

The Religion & Temples collection represents the spiritual heart of Mesopotamian civilization — objects that facilitated communication between mortals and the divine, that structured society around sacred calendars and rituals, and that expressed humanity's enduring relationship with the gods. From monumental temple foundations to intimate personal amulets, this collection illuminates how religious belief shaped every dimension of ancient Mesopotamian life.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on objects that reveal the theological concepts, ritual practices, and institutional structures of Mesopotamian religion.

## Temple Foundations & Dedications

### 001. Foundation Pegs of Gudea

**Official Name:** Foundation Pegs of Gudea

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *gú-na* — "foundation peg")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Lagash

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic III, Lagash II

**Approximate Date:** circa 2144–2124 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Various collections)

**Discovery Site:** Tell al-Hiba (ancient Lagash), southern Iraq

**Category:** Temple Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Copper alloy

**Dimensions:** Length: 15-25 cm (6-10 in) each

**Historical Description:** These copper foundation pegs were discovered in the foundations of the temple of Ningirsu at Lagash, dedicated by the ruler Gudea. The pegs feature decorative heads in the shape of the goddess Nisaba (grain) or represent the sacred *gú-na* ritual. Some bear inscriptions naming Gudea and invoking divine blessing upon the temple structure. These objects were part of a complex foundation deposit ritual that included clay nails, baskets of soil, and precious metals.

**Religious Context:** In Mesopotamian belief, foundation pegs secured the cosmic order (*me*) within the temple structure, anchoring the sacred space to the divine realm. The *gú-na* ceremony was essential for legitimizing any major construction, as it transferred divine protection from the gods to the building and its future occupants.

**Historical Importance:** The foundation pegs of Gudea provide crucial evidence for understanding ancient Mesopotamian construction rituals and the theological significance of architecture. They demonstrate how buildings were conceived not merely as human constructions but as partnerships between kings and gods.

**Discovery Story:** French archaeologists from the Délégation Archéologique Française en Irak (DAFI) discovered these foundation pegs during excavations at Tell al-Hiba (ancient Lagash) in the early 20th century. The excavations, led by Ernest de Sarzec beginning in 1877, revealed one of the most important Sumerian cities, producing numerous statues, inscriptions, and foundation deposits.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Foundation pegs were driven into the ground at the four corners of a building's foundation
- Some pegs were gilded or inlaid with precious materials
- The ritual required the king personally to drive the first peg
- Similar pegs have been found at Ur, Nippur, and other major religious centers
- The word *gú-na* also appears in later Akkadian as *kudurru*, referring to boundary stones

**Educational Summary:** Foundation pegs illustrate how Mesopotamians conceived of temples as sacred spaces that required divine inauguration. The placement of these objects connected the physical building to theological concepts of cosmic order, making the temple a microcosm of the properly ordered universe.

**References:**
- Parrot, A. "Tello: Twenty Years of Work in the Tell of the Ancient City of Lagash." Paris: Geuthner, 1956.
- Frayne, D. "Sargonic and Gutian Periods." Royal Inscriptions of Mesopotamia, Early Periods Vol. 2. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1993.
- Musée du Louvre. "Department of Near Eastern Antiquities: Sumerian Art." Paris.

### 002. Foundation Cone of Gudea

**Official Name:** Foundation Cone of Gudea

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *igi-zi-ga* — "cone of foundation")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Lagash

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic III, Lagash II

**Approximate Date:** circa 2144–2124 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 90833)

**Discovery Site:** Tell al-Hiba (ancient Lagash), southern Iraq

**Category:** Temple Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** Height: 12.5 cm (4.9 in); Diameter: 6 cm (2.4 in)

**Historical Description:** This clay foundation cone was found in the foundations of the temple of Ningirsu at Lagash, commissioned by the ruler Gudea. The cone bears a cuneiform inscription in Sumerian that records the dedication of the temple and invokes blessings from the god Ningirsu. Such cones were typically placed at doorways or within walls to ensure divine protection and to record the builder's piety for future generations.

**Religious Context:** Foundation cones were among the most important temple dedication objects, serving as physical manifestations of the king's devotional relationship with the gods. The inscription's blessing formula (*šul-gi*) ensured that any future occupant would respect the sacred nature of the space.

**Historical Importance:** The Foundation Cone of Gudea provides direct evidence for Lagash's monumental building program and the religious motivations behind it. Gudea was renowned for his piety and extensive temple construction, and these cones document his claims in his own words.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered during French excavations at Tell al-Hiba in the late 19th or early 20th century, this cone joined the British Museum's collections through purchase or exchange. Similar cones from Gudea are housed in museums worldwide, demonstrating the extensive distribution of Lagash's religious architecture.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Foundation cones typically feature a convex tip for insertion into clay mortar
- The inscription often includes the phrase "for his lord Ningirsu"
- Hundreds of such cones have been found, suggesting large-scale temple rebuilding
- Some cones were inscribed with the name of the reigning king and the year of construction
- The practice continued throughout Mesopotamian history into the Neo-Babylonian period

**Educational Summary:** Foundation cones demonstrate how Mesopotamian temples were conceived as eternal structures — their foundations containing the names and prayers of their royal builders, ensuring that the sacred character of the space would endure across generations.

**References:**
- Frayne, D. "Sargonic and Gutian Periods." RIMEP 2. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1993.
- Sollberger, E. "The Rulers of Lagash." Journal of Cuneiform Studies 21, no. 3 (1967).
- British Museum Collection Online.

### 003. Temple Brick of Nebuchadnezzar II

**Official Name:** Inscribed Temple Brick of Nebuchadnezzar II

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *libittu* — "brick")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Neo-Babylonian Empire

**Historical Period:** Neo-Babylonian

**Approximate Date:** circa 604–562 BCE

**Current Museum:** Various museums worldwide (British Museum, Louvre, Metropolitan Museum of Art)

**Discovery Site:** Babylon, Iraq

**Category:** Temple Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Clay (mud brick)

**Dimensions:** Approximately 30 × 30 × 10 cm (12 × 12 × 4 in)

**Historical Description:** This inscribed mud brick bears the cartouche of Nebuchadnezzar II, identifying him as the builder of the Esagila (the great temple of Marduk in Babylon) or one of the numerous temples he restored throughout Babylonia. The inscription reads: "Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylon, son of Nabopolassar, king of Babylon, I have built the Esagila for Marduk, the great lord, and renewed its foundations." Similar bricks have been found at temples throughout Babylonia.

**Religious Context:** The reconstruction of temples was one of the most important religious duties of Mesopotamian kings. Nebuchadnezzar II was particularly famous for his extensive building program, which claimed to restore temples to their original divine specifications. The inscription affirms the king's role as the earthly guarantor of divine worship.

**Historical Importance:** The temple bricks of Nebuchadnezzar II are among the most common Babylonian inscriptions, demonstrating the scale of his building program. They document not only his architectural achievements but also his theological commitment to maintaining proper worship of the gods.

**Discovery Story:** These bricks have been found throughout the remains of Babylon and other Babylonian cities, often in secondary use in later structures. European archaeologists in the 19th century collected them extensively, and they remain available on the antiquities market, though authenticated examples are distinguished by their specific find spots.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Nebuchadnezzar claimed to have rebuilt or restored over 40 temples
- The bricks were often stamped while the clay was still wet
- Many bricks were reused in Islamic-era construction in the region
- The Esagila was considered one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World
- Temple rebuilding was believed to renew the cosmic order (*mīšarum*)

**Educational Summary:** Temple bricks illustrate how Mesopotamian kingship was fundamentally intertwined with religious authority. The king's legitimacy derived partly from his demonstrated piety in building and maintaining temples, making architecture a religious as well as political act.

**References:**
- Da Riva, R. "The Neo-Babylonian Royal Inscriptions." Studies in Ancient Near Eastern Records 5. Berlin: De Gruyter, 2012.
- Koldewey, R. "The Excavations at Babylon." London: Macmillan, 1914.
- British Museum Collection Online.

### 004. Clay Nail Deposit

**Official Name:** Clay Nail Deposit from Ur

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *gi-dub* — "clay nail")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Ur

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic III

**Approximate Date:** circa 2600–2500 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 116193)

**Discovery Site:** Ur, southern Iraq

**Category:** Temple Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** Length: 15-20 cm (6-8 in)

**Historical Description:** This tapered clay nail was recovered from the foundations of one of the early temples at Ur, likely the temple of Nanna (Sin), the moon god. The nail bears a simple geometric incised mark, possibly a temple mark or ownership symbol. Clay nails were driven into the foundations of important buildings as part of the dedication ritual, often accompanied by other materials like bitumen, reeds, and soil from sacred sites.

**Religious Context:** Clay nail deposits (*gi-dub*) were among the oldest known foundation deposit types in Mesopotamia. They symbolically "sealed" the building's foundation to the divine sphere, ensuring that the sacred space remained protected from chaos and contamination.

**Historical Importance:** These humble objects provide rare evidence for the religious practices of the Early Dynastic period, when temple architecture was first developing its characteristic monumental forms. They demonstrate remarkable continuity with later foundation practices.

**Discovery Story:** Sir Leonard Woolley's excavations at Ur (1922-1934) produced thousands of foundation deposit objects, including these clay nails. The excavations revolutionized understanding of Sumerian religion and architecture, revealing the ziggurat and its associated temples in unprecedented detail.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Similar clay nails have been found at Uruk, Nippur, and other major cities
- Some nails bear the names of kings or officials
- The practice may derive from earlier prehistoric burial customs
- Later examples could be inscribed with prayers or invocations
- The term *gi-dub* appears in Sumerian lexical texts

**Educational Summary:** Clay nail deposits reveal the deep roots of Mesopotamian temple dedication practices. These simple objects connect modern archaeology to ancient ritual, showing how even basic construction materials could become sacred through proper ceremonial use.

**References:**
- Woolley, L. "Ur Excavations II: The Royal Cemetery." London: Museum of the University of Pennsylvania, 1934.
- Woolley, L. "Ur Excavations IV: The Early Periods." London: The British Museum, 1955.
- British Museum Collection Online.

### 005. Foundation Box of Shulgi

**Official Name:** Foundation Box of Shulgi

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *ki-ur* — "foundation place")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Ur

**Historical Period:** Ur III

**Approximate Date:** circa 2094–2047 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: AO 2046)

**Discovery Site:** Ur, southern Iraq

**Category:** Temple Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Copper alloy

**Dimensions:** 8 × 5 × 3 cm (3.1 × 2 × 1.2 in)

**Historical Description:** This small copper alloy box was discovered in the foundations of a temple at Ur, likely the temple of Nanna (Sin), and bears an inscription naming Shulgi, the second king of the Ur III dynasty. The box would have contained organic materials (now decomposed) — possibly grain, incense, or precious materials — representing the "life force" placed within the building's foundation.

**Religious Context:** Foundation boxes represented a more elaborate version of the deposit ritual, containing precious materials that symbolized abundance, life, and divine blessing. The placement of such objects created a sacred "heart" within the temple that sustained its divine presence.

**Historical Importance:** The Foundation Box of Shulgi provides evidence for the sophisticated foundation rituals of the Ur III period, when royal administration reached unprecedented levels of organization. Shulgi was famous for his building programs and his claims to divine favor.

**Discovery Story:** Part of the extensive Ur excavations conducted by Sir Leonard Woolley, this box joined the Louvre's collections through French archaeological activities in Iraq. The Ur excavations produced some of the most important Mesopotamian objects in any museum.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Foundation boxes were typically placed at the center of the building
- The Ur III period saw standardization of many temple procedures
- Similar boxes are known from the temples of Larsa and Nippur
- Some boxes contain multiple chambers for different materials
- The practice influenced later Near Eastern and Mediterranean traditions

**Educational Summary:** Foundation boxes demonstrate the Mesopotamian understanding of temples as living entities requiring a sacred "heart" to sustain their spiritual function. This concept influenced temple architecture throughout the ancient Near East.

**References:**
- Parrot, A. "Les temples d'Ur et de Larsa." Mission archéologique de Mari IV. Paris: Geuthner, 1959.

### 006. Kudurru of Nazi-Maruttash

**Official Name:** Boundary Stone (Kudurru) of Nazi-Maruttash

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *kudurru* — "boundary stone")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Kassite Babylon

**Historical Period:** Middle Babylonian, Kassite

**Approximate Date:** circa 1347–1340 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 90922)

**Discovery Site:** Southern Iraq (exact provenance uncertain)

**Category:** Temple/Foundation Dedication

**Material:** Diorite

**Dimensions:** Height: 41 cm (16.1 in); Width: 22 cm (8.7 in)

**Historical Description:** This diorite boundary stone records a land grant from the Kassite king Nazi-Maruttash to a temple of the god Enlil at Nippur. The stone bears the elaborate title of the king, invoke blessings from multiple deities, and curse formulas against anyone who would violate the grant. Such stones were typically set up in temples as permanent records of divine land transfers.

**Religious Context:** Kudurrus served both legal and religious functions, recording land transfers that supported temple economies. The invocation of divine curses ensured that temple properties would remain protected, as violating the king's grant would constitute sacrilege against the gods named in the inscription.

**Historical Importance:** This kudurru provides important evidence for the Kassite period's religious and economic policies. The Kassite dynasty, which ruled Babylon for over 400 years, maintained much of traditional Babylonian religion while introducing new elements.

**Discovery Story:** Like many kudurrus, this stone's exact find spot is uncertain, as boundary stones circulated in the antiquities trade. The British Museum acquired it through 19th-century purchases from dealers, a common provenance for many Mesopotamian monuments.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Kassite kudurrus often feature elaborate divine symbols
- The curse formulas invoke both traditional Mesopotamian and Kassite deities
- Some kudurrus record multiple grants and transfers over generations
- The word *kudurru* may derive from a Kassite term
- Over 200 kudurrus are known from the Kassite period

**Educational Summary:** Kudurrus illustrate how religion, law, and economics intertwined in Mesopotamia. Temple land grants, recorded on sacred stone, were protected by divine authority, demonstrating that religious institutions formed the economic backbone of ancient society.

**References:**
- Brinkman, J.A. "A Political History of Post-Kassite Babylon." Rome: Pontifical Biblical Institute, 1968.
- Slanski, K.E. "The Babylonian Entitlement Narûs (kudurrus)." Boston: Brill, 2003.
- British Museum Collection Online.

- The practice of placing inscribed tablets in foundations continued for millennia

## Votive Statues & Figurines

### 011. Votive Statues from Tell Asmar (Abu Temple)

**Official Name:** Votive Statues from the Abu Temple at Tell Asmar

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *alabaster* — "white stone")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Sumer

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic I-II

**Approximate Date:** circa 2700–2600 BCE

**Current Museum:** Various (Iraqi Museum, Metropolitan Museum of Art, Louvre, British Museum)

**Discovery Site:** Tell Asmar (ancient Eshnunna), Iraq

**Category:** Votive Statues

**Material:** Gypsum (alabaster), limestone

**Dimensions:** Heights range from 12-60 cm (5-24 in)

**Historical Description:** These limestone and gypsum statues were found in a cache within the Abu Temple at Tell Asmar, representing worshippers in poses of prayer before the gods. The figures feature large, hollow eyes (originally inlaid with shell and lapis lazuli), crossed hands, and elaborate wool or fleece skirts. They represent individuals making offerings or performing pilgrimage, their blank faces conveying religious devotion.

**Religious Context:** Votive statues represented the worshipper before the gods, serving as their "representative" in the temple when they could not be present physically. The hollow eyes, originally inlaid, were meant to allow the divine to "see" the worshiper's devotion, creating a permanent connection between mortal and divine.

**Historical Importance:** The Abu Temple statues are among the most famous examples of Early Dynastic sculpture, demonstrating the sophisticated artistic traditions that preceded the great Sumerian monuments of the Early Dynastic III period. They provide crucial evidence for understanding Sumerian religious practice.

**Discovery Story:** Swedish archaeologist Seton Lloyd discovered these statues during excavations at Tell Asmar in the 1930s, as part of the Iraq Expedition of the University of Pennsylvania Museum. The discovery revolutionized understanding of early Sumerian art and religion.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The statues were found stacked in a corner of the temple, suggesting they were deliberately cached

# Educational Framework

## Learning Objectives

1. **Religious Understanding**: Players will learn about Mesopotamian religious beliefs, practices, and institutions.
2. **Temple Architecture**: The collection demonstrates the development of Mesopotamian temple architecture, from early shrines to monumental ziggurats.
3. **Priesthood & Cult**: Artifacts illuminate the complex hierarchy of Mesopotamian religious professionals.
4. **Divine Manifestation**: The collection shows how Mesopotamians understood divine presence through cult statues and sacred objects.
5. **Temple Economy**: Administrative artifacts reveal how temples functioned as economic institutions.

## Key Themes

- **Temple as Cosmic Center**: How temples represented the connection between earth and heaven
- **Cult Statue Theology**: The belief that gods literally lived in temples
- **Votive Deduction**: How ordinary people participated in temple religion
- **Royal Religion**: The king's role as chief priest and temple builder
- **Ritual Practice**: Daily, weekly, and festival rituals that sustained divine favor
