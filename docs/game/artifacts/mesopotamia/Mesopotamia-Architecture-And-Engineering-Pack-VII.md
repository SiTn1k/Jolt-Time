## Collection Philosophy

The Architecture & Engineering collection represents the monumental achievements of ancient Mesopotamian builders — the ziggurats that touched the sky, the palaces that housed emperors, and the canals that transformed barren land into fertile fields. From the earliest mud brick structures of Sumer to the magnificent Ishtar Gate of Babylon, this collection illuminates how Mesopotamian architects and engineers developed the techniques and technologies that would influence building traditions for millennia.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on objects that reveal the construction techniques, engineering innovations, and architectural achievements that created the built environment of ancient Mesopotamia.

## Ziggurats & Temples

### 001. Ziggurat of Ur Foundation Brick

**Official Name:** Ziggurat Foundation Brick of Ur-Nammu

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *ziqqurat* — "ziggurat"; *libittu* — "brick")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Ur

**Historical Period:** Ur III

**Approximate Date:** circa 2100–2000 BCE (reign of Ur-Nammu)

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 91101)

**Discovery Site:** Ur, southern Iraq

**Category:** Ziggurat Element

**Material:** Mud brick

**Dimensions:** 35 cm × 17 cm × 10 cm (13.8 in × 6.7 in × 3.9 in)

**Historical Description:** This mud brick bears the inscription of Ur-Nammu, founder of the Ur III dynasty and builder of the Great Ziggurat of Ur. The brick is part of the massive foundation structure that supported the monumental temple tower. The ziggurat of Ur is one of the best-preserved examples of Mesopotamian monument architecture.

**Engineering Context:** The ziggurat represents the pinnacle of Mesopotamian mud brick construction. The massive structure required sophisticated understanding of foundations, load distribution, and material properties. The tiered design created a stable structure that has survived for four millennia.

**Historical Importance:** Ziggurat bricks document the construction techniques used in ancient Mesopotamia's most ambitious building projects. The Great Ziggurat of Ur was considered one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World by later writers.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered from the ruins of the ziggurat during Sir Leonard Woolley's excavations at Ur between 1922-1934.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The ziggurat originally had three tiers
- It stood approximately 30 meters (100 feet) tall
- The structure has been partially restored
- Similar ziggurats existed in Uruk, Nippur, and other cities
- The word "ziggurat" comes from Akkadian

**Educational Summary:** Ziggurat bricks demonstrate the construction techniques used in ancient Mesopotamia's most impressive architectural achievements.

**References:**
- Woolley, L. "Ur Excavations IV: The Great Bridge and the Ziggurat." London: British Museum, 1939.
- British Museum Collection Online.

### 002. Foundation Cone of Gudea

**Official Name:** Foundation Cone of Gudea

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *igi-zi-ga* — "cone of foundation")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Lagash

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic III, Lagash II

**Approximate Date:** circa 2144–2124 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 90833)

**Discovery Site:** Tell al-Hiba (ancient Lagash), southern Iraq

**Category:** Foundation Deposit

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** Height: 12.5 cm (4.9 in); Diameter: 6 cm (2.4 in)

**Historical Description:** This clay foundation cone was found in the foundations of the temple of Ningirsu at Lagash, commissioned by the ruler Gudea. The cone bears a cuneiform inscription in Sumerian that records the dedication of the temple and invokes blessings from the god Ningirsu. Such cones were typically placed at doorways or within walls to ensure divine protection and to record the builder's piety for future generations.

**Engineering Context:** Foundation cones were part of a complex ritual that ensured structural stability through divine intervention. The placement of inscribed cones documented construction for posterity and invoked supernatural protection for the building.

**Historical Importance:** Foundation cones provide evidence for temple construction techniques and the religious significance of building in ancient Mesopotamia.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered during French excavations at Tell al-Hiba (ancient Lagash) in the early 20th century.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Foundation cones typically feature a convex tip for insertion into clay mortar
- The inscription often includes the phrase "for his lord Ningirsu"
- Hundreds of such cones have been found
- The practice continued throughout Mesopotamian history
- Some cones were gilded or inlaid

**Educational Summary:** Foundation cones demonstrate the ritual and practical aspects of Mesopotamian building construction.

**References:**
- Frayne, D. "Sargonic and Gutian Periods." RIMEP 2. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1993.
- British Museum Collection Online.
