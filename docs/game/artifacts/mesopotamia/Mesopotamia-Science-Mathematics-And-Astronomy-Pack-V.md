## Collection Philosophy

The Science, Mathematics & Astronomy collection represents the intellectual achievements of ancient Mesopotamian civilization — the mathematical tables that enabled complex calculations, the astronomical observations that tracked celestial movements, the medical texts that preserved healing knowledge, and the engineering feats that transformed the landscape. From the sophisticated trigonometry of Plimpton 322 to the detailed planetary observations recorded in the Venus Tablet, this collection illuminates how Mesopotamian scholars developed the scientific foundations upon which later civilizations would build.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on cuneiform tablets and artifacts that document the sophisticated scientific thinking of ancient Mesopotamian scholars, scribes, and practitioners.

### 001. Plimpton 322

**Official Name:** Plimpton 322

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *pīlu* — "tablet")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Larsa

**Historical Period:** Old Babylonian

**Approximate Date:** circa 1800–1700 BCE

**Current Museum:** G. A. Plimpton Collection, Columbia University, New York (Museum Number: 322)

**Discovery Site:** Larsa (Tell Senkereh), southern Iraq

**Category:** Mathematical Tablet

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** 12.7 cm × 8.8 cm × 2 cm (5 in × 3.5 in × 0.8 in)

**Historical Description:** Plimpton 322 is one of the most famous mathematical tablets from ancient Mesopotamia, containing a sophisticated table of Pythagorean triples — sets of three numbers that satisfy the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²). The tablet organizes these triples in a systematic way, with the left-hand column listing the short side (a), the middle column listing the hypotenuse (c), and the right-hand column showing the square of the short side divided by the square of the diagonal. This represents the oldest known trigonometric table, predating Greek trigonometry by over a millennium.

**Scientific Context:** Plimpton 322 demonstrates that Babylonian mathematicians understood the relationship between the sides of right triangles long before Pythagoras. The tablet appears to be a reference table for solving problems, possibly used in construction, surveying, or astronomy. The mathematical sophistication is remarkable — the triples are organized in a way that suggests understanding of mathematical relationships beyond simple enumeration.

**Historical Importance:** Plimpton 322 is considered one of the most important mathematical artifacts in human history. It demonstrates that ancient Mesopotamians possessed sophisticated mathematical knowledge, including what we now call Pythagorean triples, nearly 4,000 years ago. This challenges conventional assumptions about the origins of trigonometry and advanced mathematics.

**Discovery Story:** Discovered at Larsa in the early 20th century, this tablet was purchased by the American publisher and collector George Arthur Plimpton (1854-1936) and bequeathed to Columbia University. It was first studied systematically by Otto Neugebauer and Abraham Sachs in the 1940s, who recognized its mathematical significance.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Contains 15 sets of Pythagorean triples
- The largest triple is (12709, 13500, 18541)
- The numbers are organized in descending order of the ratio c/a
- Some scholars believe it may have been used for generating trigonometric tables
- The mathematical precision suggests practical applications in construction

**Educational Summary:** Plimpton 322 demonstrates that advanced mathematical concepts we attribute to later civilizations were known in ancient Mesopotamia. The systematic organization of Pythagorean triples shows sophisticated mathematical thinking that influenced subsequent developments in mathematics.

**References:**
- Neugebauer, O. and Sachs, A. "Mathematical Cuneiform Texts." New Haven: American Oriental Society, 1945.
- Robson, E. "Words and Pictures: New Light on Plimpton 322." Historia Mathematica 29 (2002): 213-247.
- Columbia University Plimpton Collection Online.

### 002. YBC 7289

**Official Name:** YBC 7289

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *pīlu* — "tablet")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Unknown (Possibly Sippar)

**Historical Period:** Old Babylonian

**Approximate Date:** circa 1800–1700 BCE

**Current Museum:** Yale Babylonian Collection, New Haven (Museum Number: YBC 7289)

**Discovery Site:** Unknown (possibly Sippar)

**Category:** Mathematical Tablet

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** 8 cm × 8 cm × 2 cm (3.1 in × 3.1 in × 0.8 in)

**Historical Description:** YBC 7289 is a small Babylonian clay tablet that contains a square with its diagonals drawn. On one of the diagonals, the tablet shows the numbers 1;24,51,10 and 42;25,35, which represent the Babylonian sexagesimal (base-60) approximations of √2. The first number (1;24,51,10) represents √2 ≈ 1.4142129... while the second (42;25,35) represents 30 × √2 ≈ 42.4264... This demonstrates that Babylonian mathematicians knew that the diagonal of a square is the side multiplied by √2, and they had calculated this value to an extraordinary degree of accuracy.

**Scientific Context:** This tablet represents the Babylonian method for calculating square roots, essential for geometric calculations in architecture, surveying, and engineering. The sexagesimal representation 1;24,51,10 converts to 1 + 24/60 + 51/3600 + 10/216000 = 1.41421296..., which differs from the true value of √2 (1.41421356...) by less than one part in two hundred thousand.

**Historical Importance:** YBC 7289 demonstrates the extraordinary precision of Babylonian mathematics. The approximation of √2 to six sexagesimal places (approximately ten decimal places) shows advanced computational techniques that would not be matched for centuries.

**Discovery Story:** Part of the Yale Babylonian Collection, acquired through various excavations and purchases in the early 20th century. The exact provenience is uncertain, but the tablet is characteristic of Old Babylonian mathematical texts.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Uses the sexagesimal (base-60) number system
- The approximation error is less than 0.0001%
- Shows practical application of the Pythagorean theorem
- The same technique appears in modern computer algorithms
- Demonstrates the relationship between geometry and arithmetic

**Educational Summary:** YBC 7289 illustrates the sophisticated numerical methods of Babylonian mathematicians. The accurate calculation of √2 shows computational skills far ahead of their time.

**References:**
- Neugebauer, O. "The Exact Sciences in Antiquity." Providence: Brown University Press, 1957.
- Yale Babylonian Collection Catalog.
- Fowler, D. and Robson, E. "Square Root Approximations in Old Babylonian Mathematics." Historia Mathematica 25 (1998): 366-378.

### 015. Venus Tablet of Ammisaduqa

**Official Name:** Venus Tablet of Ammisaduqa

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *Enuma Anu Enlil* — "When Anu and Enlil")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Babylon

**Historical Period:** Old Babylonian

**Approximate Date:** circa 1550 BCE (reign of Ammisaduqa)

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 230)

**Discovery Site:** Nineveh (Ashurbanipal's Library), northern Iraq

**Category:** Astronomical Tablet

**Material:** Clay

**Dimensions:** 15 cm × 13 cm × 3 cm (5.9 in × 5.1 in × 1.2 in)

**Historical Description:** The Venus Tablet of Ammisaduqa records observations of the planet Venus over a period of 21 years, documenting its first and last appearances on the horizon. This tablet is crucial for dating ancient Mesopotamian chronology because it records specific astronomical events that can be precisely calculated using modern astronomy, allowing scholars to establish absolute dates for the Old Babylonian period.

**Scientific Context:** The Babylonians carefully tracked Venus as the morning and evening star, recording its cycles. These observations were essential for developing accurate calendars and understanding celestial patterns. The tablet also contains omens associated with Venus appearances, reflecting the Mesopotamian belief that celestial events predicted earthly outcomes.

**Historical Importance:** The Venus Tablet is one of the most important astronomical documents from the ancient world. By comparing the recorded observations with modern astronomical calculations, scholars have been able to establish absolute dates for the Old Babylonian period, providing a fixed chronology for ancient Near Eastern history.

**Discovery Story:** Discovered in the ruins of the Great Library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh in the mid-19th century by Austen Henry Layard and Hormuzd Rassam. The tablet was part of the extensive collection of cuneiform texts recovered from the Assyrian royal archives.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Records 21 years of Venus observations
- First appearance dates can be calculated astronomically
- Used to establish absolute chronology of Old Babylonian period
- Contains omen interpretations for Venus appearances
- The reign of Ammisaduqa can be dated to 1702-1682 BCE

**Educational Summary:** The Venus Tablet demonstrates both the astronomical observation skills and the omen-based interpretation of celestial events that characterized Mesopotamian science.

**References:**
- Reiner, E. and Pingree, D. "Babylonian Planetary Omens." Malibu: Undena, 1975.
- British Museum Collection Online.
- Rochberg, F. "The Heavenly Writing." Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2004.
