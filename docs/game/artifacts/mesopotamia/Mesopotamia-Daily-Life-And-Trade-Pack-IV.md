## Collection Philosophy

The Daily Life & Trade collection represents the vibrant tapestry of ordinary existence in ancient Mesopotamia — the objects that sustained families, facilitated commerce, and expressed cultural identity. From the merchant who calculated profits on a clay tablet to the farmer who harvested grain with a bronze sickle, from the weaver at her loom to the merchant weighing precious metals, this collection illuminates the economic, social, and material foundations of the world's first civilizations.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on objects that reveal the practical mechanics of daily existence and the sophisticated commercial networks that connected Mesopotamia to distant lands.

### 001. Royal Game of Ur

**Official Name:** Royal Game of Ur

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *a-ša-ab* — "game of twenty squares")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Ur

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic IIIa

**Approximate Date:** circa 2600–2400 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 120834)

**Discovery Site:** Royal Tombs of Ur, southern Iraq

**Category:** Board Game / Trade Object

**Material:** Wood inlay, shell, lapis lazuli

**Dimensions:** Board: 29.2 cm × 19.4 cm (11.5 in × 7.6 in)

**Historical Description:** The Royal Game of Ur is one of the oldest complete board games known from antiquity. Discovered in the Royal Tombs of Ur by Sir Leonard Woolley between 1922-1934, this elaborate game set includes a wooden board with shell and lapis lazuli inlay, tetrahedral dice made from ostrich bones, and twenty playing pieces (seven for each player). The board features a distinctive twenty-square track with a central row of four special squares.

**Daily Life Context:** This was not merely a game but a prestigious possession that demonstrated wealth and sophistication. The game was played by two competitors using dice to move their pieces along the track, with the goal of being the first to remove all pieces from the board. Royal tombs contained multiple game sets, suggesting gaming was a favored aristocratic pastime and possibly a symbolic contest between mortals and the divine.

**Historical Importance:** The Royal Game of Ur represents the sophisticated leisure culture of Sumerian elites and provides crucial evidence for the mathematics of probability in ancient Mesopotamia. The game's rules, reconstructed from Babylonian tablets, reveal a complex recreational tradition that combined luck and strategy.

**Discovery Story:** Discovered in the Royal Cemetery at Ur during the landmark excavations led by Sir Leonard Woolley between 1922-1934. The game board was found in the tomb of a princess (possibly Queen Puabi), along with other luxury goods demonstrating the wealth of Ur's royal court.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The game was played with two sets of seven pieces and four tetrahedral dice
- The dice were marked with two white sides and two marked sides, giving four possible values
- A Babylonian tablet from around 177 BCE provides the complete rules
- The game spread throughout the ancient Near East, with variations found in Egypt and the Levant
- The twenty-square track featured special "safe" squares marked with rosette patterns

**Educational Summary:** The Royal Game of Ur demonstrates that complex recreational activities and strategic thinking were valued pursuits in Sumerian society. The game also illustrates the mathematical sophistication required for probability calculations and the cultural connections between gaming, royalty, and religious ritual.

**References:**
- Woolley, L. "Ur Excavations II: The Royal Cemetery." London: British Museum, 1934.
- Boardman, J. "The Royal Game of Ur." Oxford: Ashmolean Museum, 1980.
- British Museum Collection Online.

### 002. Merchant Weight in Shape of Duck

**Official Name:** Duck-Shaped Balance Weight

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *gín* — "shekel"; Akkadian: *manû*)

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Ur

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic III

**Approximate Date:** circa 2500–2400 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: 90942)

**Discovery Site:** Ur, southern Iraq

**Category:** Trade Weight

**Material:** Basalt

**Dimensions:** Length: 4.5 cm (1.8 in); Weight: approximately 8.33 grams (1/60 shekel unit)

**Historical Description:** This precisely carved basalt weight is shaped in the form of a swimming duck, representing one of the standardized weight units used in Mesopotamian commerce. The duck form was one of several animal shapes used for weights, reflecting the artistic conventions of Sumerian craftspeople. Weights were essential tools for merchants engaged in the ancient equivalent of wholesale trade, ensuring fair exchange of commodities like grain, metal, and textiles.

**Daily Life Context:** Merchants throughout Mesopotamia relied on standardized weights to conduct fair trade. The duck weight, representing approximately one-eighth of a shekel, would have been used in combination with a balance scale to weigh precious commodities. The precision of such weights demonstrates the sophisticated commercial infrastructure that supported long-distance trade networks spanning from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean.

**Historical Importance:** Mesopotamian weight systems established the foundations for later monetary economies. The standardization of weights across the ancient Near East facilitated the development of complex commercial networks and established trust in marketplace transactions. The duck motif appears in weights from multiple Mesopotamian sites, suggesting standardized production methods.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered during excavations at Ur by Sir Leonard Woolley in the 1920s-1930s, this weight represents the careful documentation of everyday commercial objects from one of Mesopotamia's greatest cities.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Mesopotamian weights were based on a sexagesimal (60-based) system
- The standard unit was the shekel (approximately 8.3 grams)
- Larger weights of 1, 5, 10, and 60 shekels were commonly used
- Animal-shaped weights emphasized craftsmanship while maintaining precision
- Similar weight systems were adopted throughout the ancient Near East

**Educational Summary:** Balance weights like this duck-shaped example reveal the commercial sophistication of Sumerian society. Standardized weights enabled fair trade across vast distances, connecting Mesopotamian cities into an integrated economic network that formed the foundation for later monetary systems.

**References:**

**Historical Description:** This lapis lazuli cylinder seal depicts a merchant scene typical of Early Dynastic commercial iconography. The seal shows a figure in traditional Mesopotamian dress, likely a merchant or official, standing before a seated deity or superior, with various symbols of authority and commerce. Cylinder seals served as personal signatures in ancient Mesopotamia, rolled onto clay documents to authenticate transactions, contracts, and administrative records.

**Daily Life Context:** Merchants used cylinder seals to authenticate their commercial dealings, marking clay tablets that recorded debts, payments, and trade agreements. The seal's imagery often reflected the owner's occupation or divine patronage, providing both functional identification and spiritual protection. A merchant's seal was his most important professional tool, establishing trust in business relationships.

**Historical Importance:** Cylinder seals represent one of humanity's earliest forms of personal identification and authentication. The images carved on seals provide invaluable evidence for understanding Mesopotamian commerce, social hierarchy, and religious beliefs. The widespread use of seals across Mesopotamia created the infrastructure for complex economic record-keeping.

**Discovery Story:** Discovered at Ur during the British Museum/University of Pennsylvania excavations, this seal exemplifies the type of personal identification device used by Mesopotamian merchants and officials.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Cylinder seals were rolled across wet clay to create a continuous band of imagery
- The practice began in the Ubaid period (circa 4000 BCE) and continued for millennia
- Different materials (lapis lazuli, carnelian, agate) indicated different social statuses
- Seals could be worn as amulets, combining personal identification with spiritual protection
- Impressions from seals on clay tablets provide archaeological "snapshots" of ancient transactions

**Educational Summary:** Cylinder seals were essential tools for Mesopotamian commerce, serving as both personal signatures and professional credentials. The scenes depicted on seals — like this merchant before authority — illustrate the social structures that governed ancient trade.
