## Collection Philosophy

The Kings & Royal Power collection represents the pinnacle of Mesopotamian monarchical tradition — objects that manifested the divine authority of kings, the sophistication of royal administration, and the monumental achievements of ancient Near Eastern empires. From the divine kingship of Sumer to the imperial might of Assyria and the architectural wonders of Babylon, this collection illuminates how Mesopotamian rulers constructed, communicated, and legitimized their authority through material culture.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on objects that illuminate the political, religious, and administrative dimensions of royal power.

## Royal Stelae & Inscriptions

### 001. Stele of Hammurabi

**Official Name:** Stele with the Laws of Hammurabi

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *kittum u mīšarum* — "truth and justice")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Old Babylonian Empire

**Historical Period:** Old Babylonian, First Dynasty of Babylon

**Approximate Date:** circa 1754 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: SB 8)

**Discovery Site:** Susa, Iran (ancient Elamite capital, relocated in antiquity)

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Law Code

**Material:** Basalt (dolerite)

**Dimensions:** Height: 225 cm (88.6 in); Width at top: 165 cm (65 in); Width at base: 190 cm (74.8 in)

**Historical Description:** This monumental stele depicts Hammurabi of Babylon receiving the laws from Shamash, the sun god and god of justice. The relief shows Shamash seated on a throne, wearing a horned crown, and presenting a staff and ring — symbols of divine authority — to the kneeling king. The cuneiform inscription, comprising 282 laws, constitutes one of the earliest and most complete legal codes from the ancient Near East. The laws address topics ranging from commerce and property to family relations and criminal justice, organized according to the principle of "an eye for an eye."

**Historical Importance:** The Code of Hammurabi represents the pinnacle of Babylonian legal thought and royal ideology. It established the king's role as the guarantor of justice (*mīšarum*) and divine order on earth. The stele's monumental scale and public display in the temple of Shamash at Babylon proclaimed Hammurabi's role as the righteous ruler chosen by the gods. The code influenced subsequent legal traditions throughout the ancient Near East and represents a foundational document in the history of law.

**Discovery Story:** French archaeologists discovered the stele at Susa in December 1901 during excavations led by Jacques de Morgan. The stele had been carried to Susa as war spoils by the Elamite king Shutruk-Nahhunte approximately 1200 BCE, part of a systematic looting of Babylonian monuments. The French delegation, including archaeologists Jacques de Morgan and François Thureau-Dangin, recognized the monument's significance immediately. The stele arrived at the Louvre in 1903 and has since become one of the museum's most celebrated artifacts.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The stele weighs approximately 4 tons
- The cuneiform inscription is written in Old Babylonian, one of the earliest forms of Akkadian
- The phrase "an eye for an eye" (*lex talionis*) appears throughout the code
- The laws distinguish between social classes, with different penalties for crimes against nobles, commoners, and slaves
- A similar but smaller stele was discovered at Babylon itself, suggesting multiple copies were created
- The relief depicts Shamash with rays emanating from his shoulders, symbolizing the sun's illuminating power over justice

**Educational Summary:** The Stele of Hammurabi demonstrates how ancient monarchs legitimized their rule through divine sanction and codified law. The king's role as the upholder of divine order (*ma'at* in Egyptian, *mīšarum* in Babylonian) was central to Mesopotamian political theology. The laws reveal the sophisticated legal thinking of Babylonian society, including concepts of presumption of innocence, evidence-based judgment, and proportional punishment that would not be surpassed for millennia.

**References:**
- Driver, G.R. & Miles, J.C. "The Babylonian Laws." Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1952.
- Roth, M.T. "Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor." Atlanta: Scholars Press, 1997.
- Musée du Louvre. "Collection Orientale: Antiquités Méopotamiennes." Paris.
- Van De Mieroop, M. "King Hammurabi of Babylon." Malden: Blackwell Publishing, 2005.

### 002. Victory Stele of Naram-Sin

**Official Name:** Victory Stele of Naram-Sin

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *narû* ša Nabû — "stele of the conqueror")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Akkadian Empire

**Historical Period:** Akkadian

**Approximate Date:** circa 2254–2218 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: SB 21)

**Discovery Site:** Susa, Iran

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Victory Monument

**Material:** Limestone

**Dimensions:** Height: 200 cm (78.7 in); Width: 105 cm (41.3 in)

**Historical Description:** This iconic limestone stele depicts Naram-Sin, the "King of Kish" and founder of the Akkadian Empire, standing triumphant over his enemies. The king, depicted at nearly double the height of his soldiers and enemies, wears the horned crown of divinity and carries a bow — symbols of his supreme authority. Below his feet lie the bodies of defeated enemies, with one particularly prominent figure from the defeated army begging for mercy. The composition employs hierarchical scaling to emphasize the king's divine status, while the inscription in Sumerian cuneiform commemorates his victory over the Lullubi people of the Zagros Mountains.

**Historical Importance:** The Victory Stele of Naram-Sin represents a watershed moment in the visual representation of kingship in ancient Mesopotamia. It is the earliest known monument to depict a king as a god during his lifetime — a revolutionary assertion of divine kingship that would influence subsequent Mesopotamian and Near Eastern ruler ideology for two millennia. The stele also marks the emergence of Akkadian imperial ideology, with Naram-Sin claiming universal dominion ("King of the Four Quarters").

**Discovery Story:** French archaeologists discovered this stele at Susa in 1895-1896 during excavations conducted by Jacques de Morgan. Like the Stele of Hammurabi, this monument had been carried to Susa as war spoils by Elamite conquerors. The stele was found broken into several fragments and required restoration. Its discovery revolutionized scholarly understanding of Akkadian art and royal ideology, demonstrating that Mesopotamian kings had claimed divine status much earlier than previously believed.

**Interesting Facts:**
- This is the first known Mesopotamian monument to depict a king as a god during his lifetime
- The horned crown originally reserved for deities now appears on a living king's head
- The defeated enemy's pose (arms raised in surrender) became a standard motif in later royal art
- The inscription includes the phrase "Naram-Sin, king of Kish, king of the world"
- The stele influenced the famous "Stele of the Vultures" from the Early Dynastic period
- Ninevite curl inscriptions on the back record Naram-Sin's campaigns

**Educational Summary:** The Victory Stele of Naram-Sin demonstrates how ancient rulers used monumental art to construct and communicate their political ideology. By depicting himself at divine scale and wearing the horned crown, Naram-Sin asserted his role as the chosen representative of the gods on earth. This monument illuminates the development of imperial ideology in Mesopotamia and the evolution of the king's relationship with divine authority — from being the gods' servant to becoming the gods' embodiment.

**References:**
- Winter, I.J. "Idols of the King: Royal Images and Representation of Divinity in Ancient Mesopotamia." Leiden: E.J. Brill, 1981.
- Westenholz, J.G. "Legends of the Kings of Akkade." Winona Lake: Eisenbrauns, 1997.
- Musée du Louvre. "Art of the Akkadian Empire." Paris, 1993.
- Foster, B.R. "The Age of the Akkadian Empire." Boston: Brill, 2016.

### 003. Code of Ur-Nammu

**Official Name:** Code of Ur-Nammu

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *dinanna* — "judgments")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Third Dynasty of Ur (Ur III)

**Historical Period:** Ur III

**Approximate Date:** circa 2100–2050 BCE

**Current Museum:** Museum of the Ancient Orient (Archaeological Museum), Istanbul (Museum Number: 2261)

**Discovery Site:** Nippur, Iraq

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Law Code

**Material:** Terracotta (clay tablet)

**Dimensions:** Height: 15.5 cm (6.1 in); Width: 11 cm (4.3 in); Depth: 2.5 cm (1 in)

**Historical Description:** This clay tablet contains the earliest known law code from human history, predating Hammurabi's Code by approximately 150 years. The text, inscribed in Sumerian, establishes legal principles for the kingdom of Ur under King Ur-Nammu. The laws address compensation for bodily injuries, theft, marriage, divorce, and slave ownership. Unlike Hammurabi's code with its punitive "eye for an eye" principle, Ur-Nammu's laws primarily specify monetary compensation (*nisqa*) for offenses.

**Historical Importance:** The Code of Ur-Nammu represents the foundation of written legal tradition in Mesopotamia and potentially the world. It demonstrates that the concept of codified law predates the Akkadian expansion and was a Sumerian innovation. The code establishes the king not merely as a warrior but as the guarantor of social order and justice. It reveals a sophisticated legal system that distinguished between intentional and accidental harm and provided systematic compensation structures.

**Discovery Story:** The tablet was discovered during American excavations at Nippur conducted by the University of Pennsylvania and the German Archaeological Institute between 1889 and 1898. TheExcavation was led by Hermann Hilprecht. The tablet was subsequently transported to the Ottoman Archaeological Museum in Istanbul (now the Museum of the Ancient Orient), where it remains on display.

**Interesting Facts:**
- This is the oldest known law code in human history
- Unlike Hammurabi's code, it emphasizes monetary compensation rather than physical retaliation
- Only the prologue and a few laws survive; the full code likely contained 30-40 laws
- The prologue invokes the god Shamash, though this is unusual for Sumerian law codes
- It was written approximately 150 years before Hammurabi's code
- The document shows evidence of being a scribal exercise, possibly from a temple school

**Educational Summary:** The Code of Ur-Nammu reveals that the concept of codified law emerged in Sumer and formed the foundation for subsequent Mesopotamian legal traditions. The code demonstrates that ancient Mesopotamian society had developed sophisticated mechanisms for maintaining social order, protecting property rights, and establishing justice. The shift from physical punishment to monetary compensation represents a significant legal evolution and reflects the increasing complexity of Ur III society.

**References:**
- Roth, M.T. "Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor." Atlanta: Scholars Press, 1997.
- Kramer, S.N. "The Sumerians: Their History, Culture, and Character." Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1963.
- Museum of the Ancient Orient. "Ancient Mesopotamia: The Dawn of Civilization." Istanbul.
- Halloran, J.A. "The Code of Ur-Nammu." Lexicographical Tools for the Ancient Near East, University of Michigan.

### 004. Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III

**Official Name:** Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III

**Original Name:** (Assyrian: *ḫurāṭu* — "black stone")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Neo-Assyrian Empire

**Historical Period:** Neo-Assyrian

**Approximate Date:** circa 858–824 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 118885)

**Discovery Site:** Nimrud (ancient Kalhu), Iraq

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Victory Monument

**Material:** Black limestone (limestone with bitumen)

**Dimensions:** Height: 197 cm (77.6 in); Width at base: 50 cm (19.7 in)

**Historical Description:** This monumental black limestone obelisk is one of the most important monuments from the Neo-Assyrian Empire. Four panels depict Shalmaneser III receiving tribute from various subj ected peoples. The lowest register shows the famous scene of Israelite king Jehu (or his representative) prostrating before Shalmaneser III — the only known contemporary depiction of an Israelite king. The cuneiform inscriptions on all four sides record Shalmaneser's military campaigns and victories, including his conflicts with the kingdoms of Israel, Tyre, Sidon, and various Aramaean and Nairi states.

**Historical Importance:** The Black Obelisk provides invaluable historical information about the Assyrian empire at its height. The tribute scenes offer unprecedented visual documentation of the peoples and goods that flowed into Assyria from its vast empire. The depiction of Jehu is particularly significant as the only contemporary extra-biblical reference to the Israelite monarchy. The inscriptions confirm and expand upon textual sources, providing a royal annalistic record of military campaigns.

**Discovery Story:** British archaeologist Austen Henry Layard discovered the obelisk at Nimrud (ancient Kalhu) in 1846. Layard was conducting excavations sponsored by the British Museum when his team uncovered the monument in what was identified as the palace of Shalmaneser III. The obelisk was remarkably well-preserved, its black limestone surface still bearing the sharp relief carvings and legible inscriptions. It was transported to the British Museum in 1848 and has been on continuous display since.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The obelisk depicts tribute scenes from at least 20 different ethnic groups
- The scene of Jehu is the only known contemporary depiction of an Israelite king
- Shalmaneser III reigned for 35 years and fought in at least 32 military campaigns
- The obelisk was originally placed in the central courtyard of Shalmaneser's palace
- The name "Black Obelisk" comes from the dark limestone darkened further by bitumen
- The inscription claims victories that archaeologists have confirmed through other sources

**Educational Summary:** The Black Obelisk demonstrates how Assyrian kings used monumental art to legitimize and celebrate their military achievements. The systematic depiction of tribute-bearers from throughout the empire visually communicated the king's universal dominion. The monument also illuminates international relations in the 9th century BCE, providing rare contemporary evidence for the political landscape of the ancient Near East, including the existence and importance of the kingdom of Israel.

**References:**
- Grayson, A.K. "Assyrian Rulers of the Early First Millennium BC (858–745 BC)." Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1996.
- British Museum. "The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III." Collection Database.
- Liverani, M. "The Prestige and the Decline of the Assyrian Empire." Rome:stituto di Studi sul Vicino Oriente, 1987.

### 005. White Obelisk of Ashurnasirpal II

**Official Name:** White Obelisk of Ashurnasirpal II

**Original Name:** (Assyrian: *ḫurāṭu peṣû* — "white obelisk")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Neo-Assyrian Empire

**Historical Period:** Neo-Assyrian

**Approximate Date:** circa 875–860 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 118554)

**Discovery Site:** Nimrud (ancient Kalhu), Iraq

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Palace Decoration

**Material:** Limestone (alabaster/gypsum)

**Dimensions:** Height: 298 cm (117.3 in); Width at base: 59 cm (23.2 in)

**Historical Description:** This white limestone obelisk was discovered in the palace of Ashurnasirpal II at Nimrud. Four registers of relief decoration depict the king performing various religious and royal duties: receiving tribute, hunting lions, worshipping gods, and conducting ritual activities. The upper portions preserve traces of original paint. Cuneiform inscriptions on all sides record Ashurnasirpal's titles, genealogy, and achievements, including the famous description of his palace and the city of Kalhu.

**Historical Importance:** The White Obelisk represents one of the earliest surviving examples of Assyrian palace decoration in the round. It demonstrates the evolution of Assyrian royal imagery from the earlier Middle Assyrian period and establishes motifs that would characterize Neo-Assyrian art. The inscriptions provide crucial information about Ashurnasirpal's self-representation and royal ideology, including his carefully constructed genealogy linking him to earlier Assyrian kings.

**Discovery Story:** Sir Austen Henry Layard discovered this obelisk during his excavations at Nimrud in 1851. Like the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III, this monument came from the extensive palace complexes that Layard uncovered at what the Bible calls "Calah." The monument was found in a palace courtyard and was partially damaged, with some relief scenes broken away. It was transported to the British Museum along with numerous other artifacts from Layard's excavations.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Ashurnasirpal II's famous inscription describes a feast for 69,574 people during the palace inauguration
- The obelisk shows the earliest known depiction of the Assyrian king hunting lions
- Original pigments are still visible in some areas, showing the colorful original appearance
- The text includes a curse against anyone who would remove the obelisk from the palace
- Ashurnasirpal's reign marked the beginning of Assyrian military expansion that would create the empire
- The obelisk demonstrates the continuity of obelisk tradition from Sumer to Assyria

**Educational Summary:** The White Obelisk demonstrates the Assyrian king's role as both warrior-hunter and religious officiant. The combination of military and divine imagery in the reliefs reflects the twin foundations of Assyrian royal authority: military prowess securing the empire and proper worship maintaining divine favor. The monument illuminates the sophisticated iconographic programs that Assyrian kings employed to legitimize their rule and communicate their power to subjects and enemies alike.

**References:**
- Barnett, R.D. & Bleibtreu, E. "The Sculptures from the Southwest Palace at Nineveh." London: British Museum Press, 1998.
- Grayson, A.K. "Assyrian Rulers of the Early First Millennium BC (1114–859 BC)." Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1991.
- British Museum. Collection Database.

### 006. Stele of the Vultures

**Official Name:** Stele of the Vultures

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *ig-zi-ga* — "the vulture stele")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Early Dynastic Sumer

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic IIIa

**Approximate Date:** circa 2600–2350 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: N 1520)

**Discovery Site:** Tello (ancient Lagash), Iraq

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Victory Monument

**Material:** Limestone

**Dimensions:** Height: 180 cm (70.9 in); Width: 150 cm (59.1 in)

**Historical Description:** This limestone stele depicts the victory of Lagash over its rival city-state Umma in the Tigris-Euphrates border dispute. The upper register shows a dense array of vultures eating the bodies of fallen soldiers, while soldiers in phalanx formation advance with spears. Below, Eannatum of Lagash, wearing a tufted skirt and a helmet with a circular crest, leads infantry troops against the enemy. The inscription commemorates the victory and the boundary stones (*kudurru*) that established the new border.

**Historical Importance:** The Stele of the Vultures represents one of the earliest monumental narratives from Mesopotamia and provides crucial evidence for the military organization and royal ideology of Early Dynastic Sumer. The depiction of infantry in organized formation (*phalanx*) represents the earliest known representation of organized military units. The monument also demonstrates the sophisticated boundary dispute resolution between Sumerian city-states and the role of divine sanction in legitimizing territorial claims.

**Discovery Story:** French archaeologist Ernest de Sarzec discovered the stele during excavations at Tello (ancient Lagash) in 1880-1881. The site, located in southern Mesopotamia near modern Tell al-Hiba, had been occupied since the Early Dynastic period. The stele was found in a collapsed state and required reconstruction. It was transported to the Louvre, where it remains one of the most important examples of Early Dynastic art.

**Interesting Facts:**
- This is one of the oldest narrative monuments from Mesopotamia
- The vulture scene is the earliest known depiction of birds of prey eating fallen soldiers
- Eannatum is depicted wearing a helmet with a circular plate, possibly the earliest known helmet
- The inscription mentions the god Ningirsu and establishes divine sanction for the victory
- The monument illustrates the earliest known organized infantry formation
- It predates the famous Standard of Ur by approximately 200 years

**Educational Summary:** The Stele of the Vultures demonstrates that the tradition of royal monumental art celebrating military victory extends to the very beginnings of Mesopotamian civilization. The organized infantry formation depicted on the stele shows that military technology and organization were already sophisticated in the 3rd millennium BCE. The monument illuminates the intense rivalries between Sumerian city-states and the central role of warfare in Early Dynastic political life.

**References:**
- Civil, M. "The Stele of the Vultures: A Survey." In *The Tall al-Hamrin Story*, edited by J. Abu al-Soof, 1971.
- Musée du Louvre. "Art Sumérienne." Paris.
-正月, A. "Early Dynastic Terracotta and the Stele of the Vultures." Iraq 63 (2001).

### 007. Kurkh Monolith

**Official Name:** Kurkh Monolith

**Original Name:** (Assyrian: *aḫū* — "brother")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Neo-Assyrian Empire

**Historical Period:** Neo-Assyrian

**Approximate Date:** circa 860 BCE

**Current Museum:** The British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 118899)

**Discovery Site:** Kurkh (near Diyarbakır), Turkey

**Category:** Royal Inscription — Royal Annals

**Material:** Basalt

**Dimensions:** Height: 143 cm (56.3 in); Width: 68 cm (26.8 in)

**Historical Description:** This basalt monolith records the campaigns of Shalmaneser III, particularly his victory over a coalition of kings at the Battle of Karkar in 853 BCE. The inscription provides detailed accounts of Shalmaneser III's military campaigns against the kingdoms of Syria, Israel, Ammon, and the Neo-Hittite states. It mentions kings including Hadadezer of Aram-Damascus, Ahab of Israel, and others in what was one of the largest battles of the ancient Near East in the 9th century BCE.

**Historical Importance:** The Kurkh Monolith provides invaluable historical data for reconstructing the political landscape of the 9th century BCE. The inscription's account of the Battle of Karkar represents one of the earliest detailed descriptions of warfare in the ancient Near East and provides extra-biblical confirmation of historical figures including King Ahab of Israel. The monument demonstrates the systematic nature of Assyrian royal annals and their use for legitimizing royal power through documented military success.

**Discovery Story:** The monolith was discovered at Kurkh in eastern Turkey (ancient Mesopotamia) in 1861 by British archaeologist George Smith. Smith was searching for cuneiform inscriptions in the Ottoman Empire when he identified the monument near the modern town of Çüngüş. The monument was subsequently acquired by the British Museum and has been a key source for studying Assyrian history since its publication in 1871.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The battle of Karkar involved the largest coalition ever assembled against Assyria up to that time
- The inscription mentions King Ahab of Israel, providing extra-biblical confirmation of the Hebrew monarchy
- Shalmaneser III claims to have had 120,000 troops at Karkar
- The battle resulted in a tactical draw but was claimed as an Assyrian victory
- The inscription is written in Old Assyrian dialect
- Three versions of Shalmaneser III's Karkar campaign were found at different locations

**Educational Summary:** The Kurkh Monolith demonstrates how Assyrian kings maintained detailed historical records of their military campaigns. These royal annals served multiple purposes: documenting royal achievements for posterity, legitimizing royal authority through documented success, and providing administrative records for the empire. The monument provides crucial external confirmation for events mentioned in the Hebrew Bible and illuminates the complex political relationships between the kingdoms of the ancient Near East.

**References:**
- Grayson, A.K. "Assyrian Rulers of the Early First Millennium BC (858–745 BC)." Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1996.
- British Museum. Collection Database.
- Dalley, S. "The Mystery of the Hanging Garden." Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2013.

### 008. Cylinder Seal of King Sargon

**Official Name:** Cylinder Seal of Sargon of Akkad

**Original Name:** (Akkadian: *kunuk Šarru-kīn* — "seal of Sargon")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Akkadian Empire

**Historical Period:** Akkadian

**Approximate Date:** circa 2334–2279 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: AO 22313)

**Discovery Site:** Unknown (probably from southern Iraq)

**Category:** Royal Seal — Administrative

**Material:** Lapis lazuli

**Dimensions:** Height: 3.9 cm (1.5 in); Diameter: 2.5 cm (1 in)

**Historical Description:** This lapis lazuli cylinder seal depicts Sargon of Akkad, the founder of the world's first empire, in a scene of worship before a deity. The king, shown with a beard and wearing a characteristic fringed robe, stands before a seated god who holds a mace and water bucket. The inscription in Old Akkadian reads: "Sharru-kin, king of Kish, (appointed) by [the god] Enlil." The seal represents one of the earliest known examples of royal cylinder seals and demonstrates the sophisticated glyptic art of the Akkadian period.

**Historical Importance:** Sargon's seal provides crucial evidence for the king's self-representation and divine legitimation. The seal demonstrates that from the very beginning of Akkadian imperial ideology, kings sought divine sanction for their rule. The seal's size, material (lapis lazuli, imported from far-away Badakhshan), and sophisticated execution indicate the wealth and artistic sophistication of the Akkadian court.

**Discovery Story:** The seal's exact provenance is unknown, as it entered the antiquities market before systematic archaeological excavation became standard. It was acquired by the Louvre and is now recognized as one of the finest examples of Akkadian glyptic art. The seal's attribution to Sargon is based on the cuneiform inscription, which identifies the depicted figure as Sharru-kin (Sargon).

**Interesting Facts:**
- Sargon claims to have been "cupbearer" to King Ur-Zababa of Kish before seizing power
- He claimed to rule "from the Upper Sea to the Lower Sea" (Mediterranean to Persian Gulf)
- This seal may be the oldest known royal seal in existence
- Lapis lazuli was traded from Badakhshan (modern Afghanistan), showing extensive trade networks
- The seal was likely used to authenticate royal documents and decrees
- Sargon's reign founded the first multi-ethnic empire in history

**Educational Summary:** The Cylinder Seal of Sargon illuminates how ancient kings established legitimacy through divine sanction. The Akkadian kings pioneered the concept of the empire — a multi-ethnic political entity requiring sophisticated mechanisms of rule. The seal demonstrates the central role of religion in Akkadian political ideology and the importance of royal propaganda in maintaining imperial authority.

**References:**
- Frankfort, H. "Cylinder Seals." London: Macmillan, 1939.
- Musée du Louvre. Collection Database.
- Glassner, J.-J. "Mesopotamian Chronicles." Atlanta: Society of Biblical Literature, 2004.

### 009. Statue of Gudea

**Official Name:** Statue of Gudea of Lagash

**Original Name:** (Sumerian: *gudea* — "the one who has called")

**Civilization:** Mesopotamia — Third Dynasty of Lagash

**Historical Period:** Neo-Sumerian (Ur III/Lagash II)

**Approximate Date:** circa 2144–2124 BCE

**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (Museum Number: N 1520, N 1530, N 1535, N 1540)

**Discovery Site:** Girsu (ancient Lagash), Iraq

**Educational Summary:** The Standard of Ur demonstrates how early Mesopotamian kings presented themselves through visual art. **Historical Importance:** The lamassu are among the most iconic images of Assyrian art. The finds were transported to museums in Paris and London, where they created a sensation and established Assyrian art as a major field of study.

- The palace was abandoned after Sargon's death and never completed
- The winged sun symbol above represents the god Shamash

**Educational Summary:** The lamassu demonstrate how Assyrian kings used art to communicate power and protection. These composite creatures combined the strength of wild animals with human intelligence, representing the ideal guardian. Their massive scale and technical perfection announce the king's power to all who entered the palace.

**Dimensions:** Height: 15.2 cm (6 in); Width: 13.3 cm (5.2 in)

**Historical Description:** Tablet XI contains the famous story of Utnapishtim, the Babylonian Noah, who tells Gilgamesh the story of the Great Flood. This tablet includes the only surviving account of the flood from ancient Mesopotamia and provides crucial evidence for the relationship between Mesopotamian and biblical flood narratives. The text describes the building of the ark, the flood, and the gods' regret.

**Historical Importance:** Tablet XI of the Epic of Gilgamesh is one of the most important literary texts from the ancient Near East. It preserves the Mesopotamian account of the flood, which predates the biblical version by centuries. The tablet demonstrates the sophisticated literary culture of Mesopotamia and provides crucial evidence for understanding the transmission of narratives in the ancient Near East.

**Discovery Story:** This tablet was discovered in Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh in the mid-19th century. George Smith of the British Museum translated the tablet in 1872 and brought it to public attention. His translation caused enormous excitement and contributed to the development of biblical studies as a discipline.

**Interesting Facts:**

- Utnapishtim was granted immortality by the gods
- The tablet describes the building of the ark in detail
- The gods in the Mesopotamian version regret sending the flood
- The epic was composed over 1,000 years before this copy
- The tablet was part of a 12-tablet epic

**Educational Summary:** Tablet XI of the Epic of Gilgamesh demonstrates the sophistication of Mesopotamian literature and the ancient Near Eastern context for biblical narratives. The flood story provides crucial evidence for the transmission of narratives in the ancient world and the relationship between Mesopotamian and biblical literature.

**Historical Importance:** The Code of Lipit-Ishtar is one of the earliest known law codes from Mesopotamia. It demonstrates the tradition of royal legal reform that culminated in Hammurabi's famous code. **Discovery Story:** The code was discovered during excavations at Nippur conducted by American teams in the late 19th/early 20th century. The tablet was acquired by the Istanbul Archaeological Museum (now the Museum of the Ancient Orient) and has been studied by Assyriologists since its discovery.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Lipit-Ishtar ruled Isin for about 7 years
- The code is one of the earliest known law codes
- It predates Hammurabi by about 150 years
- The code establishes principles of fair treatment
- It addresses issues of slavery and debt
- The code invokes the god Shamash as the source of justice

**Educational Summary:** The Code of Lipit-Ishtar demonstrates the tradition of legal codification in Mesopotamia that culminated in Hammurabi's famous code. The code reveals the social and legal concerns of early 2nd millennium BCE Mesopotamia and the king's role as guarantor of justice and social order.

# Educational Framework

## Learning Objectives

1. **Historical Understanding**: Players will learn about the major civilizations of Mesopotamia, their political structures, and their achievements.
2. **Political Systems**: The collection demonstrates the evolution of kingship from city-state rulers to divine monarchs of vast empires.
3. **Cultural Significance**: Artifacts illuminate the religious, artistic, and administrative traditions of ancient Mesopotamia.
4. **Archaeological Discovery**: Each artifact includes the discovery story, introducing concepts of archaeology and the process of historical reconstruction.
5. **Global Connections**: Players will understand Mesopotamia's influence on later civilizations including ancient Israel, Persia, Greece, and Rome.

## Key Themes

- **Divine Kingship**: The evolution from representative of the gods to god-king
- **Imperial Administration**: How ancient empires were governed
- **Royal Propaganda**: How monuments communicated political power
- **Legal Tradition**: The development of codified law
- **Religious Practice**: The relationship between kings and gods
