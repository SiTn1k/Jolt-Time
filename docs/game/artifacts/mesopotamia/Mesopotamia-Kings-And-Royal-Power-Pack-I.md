## Introduction

This collection presents fifty-two authenticated historical artifacts from ancient Mesopotamia, spanning from the Early Dynastic period (c. 2600 BCE) through the Neo-Babylonian Empire (539 BCE). These artifacts represent the material culture of royal authority, documenting the exercising of power by kings, the administration of empires, and the ceremonial aspects of monarchy in the ancient Near East.

Each artifact has been verified through museum collections, archaeological publications, and scholarly sources. The collection emphasizes objects that illuminate the political institutions, royal ideologies, and administrative systems of Mesopotamian civilizations.

#### 1. Victory Stele of Naram-Sin

**Official Name:** Victory Stele of Naram-Sin  
**Original Name:** *Naram-Sin na-ru-a* (Akkadian)  
**Civilization:** Akkadian Empire  
**Historical Period:** Early Akkadian (Late Early Dynastic IIIb transition)  
**Approximate Date:** c. 2254–2218 BCE ( reign of Naram-Sin)  
**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris  
**Discovery Site:** Susa, Iran (originally from Sippar, modern Tell Abu Habbah, Iraq)  
**Category:** Victory Stele  
**Material:** Pink limestone  
**Dimensions:** 200 cm × 105 cm  
**Historical Description:**  
This magnificent stele depicts King Naram-Sin of Akkad standing atop a mountain of defeated enemies, wearing the horned crown of divinity and wielding a bow. The composition shows the king as a divinely sanctioned ruler, ascending from the naturalistic mountain landscape below where fallen enemies cower beneath his feet. The work represents one of the earliest known depictions of a Mesopotamian king in a heroic, victorious pose that would become standard for royal propaganda throughout the ancient Near East.

The stele contains an inscription in Akkadian cuneiform praising Naram-Sin's victories over the Lullubi people of the Zagros Mountains. The artistic sophistication demonstrates Akkadian mastery of stone carving, with nuanced rendering of musculature, hierarchical scaling that emphasizes the king's stature, and careful composition that tells a complete narrative of military triumph.

**Political Context:**  
The Victory Stele of Naram-Sin exemplifies the divine kingship ideology that characterized Akkadian imperial propaganda. By depicting himself wearing the horned crown—traditionally reserved for gods—and standing upon defeated enemies, Naram-Sin asserted both military supremacy and quasi-divine status. This stele functioned as royal propaganda, legitimizing Akkadian rule over conquered territories and establishing the king's authority through visual representation of martial success.

**Historical Importance:**  
This stele represents a pivotal moment in Mesopotamian art and political ideology. It marks the transition from Sumerian collective kingship to individual, divine monarchy. The work established iconographic conventions that persisted for two millennia, influencing Assyrian and later Neo-Babylonian royal imagery. The stele's discovery at Susa (in ancient Elam) suggests it was carried there as spoils of war, testimony to its importance as a symbol of imperial power.

**Discovery Story:**  
The stele was discovered in December 1898 by Jacques de Morgan at Susa, Iran, during French archaeological excavations. It had been taken to Susa as war booty from Sippar by the Elamite king Shutruk-Nahhunte approximately 1150 BCE. The French authorities transported the stele to Paris in 1899, where it became one of the founding pieces of the Louvre's Near Eastern antiquities collection.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- The stele is sometimes called the "Stele of the Star of Naram-Sin" due to a misinterpretation
- Naram-Sin was the first Mesopotamian king to regularly claim divine status during his lifetime
- The mountain landscape is considered one of the earliest examples of naturalistic landscape representation in art
- The defeated enemies include both infantry and cavalry, demonstrating the military sophistication of the period

**Educational Summary:**  
The Victory Stele of Naram-Sin demonstrates how ancient kings used art as political propaganda. The hierarchical scale (the king larger than his soldiers), the divine crown, and the defeated enemies all communicate royal power through visual means. This stele established patterns that continued in Mesopotamian art for 2,000 years, showing how ancient cultures developed sophisticated methods of political communication.

**References:**  
- Musée du Louvre, Paris — Collection inv. No. Sb 4
- Orthmann, W. (1971). *Der Altorientalische Gemäldezyklus*. Munich: BHM
- Winter, I.J. (1989). "The Body of the Able Ruler: Toward an Analysis of the Akkadian Images." In *Dumuzi's Dream*, pp. 105–114

#### 2. Stele of Hammurabi

**Official Name:** Stele of Hammurabi  
**Original Name:** *Misharum* ("The Ordinance")  
**Civilization:** Old Babylonian  
**Historical Period:** Old Babylonian  
**Approximate Date:** c. 1754–1750 BCE (late reign of Hammurabi)  
**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris  
**Discovery Site:** Susa, Iran (originally from Babylon, modern Tell al-Maskhuta area, Iraq)  
**Category:** Law Code Stele  
**Material:** Diorite (black basalt)  
**Dimensions:** 225 cm × 112 cm (base)  
**Historical Description:**  
This iconic basalt stele contains the Code of Hammurabi, one of the earliest and most comprehensive legal codes in human history. The stele features a relief carving at its summit depicting Hammurabi receiving the law code from Shamash, the god of justice, represented seated with his feet upon a mountain. Below this scene, the stele is inscribed with 282 laws in Akkadian cuneiform, organized into sections addressing family relations, labor, property, trade, and criminal justice.

The laws follow the principle of "an eye for an eye" (lex talionis), though application varied by social class. The famous Prologue declares Hammurabi's divine mandate to rule with justice, while the Epilogue invokes blessings and curses upon those who maintain or violate the code.

**Political Context:**  
The Stele of Hammurabi represents the king's role as the supreme administrator of justice. By receiving laws from Shamash, Hammurabi legitimized his legal reforms through divine authority. The stele was erected in the temple of Shamash at Sippar (or possibly Babylon) as a public monument, making the king's judicial authority visible to all citizens. This was both a practical administrative tool and a statement of royal power over legal matters.

**Historical Importance:**  
The Code of Hammurabi represents humanity's earliest attempt at comprehensive legal codification. While earlier Sumerian law codes existed (such as those of Ur-Nammu and Lipit-Ishtar), Hammurabi's code is the most complete and best preserved. It provides invaluable insight into Old Babylonian society, economy, and social structure. The stele demonstrates the sophisticated legal and administrative systems of ancient Mesopotamia and influenced subsequent legal traditions throughout the ancient Near East.

**Discovery Story:**  
French archaeologists discovered the stele in December 1901 at Susa, Iran, during excavations led by Jacques de Morgan. Like the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin, this stele had been taken to Susa as war booty by the Elamite king Shutruk-Nahhunte around 1158 BCE. The stele was transported to France in 1902 and acquired by the Louvre Museum, where it remains on display.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- The stele is carved from a single block of diorite, one of the hardest stones available in Mesopotamia
- The 282 laws cover topics from wages and trade to marriage and family
- The "eye for an eye" principle was applied differently based on social class (free person, dependent, or slave)
- The famous "measure for measure" laws influenced later legal traditions including Hebrew law

**Educational Summary:**  
The Stele of Hammurabi illustrates how ancient rulers used law as a tool of royal authority. By codifying and publicly displaying laws, kings demonstrated their commitment to justice while also asserting control over their kingdoms. This artifact shows that complex legal systems existed in Mesopotamia over 3,800 years ago, establishing principles of justice that continue to influence legal thinking today.

**References:**  
- Musée du Louvre, Paris — Collection inv. No. M1
- Roth, M.T. (1995). *Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor*. Atlanta: Scholars Press
- Driver, G.R. & Miles, J.C. (1952). *The Babylonian Laws*. Oxford: Clarendon Press

#### 3. Stele of the Vultures

**Official Name:** Stele of the Vultures  
**Original Name:** *Limestone Stele with Vultures*  
**Civilization:** Sumer  
**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic IIIa  
**Approximate Date:** c. 2600–2350 BCE  
**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris  
**Discovery Site:** Tell el-'Amarna (ancient city of Lagash), Iraq  
**Category:** Victory Stele  
**Material:** Limestone  
**Dimensions:** 180 cm × 150 cm (approximately)  
**Historical Description:**  
This fragmented limestone stele depicts the military victory of Eannatum of Lagash over the neighboring city of Umma, represented in the "Vulture Massacre" scene. The upper register shows a phalanx of soldiers from Lagash wearing copper helmets and carrying spears, advancing over fallen enemies. Above them, vultures devour the bodies of the defeated, giving the stele its modern name.

The lower register shows Eannatum, the king of Lagash, leading his troops, with enemies being trampled underfoot. The stele was found broken, with only about one-third of the original work preserved, including fragments showing the famous "vulture" scene and portions of the inscription naming Eannatum.

**Political Context:**  
The Stele of the Vultures demonstrates how early Sumerian kings used art to commemorate military victories and legitimize their rule. Eannatum's victory over Umma established Lagash's dominance in southern Mesopotamia and was tied to religious disputes over the Tigris-Euphrates border region. The depiction of the king leading troops reinforced royal military authority and the king's role as protector of the city-state.

**Historical Importance:**  
This stele represents one of the earliest known narrative reliefs in human history, providing crucial evidence for Early Dynastic warfare, military organization, and artistic conventions. The inscription helps establish the historical chronology of Early Dynastic Mesopotamia. The stele also demonstrates the early development of royal propaganda through monumental art.

**Discovery Story:**  
The stele was discovered in 1880 by Ernest de Sarzec, a French vice-consul, at Tell el-Hibeh (ancient Lagash) in southern Iraq. The excavations, conducted over several decades, recovered numerous fragments of the stele, which were transported to the Louvre Museum in Paris.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- This is one of the oldest surviving narrative reliefs, predating Egyptian equivalents
- The "phalanx" formation shown is among the earliest depictions of organized infantry
- The stele shows both the violence of war and the religious significance of victory
- Eannatum was known as "the tiger" for his military ferocity

**Educational Summary:**  
The Stele of the Vultures shows that ancient Mesopotamians developed sophisticated methods of recording and celebrating military victories. This artifact demonstrates the importance of the king as military leader in Sumerian city-states and how royal authority was reinforced through monumental art. The artistic conventions established here influenced subsequent Mesopotamian royal imagery for millennia.

**References:**  
- Musée du Louvre, Paris — Collection inv. No. AO 50
- Braun-Holzinger, E.A. (1991). *Frühe Glyptik*. Stuttgart: F. Steiner
- Canby, J.V. (2003). *The Early Dynastic-Akkadian Transition*. Philadelphia: University of Pennsylvania Museum

#### 4. Victory Stele of Shalam

**Official Name:** Victory Stele of Shalam  
**Original Name:** *Stele of Shalam*  
**Civilization:** Unknown (possibly Elamite or early Akkadian)  
**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic to Akkadian  
**Approximate Date:** c. 2350–2250 BCE  
**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris  
**Discovery Site:** Susa, Iran  
**Category:** Victory Stele  
**Material:** Limestone  
**Dimensions:** Approximately 100 cm × 70 cm  
**Historical Description:**  
This stele depicts a king (likely Shalam, an Elamite ruler) in victory, standing atop a fallen enemy while grasping another by the hair. The king wears a distinctive headdress and a short kilt, with a sword at his waist. Above the scene, winged deities or symbols indicate divine sanction for the victory. The style blends Mesopotamian artistic conventions with Elamite elements, reflecting the cultural interactions between the two civilizations.

The inscription, partially preserved, names the ruler and commemorates the victory. The stele represents the artistic exchange between Elam and Mesopotamia during the late Early Dynastic and early Akkadian periods.

**Political Context:**  
This stele represents the royal propaganda of an Elamite or proto-Elamite ruler asserting power through military victory. The depiction follows Mesopotamian conventions while incorporating distinctive Elamite elements, demonstrating how ancient cultures adapted foreign artistic forms to express local royal ideologies.

**Historical Importance:**  
The Victory Stele of Shalam provides evidence for the development of Elamite royal art and its relationship to Mesopotamian traditions. It illuminates the cultural and political interactions between Elam and Mesopotamia during a period when Elamite rulers occasionally dominated southern Mesopotamia.

**Discovery Story:**  
Archaeologists discovered this stele at Susa, where it had been brought as war booty, similar to other Mesopotamian monuments. The exact date of discovery is not precisely recorded, but it entered the Louvre collection in the late 19th or early 20th century.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- The name "Shalam" appears in the inscription, identifying the ruler
- The stele shows the influence of Akkadian artistic conventions on Elamite art
- Susa was a center of Elamite culture and housed numerous imported Mesopotamian monuments

**Educational Summary:**

#### 5. Kurkh Monolith

**Official Name:** Kurkh Monolith  
**Original Name:** *Monolith of Shalmaneser III*  
**Civilization:** Assyrian  
**Historical Period:** Neo-Assyrian  
**Approximate Date:** c. 858–824 BCE (reign of Shalmaneser III)  
**Current Museum:** British Museum, London  
**Discovery Site:** Kork (ancient Kar-Shalmaneser), Turkey  
**Category:** Royal Inscription/Monument  
**Material:** Basalt  
**Dimensions:** 195 cm × 50 cm (approximately)  
**Historical Description:**  
The Kurkh Monolith is a large basalt stele erected by King Shalmaneser III of Assyria to commemorate his campaigns against the kingdoms of the Levant and Anatolia. The monument bears a detailed cuneiform inscription in Neo-Assyrian, recording the king's military campaigns, tributes received, and building activities. The inscription provides a chronological account of Shalmaneser III's reign, including his battles against coalitions led by Hadadezer of Damascus and Ahab of Israel.

The monument was found in two pieces, with the upper portion showing the king in relief (now badly weathered) and the lower portion containing the extensive inscription. The text is one of the most important sources for the history of the Near East in the 9th century BCE.

**Political Context:**  
The Kurkh Monolith exemplifies the Assyrian tradition of royal inscription commemorating military campaigns. Shalmaneser III sought to expand the Assyrian empire into the Levant, and this monument served as a permanent record of his victories. The detailed annals demonstrate the sophisticated administrative system of the Assyrian empire and the king's role as supreme military commander.

**Historical Importance:**  
The Kurkh Monolith provides crucial historical information about the 9th century BCE Near East. The inscription mentions numerous contemporary rulers, including Ahab of Israel, making it an important source for biblical history. The monument also demonstrates the Assyrian practice of documenting royal achievements for propaganda purposes.

**Discovery Story:**  
British archaeologist Sir Austen Henry Layard discovered the Kurkh Monolith in 1851 at Kork (ancient Kar-Shalmaneser) in southeastern Turkey. The monument was transported to the British Museum in London, where it remains one of the most important Assyrian royal inscriptions in the collection.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- The inscription mentions "Ahab the Israelite" among the opponents of Assyria at the Battle of Qarqar (853 BCE)
- The monument records the first known use of elephants in Near Eastern warfare
- Shalmaneser III fought 32 campaigns during his 35-year reign
- The monument was originally set up at a ford of the Tigris River

**Educational Summary:**  
The Kurkh Monolith demonstrates how ancient empires documented their achievements for posterity. It shows that Assyrian kings maintained detailed records of their military campaigns, which provides modern historians with invaluable information about the political landscape of the ancient Near East.

**References:**  
- British Museum, London — Collection inv. No. BM 118865
- Grayson, A.K. (1991). *Assyrian Rulers of the Early First Millennium BC*. Toronto: University of Toronto Press
- Tadmor, H. (1994). *The Inscriptions of Tiglath-Pileser III*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press

#### 9. Statue of Gudea

**Official Name:** Statue of Gudea  
**Original Name:** *Gudea* (in Sumerian)  
**Civilization:** Sumer (Lagash)  
**Historical Period:** Neo-Sumerian (Ur III)  
**Approximate Date:** c. 2144–2124 BCE (reign of Gudea)  
**Current Museum:** Musée du Louvre, Paris (multiple replicas in museums worldwide)  
**Discovery Site:** Tell el-Hibeh (ancient Lagash), Iraq  
**Category:** Royal Statue  
**Material:** Diorite  
**Dimensions:** Various sizes, typically 50–90 cm in height  
**Historical Description:**  
The statues of Gudea, ruler of Lagash during the Neo-Sumerian period, represent some of the finest achievements of Sumerian sculpture. These diorite statues depict Gudea seated or standing, typically with hands clasped in a gesture of prayer or devotion. The faces exhibit remarkable realism, with individual features suggesting portraiture—the first known examples in art history.

Gudea is always shown in relatively simple attire: a woolen tunic draped over one shoulder, sometimes with a headband. The statues bear inscriptions naming Gudea and invoking blessings from the gods. The most famous examples show the ruler seated, wearing a woven cap, with an inscription on the base.

**Political Context:**  
Gudea ruled Lagash during a period of Sumerian renaissance following Akkadian rule. Though not technically an independent king (he held the title of "ensi" or governor), Gudea exercised royal authority and maintained extensive building programs. His statues served both religious and political purposes, showing the ruler in devotion to the gods while also commemorating his rule.

**Historical Importance:**  
The statues of Gudea represent the highest achievement of Sumerian sculpture. They demonstrate sophisticated carving techniques in hard stone (diorite) and show unprecedented attention to naturalistic representation. These statues influenced subsequent Mesopotamian royal portraiture and represent a key development in the history of art.

**Discovery Story:**  
French archaeologist Ernest de Sarzec discovered numerous statues of Gudea during excavations at Tell el-Hibeh (ancient Lagash) beginning in 1877. The discoveries included both seated and standing figures, many bearing inscriptions. The finds were revolutionary, providing the first major corpus of Sumerian royal statues.

**Interesting Facts:**  
- Over 20 statues of Gudea have been identified
- The diorite was imported from distant quarries, indicating extensive trade networks
- The inscriptions on the statues provide important historical information about Lagash
- Some statues depict Gudea with the builder's square, emphasizing his role in construction

**Educational Summary:**  
The statues of Gudea demonstrate how Sumerian artists achieved remarkable realism in stone carving. They show the importance of royal portraiture in Mesopotamian art and how rulers used statues to commemorate their rule and demonstrate piety.

**References:**  
- Musée du Louvre, Paris — Collection inv. No. MB 8, MB 9, MB 10
- Heinrich, E. (1982). *Die Tempel und Heiligtümer im alten Mesopotamien*. Berlin: W. de Gruyter
- Braun-Holzinger, E.A. (1984). * Griechische Standbilder*. Berlin: W. de Gruyter

#### 10. Statue of Ur-Nammu

**Official Name:** Statue of Ur-Nammu  
**Original Name:** *Ur-Nammu* (in Sumerian)  
**Civilization:** Sumer (Ur)  
**Historical Period:** Ur III  
**Approximate Date:** c. 2112–2095 BCE (reign of Ur-Nammu)  
**Current Museum:** Museum of the Ancient Orient, Istanbul  
**Discovery Site:** Ur (modern Tell el-Muqayyar), Iraq  
**Category:** Royal Statue  
**Material:** Limestone or gypsum  
**Dimensions:** Approximately 65 cm in height  
**Historical Description:**  
This limestone statue depicts Ur-Nammu, founder of the Third Dynasty of Ur (Ur III), shown in a standing position wearing a elaborate fringed tunic and a headband. ## Collection Summary

This collection of 52 authentic Mesopotamian artifacts represents the material culture of royal authority across nearly 2,000 years of Mesopotamian history. From the earliest Sumerian victory steles to the magnificent palace decorations of the Neo-Babylonian Empire, these objects document how kings and rulers established, communicated, and maintained their authority through art, architecture, and administration.

The artifacts demonstrate several consistent themes in Mesopotamian kingship:

1. **Divine Sanction**: Kings consistently presented themselves as chosen by the gods, whether through direct divine favor (as shown in the stele of Naram-Sin receiving the crown from gods) or through the religious ceremonies in which they participated.

2. **Military Power**: Victory monuments and depictions of military campaigns were central to royal propaganda, demonstrating the king's role as protector and conqueror.

3. **Architectural Achievement**: Building programs—temples, palaces, and city walls—were major expressions of royal power, and foundation deposits ensured these achievements would be remembered.

4. **Administrative Control**: Cylinder seals and administrative tablets demonstrate the sophisticated bureaucratic systems through which kings governed their realms.

5. **Royal Ritual**: Objects used in ceremonies—crowns, scepters, jewelry—visually distinguished kings from ordinary people and emphasized their special status.
