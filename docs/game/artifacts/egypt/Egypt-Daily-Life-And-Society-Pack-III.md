## Collection Philosophy

The Daily Life & Society collection reveals the world of ordinary Egyptians — the farmers, craftsmen, merchants, and families who made up the fabric of ancient Egyptian civilization. While temples and tombs have yielded spectacular treasures, it is the everyday objects that truly illuminate how Egyptians lived, worked, and interacted with one another.

This collection draws from archaeological excavations of homes, workshops, and villages, as well as tomb scenes that depict daily activities. Each artifact has been verified through museum collections and academic publications.

### 001. Black-Topped Red Pottery Jar

**Official Name:** Black-Topped Redware Jar

**Original Name:** 𓆄 (ḥm — "Jar")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** Early Dynastic

**Approximate Date:** circa 3100–2686 BCE

**Current Museum:** Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (Museum Number: 26.7.1406)

**Discovery Site:** Hierakonpolis, Upper Egypt

**Category:** Pottery — Storage Vessel

**Material:** Nile silt clay, burnished

**Dimensions:** Height: 28 cm; Diameter: 22 cm

**Historical Description:** This cylindrical jar is made of red-brown Nile clay with a distinctive blackened top, created by inverting the jar during firing so the smoke would blacken the rim. The jar's surface is highly burnished, creating a smooth, almost glossy finish. Such jars were among the most common pottery forms in early Egypt, used for storing grains, water, and other provisions.

**Social Context:** Black-topped redware was the basic container of Egyptian households throughout the Predynastic and Early Dynastic periods. Every household would have owned several such jars for basic storage needs. The distinctive black top was not merely decorative but a byproduct of the firing technique.

**Historical Importance:** This jar represents one of the most characteristic and long-lived pottery forms in Egyptian history. The tradition of black-topped pottery continued for over a thousand years, demonstrating the conservatism of Egyptian material culture.

**Discovery Story:** Hierakonpolis, the ancient capital of Upper Egypt in the Predynastic period, was excavated in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The site yielded numerous pottery vessels and other artifacts that document the transition from prehistory to the dynastic period.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The black-topped technique required specific firing conditions in inverted position
- Similar pottery has been found across Egypt, showing widespread standardization
- The jars were sometimes decorated with simple painted designs
- The form remained essentially unchanged for over 1,500 years

**Educational Summary:** The black-topped jar represents the foundation of Egyptian material culture — simple, functional, and beautifully made. The consistency of this pottery form across time and space shows the remarkable continuity of Egyptian household traditions.

**References:**
- Petrie, W.M.F. "Ceramic Art in Ancient Egypt." London, 1903.
- Metropolitan Museum of Art. Collection Online.
