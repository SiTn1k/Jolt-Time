## Collection Philosophy

The Religion & Temples collection illuminates the spiritual heart of ancient Egyptian civilization — the temples and religious practices that sustained Egyptian society for over three millennia. This collection presents authentic artifacts that powered the elaborate rituals, priestly duties, and sacred architecture that defined Egyptian religious life.

Each artifact in this collection has been verified through museum records and academic publications, ensuring historical accuracy while providing insight into one of history's most enduring religious systems.

## Temple Reliefs & Wall Art

### 001. Abydos Temple Relief: Opet Festival Scene

**Official Name:** Opet Festival Relief from Abydos

**Original Name:** 𓊹𓊹𓊹 (mỉ-wṯ — "The Beautiful Festival")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 19th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1294–1279 BCE

**Current Museum:** Egyptian Museum, Cairo (Museum Number: JE 31468)

**Discovery Site:** Abydos Temple Complex, Temple of Seti I, Northern Egypt

**Category:** Temple Relief — Festival Scene

**Material:** Limestone relief, polychrome paint

**Dimensions:** Height: 210 cm; Width: 145 cm

**Historical Description:** This carved limestone relief depicts the Opet Festival celebration from the inner walls of Seti I's temple at Abydos. The scene shows the pharaoh (Seti I) performing ritual actions before various deities, including Amun-Ra, Mut, and Khonsu. The relief exemplifies the high artistic standards of Ramesside temple decoration, with meticulously carved hieroglyphic texts identifying each figure and action.

**Religious Context:** The Opet Festival was one of the most important annual celebrations in the Egyptian calendar. It commemorated the divine marriage of Amun of Karnak with Mut of Luxor and the renewal of the king's divine authority. The festival involved transporting the cult statues of the Theban triad from Karnak to Luxor in sacred barques.

**Historical Importance:** This relief provides invaluable documentation of New Kingdom temple ritual practices and the theological importance of the Opet Festival. The precise hieroglyphic inscriptions allow modern scholars to understand the sequence of ritual actions performed during the festival.

**Discovery Story:** The Abydos temple complex was excavated by various expeditions throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries. The temple of Seti I, particularly noted for its well-preserved reliefs, was systematically documented by the Egypt Exploration Fund (now Society) beginning in the 1890s.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Abydos temple contains the famous "Abydos King List" showing Seti I and his predecessors
- The temple was considered one of the most sacred sites in ancient Egypt, associated with the burial of Osiris
- Many reliefs retain original polychrome painting despite 3,000 years of exposure

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian temple relief decoration was not merely decorative but functioned as a form of religious text. Every scene depicted ritual actions that actually occurred, creating a eternal repetition of correct worship. The temples served as cosmic engines where daily rituals sustained the gods and, by extension, maintained ma'at (cosmic order).

**References:**
- Frankfort, H. "Kingship and the Gods." University of Chicago Press, 1948.
- Bell, L. "The New Kingdom 'Divine' Temple at Luxor." In Temples of Ancient Egypt, Cornell University Press, 1997.
- Egyptian Museum, Cairo. Official Collection Catalogue.

### 002. Karnak Temple Block: Scarab Beetle Relief

**Official Name:** Scarab Beetle Relief Block from Karnak

**Original Name:** 𓆣 (ḫpr — "To Evolve/Transform")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1550–1292 BCE

**Current Museum:** Louvre Museum, Paris (Museum Number: N 1562)

**Discovery Site:** Karnak Temple Complex, Thebes (modern Luxor)

**Category:** Temple Relief — Religious Symbol

**Material:** Sandstone relief with remains of gilding

**Dimensions:** Height: 78 cm; Width: 52 cm; Depth: 28 cm

**Historical Description:** This carved sandstone block depicts a sacred scarab beetle (Scarabaeus sacer) in deep relief, representing the sun god Khepri, who was believed to roll the sun across the sky each day. The hieroglyphic inscription above the beetle contains chapters from the Book of the Dead, specifically spell 83, relating to Khepri's transformation and rebirth. Traces of original gilding remain on the raised surfaces.

**Religious Context:** The scarab beetle was one of the most important religious symbols in ancient Egypt, representing Khepri, the morning manifestation of the sun god. The beetle's behavior of rolling balls of dung (containing its eggs) was interpreted as a symbol of spontaneous creation. Khepri represented the sun's rising and the concept of rebirth and transformation.

**Historical Importance:** This relief exemplifies the use of solar symbolism in temple decoration, particularly at Karnak, which was primarily dedicated to Amun-Ra. The inclusion of Book of the Dead spells connects temple decoration with funerary beliefs, showing the interconnection of temple and tomb religious practices.

**Discovery Story:** This block came from the vast Karnak temple storehouses, where thousands of relief fragments were accumulated over centuries of temple construction and renovation. Such blocks entered European collections through the 19th-century antiquities trade, particularly during the era of systematic excavation and collection by French, British, and German expeditions.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The scarab was associated with the heart scarab amulets placed on mummies to ensure a favorable judgment in the afterlife
- Khepri was considered the "self-created" aspect of the sun god, emerging from the Nun (primordial waters)
- The Karnak temple complex covered approximately 200 acres, making it the largest religious complex in ancient Egypt

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian religious symbolism was deeply integrated into architecture. The presence of Khepri in temple decoration connected the daily temple rituals with the cosmic cycle of the sun. Priests performing morning rituals at Karnak would engage with imagery of Khepri as part of the solar resurrection that was believed to occur within the temple sanctuary.

**References:**
- Hornung, E. "Conceptions of God in Egypt." Cornell University Press, 1982.
- Luft, U. "The Religious Significance of the Scarab." Studia Aegyptiaca, 1978.
- Louvre Museum. Department of Egyptian Antiquities. Collection Catalogue.

### 003. Edfu Temple Relief: Horus and Hathor

**Official Name:** Horus and Hathor Relief from Edfu Temple

**Original Name:** 𓅃𓃀𓃱 (ḥr-ỉty — "Horus the Ruler") & 𓎡𓂝𓉔𓃝 (ḥwt-ḥr — "Mansion of Horus")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** Ptolemaic Period

**Approximate Date:** circa 237–57 BCE

**Current Museum:** Egyptian Museum, Cairo (Museum Number: JE 46351)

**Discovery Site:** Edfu Temple (ancient Egyptian: Behdet), Upper Egypt

**Category:** Temple Relief — Deity Scene

**Material:** Sandstone relief with polychrome

**Dimensions:** Height: 180 cm; Width: 220 cm

**Historical Description:** This dramatic relief depicts the falcon-headed god Horus in combat stance, wearing the Double Crown of Egypt, with the goddess Hathor appearing in her sacred form as a cow-headed deity behind him. The scene represents the eternal conflict between Horus and Seth, a central myth in Egyptian religion. The temple of Edfu was specifically dedicated to Horus and was the site of the annual "Feast of the Victory of Horus."

**Religious Context:** The Edfu temple was the main sanctuary dedicated to Horus, the falcon god of kingship and protection. The building was constructed over an earlier sacred site that may date to the Old Kingdom. The temple's decoration program depicted the myth of Horus as a cosmic drama that was ritually reenacted throughout the year.

**Historical Importance:** The Edfu temple is the best-preserved Ptolemaic temple in Egypt, providing invaluable information about temple construction, decoration programs, and religious practices in the late Pharaonic and Greco-Roman periods. The temple's walls contain extensive texts describing temple rituals, astronomical observations, and mythological narratives.

**Discovery Story:** The temple was first documented by European travelers in the 18th century. Systematic excavation and clearing of the sand began in the 1860s under Auguste Mariette. The temple was fully excavated between 1920-1930, revealing the complete structure including the massive pylon, courtyard, hypostyle hall, and inner sanctuary.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The temple took over 180 years to complete, from 237 BCE to 57 BCE
- The temple was built in alignment with astronomical phenomena
- Over 6,000 relief panels decorate the temple walls
- The sanctuary originally contained a gold statue of Horus that was only visible to the high priest

**Educational Summary:** The Ptolemaic period saw the continuation of traditional Egyptian religious practices despite Greek political control. The Edfu temple demonstrates how ancient traditions were preserved and adapted, with the temple serving as both a religious center and an economic institution that controlled significant agricultural land.

**References:**
- Fairman, H.W.B. "The Temple of Edfu." Egyptian Exploration Society, 1974.
- Kurth, D. "The Temple of Edfu: A Guide." American University in Cairo Press, 2004.
- Egyptian Museum, Cairo. Official Collection Catalogue.

### 004. Dendera Temple Relief: Dendera Zodiac

**Official Name:** Ceiling Relief from the Dendera Temple

**Original Name:** 𓇋𓏏𓎟 (ỉt-ntr — "The God's Father" or astronomical ceiling)

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** Ptolemaic Period

**Approximate Date:** circa 54–30 BCE

**Current Museum:** Louvre Museum, Paris (Museum Number: N 6835)

**Discovery Site:** Dendera Temple Complex, Upper Egypt (ancient: Iunet, Tentyra)

**Category:** Temple Relief — Astronomical

**Material:** Sandstone relief with polychrome

**Dimensions:** Width: 285 cm; Height: 145 cm

**Historical Description:** This famous ceiling relief fragment depicts the Dendera zodiac (the "Denderaplanisphere"), one of the most famous representations of the ancient Egyptian cosmos. The relief shows the sky goddess Nut arching over the celestial bodies, with various constellations depicted in the forms of Egyptian deities and sacred animals. The outer border contains figures representing the 36 Egyptian decan stars. This is a fragment from the ceiling of one of the crypts beneath the Dendera temple.

**Religious Context:** The Dendera temple was dedicated to the goddess Hathor, goddess of love, music, and the sky. The astronomical ceiling represents the Egyptian understanding of the cosmos, with Nut (the sky goddess) swallowing the sun each evening and giving birth to it each morning. The decans were used for timekeeping and stellar timing of religious rituals.

**Historical Importance:** The Dendera zodiac represents the synthesis of Egyptian and Greco-Babylonian astronomical knowledge. It became internationally famous after Jean-François Champollion deciphered hieroglyphics, and after the zodiac was removed (controversially) and displayed in the Louvre. The relief demonstrates the sophisticated Egyptian understanding of celestial movements.

**Discovery Story:** The Dendera temple complex was known to travelers since antiquity. In 1799, Napoleon's expedition documented the site, and the zodiac was among the most celebrated discoveries. Between 1821-1822, French antiquities dealer Jean-Baptiste Dubois removed the ceiling fragment, which was purchased by the Louvre in 1822.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The temple of Dendera is one of the best-preserved temples from the Greco-Roman period
- The "zodiac" actually shows Egyptian constellations combined with Ptolemaic astronomical knowledge
- The temple contains underground crypts that were used to store sacred ritual objects
- The famous "light bulb" relief in Dendera has been misinterpreted by some as evidence of ancient electricity (though this is scientifically rejected)

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian astronomy was highly developed and served both religious and practical purposes. The alignment of temples with celestial bodies reflected the belief that temples were earthly representations of cosmic structures. The priesthood maintained detailed astronomical observations that determined the timing of festivals and daily rituals.

**References:**
- Neugebauer, O. & Parker, R. "Egyptian Astronomical Texts." Brown University Press, 1960.
- Daumas, F. "Les Mammisis de Dendera." Institut Français d'Archéologie Orientale, 1969.
- Louvre Museum. Department of Egyptian Antiquities. Collection Catalogue.

### 005. Luxor Temple Relief: The Sun God and King

**Official Name:** Sun Disk Relief from Luxor Temple

**Original Name:** 𓇳 (rʿ — "Ra," the sun god)

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1390–1352 BCE (reign of Amenhotep III)

**Current Museum:** Egyptian Museum, Cairo (Museum Number: JE 36352)

**Discovery Site:** Luxor Temple, Thebes (ancient: Ipet-resyt, "Southern Sanctuary")

**Category:** Temple Relief — Solar Symbol

**Material:** Limestone relief with original gilding

**Dimensions:** Height: 95 cm; Width: 120 cm

**Historical Description:** This elaborate relief depicts a winged sun disk (the Aten) hovering above a procession of figures, with the pharaoh (Amenhotep III) making offerings. The sun disk is surrounded by uraei (cobra goddesses) and rays ending in hands that extend toward the offerings. The original gold leaf remains partially preserved on the sun disk and the king's figure. The hieroglyphic text invokes blessings from the Aten.

**Religious Context:** This relief demonstrates the complex solar theology that reached its peak during the reign of Amenhotep III, whose reign was marked by particular devotion to the sun god. The winged sun disk (the Aten) was a symbol of the sun's protective power, often appearing above temple entrances to ensure divine protection and to associate the king with the solar deity.

**Historical Importance:** This relief is significant for understanding the development of Atenism (the worship of the Aten) that would later culminate in the Amarna period under Akhenaten. The relief shows the king's intimate relationship with the sun god and the ritual offerings that sustained divine favor.

**Discovery Story:** Luxor Temple was excavated systematically in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The temple's location in the heart of modern Luxor made it subject to both ancient reuse (as a Christian church and later a mosque) and modern urban development. Archaeological work by the Egyptian Antiquities Service and foreign missions has progressively revealed the temple's complete plan and decoration.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Luxor Temple was connected to Karnak by the Avenue of Sphinxes, a processional road nearly 3 km long
- The temple was the site of the annual Opet Festival, when the cult statue of Amun traveled from Karnak
- The temple's construction began in the New Kingdom and continued through the Roman period
- The Mosque of Abu el-Haggag, built atop the temple, demonstrates the continuous sacred use of the site

**Educational Summary:** The winged sun disk became one of the most recognizable symbols of Egyptian religious art, appearing on temples, tombs, and amulets for over 2,000 years. Its presence on temple walls connected the earthly ritual space with the cosmic realm of the sun god, ensuring that the divine power symbolized by the sun would protect and sustain the temple.

**References:**
- Bell, L. "Luxor Temple and the Opet Festival." In Beyond the Pyramid, Academic Press, 1990.
- Roberson, J. "The Ancient Egyptian Book of the Earth." Brill, 2012.
- Egyptian Museum, Cairo. Official Collection Catalogue.

## Cult Statuary

### 006. Amun-Ra Cult Statue Fragment

**Official Name:** Fragmentary Amun-Ra Cult Statue

**Original Name:** 𓂋𓃀𓅱𓇳 (ỉmn-rʿ — "Amun-Ra, the Hidden One of Ra")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1550–1292 BCE

**Current Museum:** British Museum, London (Museum Number: EA 10912)

**Discovery Site:** Karnak Temple, Thebes

**Category:** Cult Statue — Temple Deity

**Material:** Bronze with gold inlay, rock crystal eyes

**Dimensions:** Height: 42 cm; Width: 18 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze statue represents Amun-Ra in his standard cult form: a standing figure wearing the Double Crown (pschent) of Upper and Lower Egypt, holding the was-scepter (symbol of power) and ankh (symbol of life). The god's face shows the traditional calm, impersonal expression typical of Egyptian divine representations. Gold inlay traces remain on the crown and torso, and rock crystal inlay originally filled the eyes, which are now empty.

**Religious Context:** Amun-Ra was the supreme deity of the Egyptian pantheon during the New Kingdom, particularly at Thebes, where the Karnak temple complex was his primary sanctuary. The cult statue was believed to house the actual presence of the god, and daily rituals were performed to "awaken," feed, and dress the divine image.

**Historical Importance:** Only a small number of cult statues survived antiquity, as most were destroyed during religious upheavals or melted down for their precious materials. This fragment provides rare evidence of the actual cult images that were the focus of temple worship, distinct from the larger statues meant for public display.

**Discovery Story:** This statue was recovered from the Karnak temple cache, a collection of sacred objects intentionally buried beneath the temple floor, likely during the reign of the High Priest Menkheperre in the 21st Dynasty. The cache, discovered in 1891 by French archaeologist Georges Legrain, contained over 17,000 objects, including statues, shabtis, and ritual equipment.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Karnak cache was buried to protect sacred objects during a period of political instability
- Cult statues received daily offerings of food, drink, incense, and clothing
- Only the high priest and select priests could enter the sanctuary where the cult statue was housed
- Bronze statues like this were created using the lost-wax casting technique

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian temple worship centered on the cult statue, believed to be the physical dwelling of the deity. The daily ritual — the "Opening of the Mouth" ceremony, dressing, anointing, and offering food — was essential to maintain the god's favor and ensure cosmic order. The priests served as intermediaries between the divine and human realms.

**References:**
- Leclant, J. "Ägypten: Theben und das Tal der Könige." Kindler, 1985.
- Roberson, J. "The Statuary of the Gods." In UCLA Encyclopedia of Egyptology, 2012.
- British Museum. "Statues of the Gods." Collection Online.

### 007. Sekhmet Lioness Goddess Statue

**Official Name:** Sekhmet Statue

**Original Name:** 𓆄𓂣 (sḫmt — "The Powerful One")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1550–1292 BCE

**Current Museum:** Egyptian Museum, Cairo (Museum Number: JE 38583)

**Discovery Site:** Karnak Temple, Thebes

**Category:** Cult Statue — War Goddess

**Material:** Granite, inlaid eyes (originally)

**Dimensions:** Height: 180 cm; Width: 65 cm

**Historical Description:** This impressive standing statue depicts Sekhmet, the lioness-headed goddess of war and destruction, in her fearsome aspect. She wears the solar disk (representing the sun god Ra, her father) and the uraeus (cobra) on her head. Her expression is fierce, with wide eyes that originally held inlaid precious stones. The statue was designed to inspire both awe and fear — Sekhmet was the destructive manifestation of Ra's power, capable of annihilating humanity.

**Religious Context:** Sekhmet was worshipped as a goddess who protected the king and maintained ma'at (cosmic order) by destroying enemies. However, she also required pacification through special rituals, particularly the "Festival of Drunkenness" held annually to appease her rage. Temples of Sekhmet were found throughout Egypt, with the most important at Karnak.

**Historical Importance:** The statue demonstrates the complex Egyptian understanding of divine nature — Sekhmet represented both destruction and protection, violence and healing. Her worship involved elaborate rituals to ensure her destructive power was directed at enemies rather than the community. Over 600 statues of Sekhmet were found in the temple of Mut at Karnak.

**Discovery Story:** This statue was among the numerous divine images discovered in the Karnak temple complex. The statue's severe expression and powerful form made it one of the most impressive examples of New Kingdom goddess statuary. The statue was likely part of a processional group or a stationary cult image within the temple.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Sekhmet's name appears in over 300 spells in the Coffin Texts and Book of the Dead
- The "Festival of Drunkenness" involved ritual consumption of beer mixed with pomegranate
- Sekhmet was associated with the destructive solar eye and the goddess Hathor
- Many Sekhmet statues show her holding a wand of power (sekhem-scepter)

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian deities embodied complex concepts — Sekhmet represented the dangerous aspects of divine power that required careful management through ritual. The annual pacification festival demonstrates how the Egyptians understood the need to maintain divine balance, appeasing destructive forces through proper worship.

**References:**
- Pinch, G. "Votive Offerings to Hathor." Griffith Institute, 1993.
- Roberson, J. "Sekhmet." In UCLA Encyclopedia of Egyptology, 2012.
- Egyptian Museum, Cairo. Official Collection Catalogue.

### 009. Bes Guardian Figure

**Official Name:** Bes Figure

**Original Name:** 𓃠 (bs — name of the deity)

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1550–1292 BCE

**Current Museum:** Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (Museum Number: 26.7.1404)

**Discovery Site:** Thebes (modern Luxor)

**Category:** Cult Statue — Protective Deity

**Material:** Faience, polychrome glaze

**Dimensions:** Height: 14.6 cm; Width: 8.9 cm

**Historical Description:** This faience figure depicts Bes, the dwarf guardian deity, in his characteristic form: a short, bandy-legged figure with a lion-like mane, protruding tongue, and large ears. His face shows the fierce yet comical expression that distinguished Bes from other Egyptian deities. He wears a leopard skin draped over his back and a tail that hangs between his legs. The blue-green faience glaze is largely intact.

**Religious Context:** Bes was unique among Egyptian deities — rather than appearing in temple cult, he was a domestic protector, particularly associated with childbirth and infancy. His fierce appearance was believed to scare off evil spirits and protect households. Bes amulets and figures were placed in bedrooms and around birthing areas.

**Historical Importance:** Bes figures were among the most common protective objects in Egyptian households, demonstrating how religious practice extended beyond temples into daily life. The figure's unusual appearance (unlike typical Egyptian idealized deity forms) suggests Bes may have originated from outside the Egyptian cultural sphere, possibly from Nubia or the eastern desert.

**Discovery Story:** Bes figures were found throughout Egypt, particularly in domestic contexts and in the tombs of New Kingdom nobles. This figure likely came from Thebes, where numerous examples of domestic religious objects have been recovered from houses and tombs in the workers' village and surrounding areas.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Bes was the only Egyptian god shown frontally rather than in profile
- He was associated with music and dancing, often depicted playing a lyre
- Bes protected the sun god Ra on his nightly journey through the underworld
- The goddess Taweret and the god Aha were sometimes shown with Bes-like features

**Educational Summary:** Egyptian religion was not confined to temples — domestic worship of protective deities like Bes was equally important for everyday life. The placement of Bes figures in homes, particularly near beds and birthing areas, demonstrates the integration of religious protection into daily existence.

### 010. Hathor Cow Goddess Head

**Official Name:** Hathor Head Relief

**Original Name:** 𓎡𓂝𓉔𓃝 (ḥwt-ḥr — "Mansion of Horus")

**Civilization:** Ancient Egypt

**Historical Period:** New Kingdom, 18th Dynasty

**Approximate Date:** circa 1400–1350 BCE

**Current Museum:** Ägyptisches Museum, Berlin (Museum Number: 15700)

**Discovery Site:** Deir el-Bahri (Temple of Hatshepsut), Thebes

**Category:** Cult Relief — Goddess Representation

**Material:** Limestone, polychrome paint

**Dimensions:** Height: 52 cm; Width: 38 cm

**Historical Description:** This carved limestone head depicts Hathor in her cow manifestation, with characteristic cow ears and a elaborate wig adorned with a vulture cap and uraeus. The face retains traces of original red, blue, and gold paint. The serene expression represents the nurturing aspect of the goddess. This type of relief would have appeared on temple walls, particularly in areas associated with the goddess's cult.

**Religious Context:** Hathor was one of the most important goddesses in the Egyptian pantheon, associated with love, music, joy, fertility, and the sky. Her cult centers at Dendera, Edfu, and the Hathor chapel at Deir el-Bahri attracted pilgrims seeking the goddess's blessings for fertility, safe childbirth, and musical abilities.

**Historical Importance:** The temple of Hatshepsut at Deir el-Bahri contained a dedicated Hathor chapel, reflecting the queen's particular devotion to this goddess. Reliefs and statues of Hathor were important elements of temple decoration, and the goddess's iconography was incorporated into royal monuments.

**Discovery Story:** The Deir el-Bahri temple was excavated by the Egyptian Antiquities Service beginning in the 1880s, with significant work by the Egypt Exploration Fund in the 1920s. The temple's location on the west bank of Thebes, in the necropolis area, made it accessible for study and documentation.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Hathor was worshipped from the Old Kingdom through the Ptolemaic period (over 2,500 years)
