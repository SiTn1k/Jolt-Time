## Collection Philosophy

The Daily Life & Society collection illuminates the ordinary realities of ancient Greek existence — the pottery that held wine at symposiums, the loom weights that produced household textiles, the toys that entertained children, and the musical instruments that accompanied daily routines. This collection reveals that ancient Greeks were not merely philosophers and warriors but farmers, craftspeople, merchants, parents, and neighbors whose daily concerns echo across millennia.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on domestic contexts from Athenian houses, Corinthian workshop sites, and Hellenistic domestic assemblages that preserve the material evidence of everyday Greek existence.

### 001. Dipylon Geometric Amphora

**Official Name:** Large Geometric Amphora from the Dipylon Cemetery

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Ἀμφορεύς — *Amphoreus*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 740–730 BCE

**Current Museum:** Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (Museum Number: 34.11.1)

**Discovery Site:** Dipylon Cemetery, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Storage Vessel — Funerary Amphora

**Material:** Terracotta with geometric painted decoration

**Dimensions:** Height: 155 cm (5 feet 1 inch); Diameter: 60 cm

**Historical Description:** This monumental funerary amphora, one of the largest surviving Greek vases, stood over a burial at the Dipylon cemetery. The vessel bears the characteristic Geometric style decoration: horizontal bands of meanders, triglyphs, and figured scenes depicting mourning women around a prostrate body. Such amphorae marked wealthy graves and demonstrated the deceased's status. The vessel's extraordinary size indicates it was made for funerary display rather than practical use.

**Historical Importance:** The Dipylon amphora represents the Geometric period's monumental art, marking the transition from prehistoric to historical Greek culture. The painted funerary scenes provide our earliest substantial figured representations of Greek social practices.

**Discovery Story:** Excavated from the Dipylon cemetery in the 1880s during systematic excavations of Athens' principal burial ground.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Dipylon Master was the primary painter of these large funerary amphorae
- Similar amphorae marked graves of both men and women
- The geometric decoration influenced later Greek art
- Only wealthy families could afford such monumental grave markers
- Some Dipylon amphorae were hollow to allow libations to reach the burial

**Educational Summary:** The Dipylon amphora introduces students to Greek funerary practices and Geometric art. The monumental scale reveals how Greeks used art to communicate social status.

**References:**
- Boardman, J. "Greek Art." London: Thames and Hudson, 2015.
- Coldstream, J.N. "Geometric Greece." London: Methuen, 1977.

### 002. Panathenaic Prize Amphora

**Official Name:** Panathenaic Amphora Awarded as Athletic Prize

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Παναθηναϊκὸς ἀμφορεύς — *Panathēnaikos amphoreus*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 530 BCE

**Current Museum:** Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (Museum Number: 14.130.12)

**Discovery Site:** Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Prize Vessel — Athletic Award

**Material:** Terracotta with black and red figure decoration

**Dimensions:** Height: 65.5 cm; Diameter: 30 cm

**Historical Description:** This Panathenaic amphora was awarded as a prize in the Panathenaic Games, held every four years in Athens. The amphora contains olive oil from Athena's sacred olives, won by athletic victors. The front depicts Athena Promachos (warrior) between two columns of the temple facade, while the reverse shows the athletic event — here, the pentathlon or chariot race. The distinctive black-glaze style with applied red figures identifies Panathenaic prize vessels.

**Historical Importance:** Panathenaic amphorae demonstrate how athletics and religion intertwined in Greek society. The state sponsored athletic competition as religious festival, and prizes reinforced civic pride and religious devotion.

**Discovery Story:** Found in contexts suggesting burial in a private house or sanctuary; victors often dedicated their prizes in family shrines.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Panathenaic Games preceded the Olympian Games by one year
- Victorious chariot owners received the oil even if they did not drive
- Over 1,000 Panathenaic amphorae survive
- The oil had sacred as well as practical value
- Panathenaic amphorae were specifically produced for the games

**Educational Summary:** Panathenaic amphorae reveal the fusion of athletics, religion, and civic identity in Athens. Students can analyze how athletic competition reinforced religious observance.

### 003. Red-Figure Kylix (Drinking Cup)

**Official Name:** Red-Figure Kylix Depicting a Symposium Scene

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Κύλιξ — *Kylix*, "cup")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 490–480 BCE

**Current Museum:** Antikensammlung, Berlin (Museum Number: F 2278)

**Discovery Site:** Etruscan tomb, Italy (found in trade, originally Athenian)

**Category:** Drinking Vessel — Symposium Cup

**Material:** Terracotta with red-figure painting

**Dimensions:** Height: 10.5 cm; Diameter: 32.5 cm

**Historical Description:** This kylix (wide, shallow drinking cup) depicts a symposium scene on its interior tondo: a reclining reveler with a kantharos (wine cup) and a female aulos player. The red-figure technique (figures in the natural terracotta color against black backgrounds) allowed greater detail than earlier black-figure painting. The kylix's broad interior bowl displayed elaborate compositions visible to symposium participants.

**Historical Importance:** The symposium was central to Greek elite male social life, where political alliances formed, cultural values transmitted, and masculine identity constructed. The decorated kylix transformed practical drinking into aesthetic and social experience.

**Discovery Story:** Found in an Etruscan tomb in Italy; Athenian pottery was widely traded throughout the Mediterranean, especially to Etruria.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Kylix means "cup" in Greek
- The interior tondo (circular panel) often showed mythological scenes
- Symposiasts reclined on couches, drinking wine mixed with water
- The kylix's shape prevented spilling while reclining
- Kylikes were the most common Greek drinking cup

**Educational Summary:** Symposium cups reveal Greek elite social practices. Students can analyze how drinking vessels served social and cultural functions beyond mere utility.

**References:**

### 015. Bronze Loom Weights

**Official Name:** Group of Lead Loom Weights from a Domestic Weaving Context

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Ἀγνῦθες — *Agnythes*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–300 BCE

**Current Museum:** Agora Museum, Athens (Museum Number: L 42-57)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Textile Tool — Loom Equipment

**Material:** Lead

**Dimensions:** Height: 3.5–4.5 cm; Weight: 80–120 grams each

**Historical Description:** These pyramidal loom weights held warp threads taut on the vertical Greek loom.
