## Collection Philosophy

The Democracy, Philosophy & Writing collection represents the extraordinary intellectual achievements of Classical Greece — the civilization that gave birth to democracy, systematic philosophy, formal education, and revolutionary approaches to literature and law. This collection illuminates how Greek thinkers fundamentally transformed human understanding of governance, ethics, knowledge, and artistic expression.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on inscribed stelae from the Athenian Agora, philosophical portraiture from Roman copies of Greek originals, writing equipment from Greek educational contexts, and literary papyri preserved in the dry conditions of Egypt.

### 001. Ostrakon of Themistocles

**Official Name:** Ostrakon (Pottery Sherd) with Name of Themistocles

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Ὀστρακον — *Ostrakon*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 471–470 BCE

**Current Museum:** Agora Museum, Athens (Museum Number: A-P 1281)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Voting Artifact — Ostracism Sherd

**Material:** Terracotta ( Attic Black-Figure or Red-Figure pottery fragment)

**Dimensions:** Height: 12.5 cm; Width: 9.2 cm; Thickness: 1.1 cm

**Historical Description:** This fragmentary ostrakon bears the incised name of Themistocles, son of Neocles, written in Attic script of the early fifth century BCE. Ostracism was an Athenian democratic procedure used to exile potential tyrants for ten years without formal charges. Citizens wrote the name of the person they wished to exile on pottery sherds (ostraká). If more than 6,000 votes were cast, the individual with the most votes was required to leave Athens. Themistocles was ostracized around 471 BCE, likely due to political rivalry following his role in the Persian Wars.

**Historical Importance:** This ostrakon provides material evidence for the functioning of Athenian ostracism — one of the most remarkable democratic institutions of the ancient world. The procedure assumed that any citizen, no matter how accomplished, might become a tyranny threat. Themistocles, hero of Salamis, was exiled not for any crime but for the suspicion of his potential intentions.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered during systematic excavations of the Athenian Agora by the American School of Classical Studies at Athens between 1931 and 1960s. The Agora excavation yielded thousands of ostraka, forming the world's largest collection of these democratic voting artifacts.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Over 10,000 ostraka have been found in the Athenian Agora
- The procedure required exactly 6,000 votes minimum — not a simple majority
- Both Themistocles and his rival Aristides were ostracized
- The ostraka of Themistocles cluster around the area where public assemblies gathered
- Writing implements were sometimes provided so illiterate citizens could participate
- Some ostraka were pre-written by political supporters, suggesting early vote-buying

**Educational Summary:** Ostracism demonstrates Athenian democracy's sophisticated approach to preventing tyranny. Students can analyze how democratic institutions sometimes conflicted with military necessity, as when Athens exiled its greatest naval commander during the Persian Wars.

**References:**
- Lang, M. "The Ostraka." Princeton: American School of Classical Studies at Athens, 1990.
- Forsdyke, S. "Exile, Ostracism, and Democracy." Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2005.
- Agora Excavations, American School of Classical Studies at Athens.

### 002. Ostrakon of Megacles son of Hippocrates

**Official Name:** Ostrakon Naming Megacles, Relative of Cleisthenes

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Μεγακλῆς Ἱπποκράτους — *Megaklēs Hippokrátous*)

**Approximate Date:** circa 487–486 BCE

**Discovery Site:** Kerameikos, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Voting Artifact — Ostracism Sherd

**Material:** Terracotta (roof tile fragment)

**Dimensions:** Height: 14.8 cm; Width: 11.2 cm; Thickness: 1.8 cm

**Historical Description:** This large roof tile fragment bears the incised name of Megacles, son of Hippocrates, a prominent Alcmaeonid aristocrat and relative of the reformer Cleisthenes. The Alcmaeonids were a powerful Athenian family who had supported the establishment of democracy. Megacles' ostracism around 487/486 BCE demonstrates that democracy's new leaders were not exempt from the procedures they had created — the very system that gave them power could be used against them.

**Historical Importance:** Megacles' ostracism illustrates how democratic procedures could challenge even reformist aristocrats. His exile shows that Cleisthenes' democratic system was not merely a tool for factional advantage but a genuine political institution with its own logic.

**Discovery Story:** Found during German Archaeological Institute excavations at Kerameikos, the ancient pottery and ceramics district of Athens, where many ostraka were discarded after voting procedures concluded.

- His father Hippocrates was also a public figure during reforms
- The Alcmaeonid family had previously been exiled under Peisistratus
- Megacles' ostracism was part of a broader pattern of limiting aristocratic influence
- Similar roof tiles were commonly used for ostraka due to their availability

**Educational Summary:** Megacles' ostracism teaches students about the impartiality of democratic institutions. Even those who created democratic systems were subject to their operation.

### 003. Bronze Kleroterion Fragment

**Official Name:** Bronze Fragment from Kleroterion Selection Machine

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Κληρωτήριον — *Klērōtērion*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–350 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: BΘ 95)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Democratic Instrument — Random Selection Device

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Length: 8.5 cm; Width: 4.2 cm; Thickness: 0.8 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze fragment preserves part of the rotating mechanism from an Athenian kleroterion — a device used to randomly select citizens for public offices and juries. The kleroterion operated on the principle of lot (random selection): citizens' name tiles were placed in a rotating tube, and a slider mechanism selected individuals based on a random geometric pattern. Random selection was considered essential to democracy — the principle that all qualified citizens had an equal chance of serving the state.

**Historical Importance:** The kleroterion represents ancient democracy's sophisticated mechanical approach to fair selection. The device ensured that office-holding could not be bought or rigged, as the geometric random selection was unpredictable. This mechanical democracy stands in stark contrast to modern representative systems.

**Discovery Story:** Recovered from the Athenian Agora excavations. Multiple kleroterion fragments have been found, allowing reconstruction of the device's operation.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Kleroterion selected citizens for the famous dikasteria (jury courts)
- The largest juries could include 6,000 jurors selected daily
- Selection took place at dawn each day before courts convened
- Name tiles were organized by tribe, ensuring proportional representation
- Bronze sliders were critical to the random selection mechanism

**Educational Summary:** The kleroterion demonstrates how Athenians embodied democratic principles in physical technology. Students can analyze how random selection prevents corruption and ensures equal civic opportunity.

**References:**
- Crosby, M. "The Kleroterion." Hesperia Supplement 8 (1949): 43-47.
- Ober, J. "Mass and Elite in Democratic Athens." Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1989.

### 004. Bronze Pinakion (Juror Token)

**Official Name:** Bronze Juror Identification Pinakion

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Πινάκιον — *Pinakion*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–300 BCE

**Current Museum:** Agora Museum, Athens (Museum Number: B 170)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Democratic Instrument — Identity Token

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Height: 9.2 cm; Width: 3.8 cm; Thickness: 0.3 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze pinakion is a thin rectangular identification token issued to Athenian citizens who had been selected for jury service through the kleroterion. The pinakion displays the citizen's name, father's name, and deme (village) of registration, along with a symbol identifying his tribe. Jurors presented these tokens at the court entrance each morning, where officials verified eligibility and assigned jurors to specific courtrooms.

**Historical Importance:** The pinakion represents the democratic emphasis on civic identity and accountability. Unlike modern anonymous jury duty, Athenian jurors were publicly identified and accountable for their verdicts. The token also demonstrates sophisticated state administration, as Athens maintained registries of all eligible citizens.

**Discovery Story:** Numerous pinakia have been recovered from the Athenian Agora, where they were likely discarded when their owners died or lost citizenship. The tokens' ubiquity makes them important evidence for Athenian civic administration.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Citizens who served received a small daily payment (obol)
- The payment system was introduced by Pericles
- Pinakia were required for all jury service
- Loss of pinakion required reporting to officials
- Similar tokens were used for other civic duties

**Educational Summary:** Pinakia illustrate how democratic participation required formal identification. Students can compare Athenian civic registration with modern voter registration systems.

**References:**
- Lang, M. "Pinakia." Hesperia 66 (1997): 253-263.
- Rhodes, P.J. "The Athenian Revolution." Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1993.

### 005. Bronze Prytany Token

**Official Name:** Bronze Token of the Prytany (Executive Committee)

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Πρυτανεῖον — *Prytaneion*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 350–300 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: B 212)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Democratic Instrument — Civic Token

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Diameter: 4.1 cm; Thickness: 0.4 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze token belonged to a prytanis — a member of the rotating executive committee that managed Athenian daily government. Athens was divided into ten tribes, each providing fifty members (prytanes) who served on the executive council (Boule) for one-tenth of the year. Prytanes dined together in the Tholos building, where they organized by lot the business of the Assembly. This token identified the bearer's status during his prytany service.

**Historical Importance:** The prytany system distributed executive power among all citizens, preventing any individual or faction from dominating governance. Daily rotation of the presiding officer (epistates) among prytanes ensured that even this ceremonial role could not be monopolized.

**Discovery Story:** Found in the Athenian Agora, near the Tholos building where prytanes conducted their duties. Bronze tokens were discarded when their owners' prytany service concluded.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Ten tribes served as prytanes in rotation throughout the year
- Prytanes met in the Tholos, a circular building in the Agora
- They organized the Assembly's agenda and presided over debates
- One epistates (leader) was selected by lot each day
- Foreign ambassadors were entertained at prytany expense

**Educational Summary:** The prytany token demonstrates how Athens distributed executive power through rotation and lot. Students can analyze how this system prevented the accumulation of power.

**References:**
- Rhodes, P.J. "The Athenian Boule." Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1972.
- Hansen, M.H. "The Athenian Democracy in the Age of Demosthenes." Oxford: Blackwell, 1991.

### 006. Bronze Sphaira (Lot Machine Ball)

**Official Name:** Bronze Ball from Kleroterion Lot Machine

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Σφαῖρα — *Sphaira*, "ball")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–300 BCE

**Current Museum:** Agora Museum, Athens (Museum Number: B 44)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Democratic Instrument — Lot Machine Component

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Diameter: 1.8 cm

**Historical Description:** This small bronze sphere is one of many balls used in the kleroterion random selection device. The machine contained a series of parallel tubes holding citizen name tiles, with a geometric device (pinax) that selected individuals by lot. Bronze balls of various sizes were used both in the selection mechanism and as counter-marks. The use of lot (klerosis) was fundamental to Athenian democracy, as it was considered the fairest method of selection.

**Historical Importance:** The sphaira exemplifies the democratic principle of isonomia — equality before the law. Selection by lot meant that social status, wealth, and eloquence did not determine office-holding. Every qualified citizen had an equal mathematical chance of selection.

**Discovery Story:** Hundreds of bronze balls have been found throughout the Athenian Agora, where the kleroterion was located and operated.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Various sized balls may have indicated different selection categories
- The word "klerosis" (lot) gave us "clergy" (chosen by lot in medieval church)
- Similar lottery systems were used throughout the Greek world
- Modern random selection algorithms trace lineage to these devices
- The Greeks considered selection by lot divinely sanctioned

**Educational Summary:** Bronze lot balls illustrate the democratic principle that governance should not be bought or inherited. Students can trace how random selection principles influenced later democratic theory.

**References:**
- Crosby, M. "The Kleroterion." Hesperia Supplement 8 (1949): 43-47.
- Carter, L.B. "The Quiet Athenian." Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1986.

### 007. Bronze Voting Tablet (Pseudomenos)

**Official Name:** Bronze Voting Tablet with Letter Markings

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Ψῆφος — *Psēphos*, "voting pebble")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–322 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: B 328)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Agora, Athens, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Democratic Instrument — Voting Device

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Height: 3.2 cm; Width: 2.1 cm; Thickness: 0.4 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze tablet bears Greek letters used in the Phaenippos procedure for fining or absolving accused individuals. Athenian courts used letter-marked bronze tablets for specific voting procedures. Citizens inserted their tablet into a bronzen gate device (pteron) that recorded their vote privately. Bronze was preferred over pottery for permanent voting records that might be consulted later.

**Historical Importance:** The bronze voting tablet represents Athenian democratic secrecy in voting. Unlike ostraka, which were cast publicly, certain procedures required secret voting to prevent intimidation. The bronze tablets allowed citizens to vote their true conscience without fear of retribution.

**Discovery Story:** Found in contexts suggesting deposition in a dedicated voting deposit (perirrhantērion) after use. Athens maintained sacred deposits of broken voting equipment, similar to other Greek practices of dedicating used civic equipment.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Secret voting prevented aristocratic intimidation of poor citizens
- The pteron (gate) device was made of bronze
- After voting, tablets were checked for proper marking
- Deposits have been found near the Tholos and other civic buildings
- Similar voting procedures existed in many Greek cities

**Educational Summary:** Bronze voting tablets demonstrate Athenian concern for voting privacy. Students can compare Greek and modern secret ballot systems.

**References:**
- Pritchett, W.K. "The Greek State at War." Berkeley: University of California Press, 1974.
- Boegehold, A. "The Vessel for the Vote." American Journal of Archaeology 98 (1994): 425-432.

### 008. Bronze Ostrakon with Name of Hyperbolus

**Official Name:** Bronze Ostrakon for Hyperbolus the Alchemist

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Ὑπέρβολος — *Hyperbolos*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 417 BCE

**Current Museum:** Agora Museum, Athens (Museum Number: A-P 2347)

**Category:** Voting Artifact — Bronze Ostrakon

**Material:** Bronze (unusual material for ostraka)

**Dimensions:** Height: 7.8 cm; Width: 5.9 cm; Thickness: 0.6 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze ostrakon bears the incised name of Hyperbolus, an Athenian politician ostracized in 417 BCE. Bronze was rarely used for ostraka, making this example unusual. Hyperbolus was the last person ostracized in Athens — after his exile, the procedure fell into disuse, possibly because it had become too obviously a weapon of political rivalry rather than tyranny prevention. The unusual material may indicate this ostrakon's use in a special or ceremonial context.

**Historical Importance:** Hyperbolus' ostracism marks the end of a democratic institution that had operated for nearly a century. The procedure's obsolescence suggests how democratic institutions can be corrupted through misuse.

- Hyperbolus was the LAST person ever ostracized in Athens
- After 417 BCE, the procedure was considered beneath use
- His exile was probably engineered by Nicias and Alcibiades working together
- He was assassinated in Samos in 411 BCE during the oligarchic coup
- Bronze ostraka are extremely rare

**Educational Summary:** Hyperbolus' ostrakon teaches students about institutional decline. Democratic procedures can be undermined by the very citizens they were designed to control.

### 010. Bronze Probouleusis Tablet

**Official Name:** Bronze Tablet Recording Probouleusis (Prior Council Review)

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Προβούλευσις — *Probouleusis*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Athenian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 360–340 BCE

The Boule screened all legislation, ensuring that proposals were legally proper and practically feasible before full citizen debate. ### 013. Gortyn Law Code Fragment

**Official Name:** Inscribed Stone Stele of the Gortyn Law Code

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Νόμοι Γόρτυνος — *Nomoi Gortynos*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Cretan (Gortyn)

**Historical Period:** Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 650–450 BCE (with later additions)

**Current Museum:** Archaeological Museum of Heraklion, Crete (Museum Number: HER.H.163)

**Discovery Site:** Gortyn (Gortyna), Crete, Greece

**Category:** Legal Inscription — Law Code

**Material:** Limestone

**Dimensions:** Height: 85 cm; Width: 55 cm; Thickness: 25 cm

**Historical Description:** This fragment preserves part of the famous Gortyn Law Code — the most extensive surviving body of early Greek law. Inscribed in the Dorian Greek dialect on the walls of the city's prytaneion (town hall), the code regulated family law, property, inheritance, and criminal offenses. The laws are notable for their combination of harsh penalties with sophisticated procedural protections.

**Historical Importance:** The Gortyn Law Code provides our most complete picture of Archaic Greek law. Its provisions reveal a society balancing tradition with reform, protecting women's property rights while maintaining patriarchal structures. The code influenced later Greek legal thinking.
