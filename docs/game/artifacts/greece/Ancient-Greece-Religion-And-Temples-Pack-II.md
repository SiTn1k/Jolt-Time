## Collection Philosophy

The Religion & Temples collection explores the spiritual dimension of ancient Greek civilization — from the mysterious religious practices of Minoan Crete to the magnificent temple complexes of the Classical period. Ancient Greek religion permeated every aspect of daily life, with gods and goddesses intervening in human affairs, demanding offerings, and rewarding the pious with prosperity and victory.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on objects from the great Pan-Hellenic sanctuaries of Delphi, Olympia, and Athens, as well as local cult sites throughout the Greek world.

### 001. Parthenon Metope — Lapith and Centaur

**Official Name:** Parthenon Metope from the South Side, Athens

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *metopē* — "between the holes")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Classical Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** High Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 447–438 BCE

**Current Museum:** British Museum, London (Museum Number: SC 645); Parthenon sculptures in the Acropolis Museum, Athens (most fragments)

**Discovery Site:** Parthenon, Athenian Acropolis, Greece

**Category:** Temple Sculpture — Metope

**Material:** Pentelic marble

**Dimensions:** Panel height: 115 cm (45.3 in); Width: 115 cm (45.3 in)

**Historical Description:** This marble metope depicts the battle between a Lapith warrior and a centaur — one of the most famous sculptural groups from the Parthenon. The scene, part of the metopes running around the entire temple, represents the cosmic struggle between civilization (Lapiths) and barbarism (Centaurs). The battle originated in the myth of the Centauromachy at the wedding of Peirithous and Hippodameia, symbolizing Greek victory over primitive forces. The metope's dynamic composition and anatomically detailed figures represent the High Classical style at its peak, carved under the supervision of Phidias for the Parthenon's original dedication.

**Historical Importance:** The Parthenon metopes represent the finest example of High Classical temple sculpture, demonstrating the Athenian understanding of human anatomy, movement, and dramatic narrative. The Centauromachy theme, recurring throughout Greek art, expressed fundamental Greek values of civilization overcoming chaos. These sculptures influenced artistic conventions for over two millennia, inspiring Renaissance and Neoclassical artists.

**Discovery Story:** Most Parthenon sculptures were removed by Lord Elgin in 1801-1803 under a controversial Ottoman permit. The sculptures were sold to the British Museum, where they remain on display despite Greek requests for return. Other fragments remain in Athens, Istanbul, and other collections.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Parthenon had 92 metopes depicting four mythological battles
- The south side showed the Centauromachy, possibly referring to the Persian Wars
- The Elgin Marbles remain one of the most disputed artifacts in museum collections
- Phidias supervised the sculptural program while his assistants executed much of the work
- The sculptures were originally painted with bright colors, now lost

**Educational Summary:** Parthenon metopes demonstrate how Greek temples used mythological narratives to express civic values. The battle between civilization and chaos reflected Athens' self-image after the Persian Wars, transforming the Parthenon into a monument of Panhellenic Greek achievement.

**References:**
- Boardman, J. "The Parthenon and Its Sculptures." Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1985.
- Hurwit, J.M. "The Athenian Acropolis." Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1999.
- Jenkins, I. "The Parthenon Sculptures." London: British Museum Press, 2006.

### 002. Temple of Aphaia — Aegina Pediment

**Official Name:** Pediment Sculptures from the Temple of Aphaia, Aegina

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Aphaia* — goddess of the island)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic/Early Classical Period (Aeginetan)

**Historical Period:** Late Archaic to Early Classical transition

**Approximate Date:** circa 490–480 BCE

**Current Museum:** Glyptothek, Munich (Museum Numbers: 70-73); National Archaeological Museum, Athens (fragments)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Aphaia, Aegina Island, Greece

**Category:** Temple Sculpture — Pediment

**Material:** Parian marble

**Dimensions:** Pediment width: approximately 15 meters (49 ft)

**Historical Description:** The pediments of the Temple of Aphaia depict two battles separated by Athena and two warriors. The eastern pediment shows a battle between Greeks and Trojans, possibly referencing the Trojan War myth. The western pediment depicts a battle between Greeks and Persians — a direct reference to the recent Persian Wars (490 BCE). The sculptor's style shows the transition from Archaic to Early Classical, with figures beginning to move naturally while retaining some Archaic stiffness. The temple's position overlooking the Saronic Gulf made it a strategic observation point during the Persian invasions.

**Historical Importance:** The Aegina pediments represent a crucial moment in Greek sculptural development, showing the transition from Archaic to Classical style. The temple's dedication to Aphaia (a local deity associated with Artemis and Athena) and its decoration commemorating Persian War victories demonstrate how local cults incorporated Panhellenic themes. The sculptures influenced later pedimental compositions at the Temple of Zeus at Olympia and the Parthenon.

**Discovery Story:** The temple was excavated by German archaeologists in 1901, revealing the sculptural fragments now in Munich and Athens. The sculptures' quality established Aegina as an important artistic center in the Archaic and Early Classical periods.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Aegina was a rival of Athens before Persian Wars
- The temple's position suggests it served as a navigational landmark
- Different sculptural styles in the two pediments suggest multiple sculptors
- The Persian War reference in the west pediment is unique for Archaic temples
- Aegina's fall to Athens in 457 BCE ended its artistic prominence

**Educational Summary:** The Aegina pediments demonstrate how Greek temples evolved artistically and politically during the Persian Wars era. Students can analyze how artists balanced traditional Archaic conventions with new Classical naturalism.

**References:**
- Boardman, J. "Greek Sculpture: The Archaic Period." London: Thames and Hudson, 1978.
- Ohly, D. "Die Tempel der Aphaia." Munich: Biermann, 1985.

### 003. Temple of Zeus at Olympia — Metope Fragment

**Official Name:** Metope from the Temple of Zeus, Olympia

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *labōn* — "to take/receive")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic Period (Elean)

**Historical Period:** Early Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 470–460 BCE

**Current Museum:** Archaeological Museum of Olympia, Greece (Museum Number: Olympia 38)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Zeus, Olympia, Greece

**Category:** Temple Sculpture — Metope

**Material:** Local Olympia limestone (concrete-like stone)

**Dimensions:** Height: 163 cm (64.2 in); Width: 95 cm (37.4 in)

**Historical Description:** This metope fragment depicts one of the Twelve Labors of Heracles, likely the Cleansing of the Augean Stables or the Capture of the Cretan Bull. The high relief style, with figures projecting dramatically from the background, demonstrates the developed Archaic sculptural technique. The metopes of the Temple of Zeus at Olympia originally decorated the temple's entablature, each depicting a labor of Heracles. The hero's exploits, representing human effort achieving divine tasks, perfectly suited the sanctuary of Zeus — king of the gods who granted Heracles immortality.

**Historical Importance:** The Olympia metopes represent one of the earliest extensive sculptural programs depicting Heracles, establishing iconographic conventions that persisted throughout Greek art. Heracles' labors, as recorded in literary sources, included twelve impossible tasks that the hero accomplished through strength and cleverness. The temple's decoration proclaimed Zeus's power and the possibility of mortals achieving divine status through heroic virtue.

**Discovery Story:** The Temple of Zeus at Olympia was excavated by German archaeologists in the 1870s-1880s, revealing the temple's sculptural program including metopes, pediments, and the famous chryselephantine statue of Zeus. The metope fragments, though damaged, preserve significant portions of the Heracles narrative.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Temple of Zeus at Olympia was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World
- The metopes depicted eleven identifiable labors; the twelfth remains debated
- Pausanias described the temple decoration during his visit in the 2nd century CE
- The Olympic Games were held in Zeus's honor every four years
- The temple's completion coincided with the Persian Wars

**Educational Summary:** Olympia metopes demonstrate how temple decoration communicated religious narratives to worshippers. Heracles' trials, as a model of human effort achieving divine approval, reinforced the sanctuary's message about the relationship between mortals and Zeus.

**References:**
- Mallwitz, A. "Olympia und seine Sportler." Frankfurt: Societäts-Verlag, 1986.
- Herodotus. "Histories." Complete works.
- Pausanias. "Description of Greece." Book 5, Chapter 10-12.

### 004. Temple of Artemis at Ephesus — Column Fragment

**Official Name:** Column Fragment from the Temple of Artemis, Ephesus

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Artemis*; Lydian: *Artemisios*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic Period (Ephesian)

**Historical Period:** Late Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 560–546 BCE (earlier temple); rebuilt 4th century BCE

**Current Museum:** British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 1106); Ephesus Archaeological Museum, Turkey

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Artemis, Ephesus (modern Selçuk, Turkey)

**Category:** Temple Architecture — Column

**Material:** Marble (various types including dokimeion)

**Dimensions:** Column height: approximately 18 meters (59 ft) for full columns; Fragment: 120 cm (47.2 in)

**Historical Description:** This fragment comes from one of the 127 columns that surrounded the great Temple of Artemis at Ephesus — one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The column fragment preserves carved decorative bands with reliefs depicting figures, animals, and mythological scenes. The temple, rebuilt in the 4th century BCE after the original was burned by Herostratus in 356 BCE, represented the pinnacle of Ionic architecture. The famous story that Alexander the Great offered to rebuild the temple but was refused by the Ephesians demonstrates the sanctuary's pride in its自主权.

**Historical Importance:** The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus was one of the largest temples in the ancient world, influencing architectural developments throughout the Mediterranean. The temple's wealth, expressed in precious materials and elaborate decoration, attracted visitors and pilgrims from across the Greek world. Acts in the New Testament records that Paul's missionary work in Ephesus so threatened the Artemis cult that silversmiths who made Artemis statues rioted (Acts 19:23-41).

**Discovery Story:** British archaeologist J.T. Wood excavated Ephesus from 1863-1874, finally locating the temple beneath the Christian church built over it. The excavations recovered thousands of column and sculptural fragments, now distributed among museums in London, Istanbul, and Ephesus.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The original Archaic temple was burned by Herostratus in 356 BCE, the same night Alexander the Great was born
- Pliny the Elder recorded the temple dimensions: 115 meters long, 55 meters wide
- The temple's foundation was built on marshy ground to protect against earthquakes
- Pliny recorded that 127 columns surrounded the temple, 36 of which were carved
- The rebuilding after the fire was financed by Croesus of Lydia

**Educational Summary:** Ephesus column fragments demonstrate how Panhellenic religious sites attracted international contributions and architectural innovation. The temple's destruction and rebuilding illustrate how religious institutions could survive catastrophe through community support.

**References:**
- Wood, J.T. "Discoveries at Ephesus." London: Longmans, 1877.
- Akurgal, E. "The Art of Ephesus." Istanbul: Net Turistik, 1990.

### 005. Temple of Hephaestus — Doric Capital

**Official Name:** Doric Capital from the Temple of Hephaestus, Athens

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Hēphaistos* — god of craftsmen)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Classical Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** High Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 449–415 BCE

**Current Museum:** In situ at the Hephaesteion, Athens; National Archaeological Museum, Athens (replicas)

**Discovery Site:** Hephaesteion (Temple of Hephaestus), Agora of Athens, Greece

**Category:** Temple Architecture — Capital

**Material:** Pentelic marble

**Dimensions:** Capital diameter: approximately 1.5 meters (4.9 ft); Height: 0.8 meters (2.6 ft)

**Historical Description:** This Doric capital crowns one of the best-preserved Greek temples in existence. The Hephaesteion, dedicated to Hephaestus (god of craftsmen) and Athena Ergane (protector of craftsmen), stands overlooking the Agora where craftsmen and merchants conducted business. The capital's elegant proportions demonstrate the refinements of High Classical Doric architecture, with carefully calibrated curves (entasis) that counteract optical illusions of sagging. The temple's preservation allows modern scholars to understand the details of Greek temple construction.

**Historical Importance:** The Hephaesteion represents the finest surviving example of Doric temple architecture, demonstrating the proportions and decorative details that characterized Classical Greek temples. The temple's dedication to Hephaestus and Athena Ergane reflects Athens' identity as a center of craftsmanship and industry, honoring the divine patrons of Athenian craftsmen who produced the city's famous pottery, bronze work, and sculpture.

**Discovery Story:** The Hephaesteion survived largely intact because it was converted into the Church of Saint George in the medieval period. The church's modifications, though改变了 temple's original appearance, protected the structure from destruction. Archaeological restoration in the 20th century returned the temple to its Classical appearance.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The temple contains frieze sculptures depicting Theseus and the Lapiths
- The Hephaesteion is one of the few temples with surviving roof tiles
- It was dedicated to both Hephaestus and Athena Ergane (Athena worker)
- The temple overlooks the Agora, connecting religious and commercial spaces
- It was built on the site of an earlier Archaic temple

**Educational Summary:** The Hephaesteion demonstrates how temple architecture expressed civic identity. The dedication to craft gods reflected Athens' self-image as a center of skilled workmanship, linking religious worship to economic activity.

**References:**
- Camp, J.M. "The Athenian Agora." London: British School at Athens, 1986.
- Thompson, H.A. "The Temple of Hephaestus." Hesperia 46 (1977): 1-28.

### 006. Temple of Apollo at Bassae — Capital and Column

**Official Name:** Capital from the Temple of Apollo Epikourios, Bassae

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Bassai* — "the projecting part")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Classical Period (Phigalian)

**Historical Period:** High Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 430–420 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: NAMA 3014)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Apollo Epikourios, Bassae, Arcadia, Greece

**Category:** Temple Architecture — Capital

**Material:** Local grey limestone

**Dimensions:** Column height: approximately 10 meters (32.8 ft); Capital preserved fragments

**Historical Description:** The Temple of Apollo at Bassae, designed by Iktinos (architect of the Parthenon), was renowned for its innovative features including the only known example of a Greek temple using all three architectural orders. The interior combined Doric columns on the exterior with Ionic columns surrounding the cult statue and a single Corinthian column in the interior. The capital fragment demonstrates the transition from Doric to Ionic conventions, reflecting the experimental approach of High Classical architecture.

**Historical Importance:** The Bassae temple's architectural innovations influenced later Greek and Roman temple design. The interior Ionic colonnade and single Corinthian column established conventions that would define Hellenistic and Roman temple architecture. Pausanias, visiting in the 2nd century CE, considered the temple among the finest in Greece, praising its beauty despite its remote mountain location.

**Discovery Story:** The temple was excavated by French archaeologists in 1889-1895, revealing the unusual interior arrangement and the famous metopes depicting the Trojan War. The metopes, now in the National Archaeological Museum, show the transition from Archaic to Classical style.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The temple was dedicated "to Apollo the Helper" (Epikourios) for saving the area from plague
- The interior Ionic columns were unusual for a Doric peripteral temple
- The single Corinthian column was possibly symbolic of the god's presence
- The temple faces east, with the cult statue receiving morning light
- It was one of the first temples to use a continuous frieze

**Educational Summary:** The Bassae temple demonstrates how Greek architects experimented with forms and conventions. The temple's remote location and unusual dedication reveal how religious beliefs could motivate monumental construction even in difficult terrain.

**References:**
- Courby, F. "Le Temple de高二." Paris: De Boccard, 1927.
- Pausanias. "Description of Greece." Book 8, Chapter 41.

### 007. Temple Acroterion — Nike Temple

**Official Name:** Acroterion from the Temple of Athena Nike, Athens

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Nikē* — "victory"; *akrotērion* — "highest point")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Classical Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** High Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 425 BCE

**Current Museum:** Acropolis Museum, Athens (Museum Number: Acr. 2485)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Athena Nike, Athenian Acropolis, Greece

**Category:** Temple Decoration — Roof Ornament

**Material:** Pentelic marble

**Dimensions:** Preserved height: 85 cm (33.5 in); Original estimated height: 150 cm (59 in)

**Historical Description:** This acroterion originally crowned the corner of the Temple of Athena Nike's roof, serving as one of the decorative finials that marked the building's completion. Acroteria (roof ornaments) were cast in bronze or carved in marble, often depicting Nikai (Victory figures), scrolls, or floral designs. The Nike temple acroteria originally supported bronze Nike statues that would have fluttered in the wind, creating a dynamic effect visible from below. The temple, built to commemorate Athenian victory in the Persian Wars, used Victory imagery throughout.

**Historical Importance:** Acroteria represent the final decorative element of Greek temple architecture, transforming functional roofs into visual celebrations of divine power and human achievement. The Temple of Athena Nike's acroteria, visible against the sky from the Athenian Agora below, proclaimed Athens' victory and divine favor to all viewers. Such roof ornaments influenced Roman and Renaissance architecture.

**Discovery Story:** The Temple of Athena Nike was dismantled in 1687 during the Venetian siege of Athens and rebuilt in the 19th century. Fragments of the original acroteria were recovered during archaeological restoration and are now displayed in the Acropolis Museum.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Temple of Athena Nike was destroyed in 1687 when Venetian cannonballs hit a Turkish powder magazine stored inside
- The rebuilt temple was dismantled again in the 19th century for accurate restoration
- Nike acroteria often held victory crowns or trumpets
- Similar acroteria appear on temples throughout the Greek world
- The temple's position commanded views of the Salamis straits

**Educational Summary:** Acroteria demonstrate how Greek temples used every available surface for religious and political messaging. The Victory imagery on the Nike temple's roof connected the goddess Athena to Athenian military success.

**References:**
- Meritt, B.D. "The Temple of Athena Nike." Hesperia 38 (1969): 1-17.
- Hurwit, J.M. "The Athenian Acropolis." Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1999.

### 008. Antefix from the Temple of Poseidon

**Official Name:** Antefix from the Temple of Poseidon, Sounion

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *antefixum* — "fixed before")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 490–480 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: NAMA 1897)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Poseidon, Cape Sounion, Attica, Greece

**Category:** Temple Decoration — Antefix

**Material:** Terracotta with painted decoration

**Dimensions:** Height: 35 cm (13.8 in); Width: 25 cm (9.8 in)

**Historical Description:** This antefix, originally attached to the eaves of the Temple of Poseidon at Sounion, depicts a palmette and lotus flower motif — characteristic of Archaic temple decoration. Antefixes covered the ends of roof tiles, preventing water infiltration while providing decorative detail. The Sounion temple, positioned on a dramatic headland overlooking the Aegean Sea, was dedicated to Poseidon, god of the sea. Sailors approaching Athens by sea would see the temple's gleaming marble against the sky, a symbol of divine protection over maritime activities.

**Historical Importance:** Antefixes demonstrate how every element of Greek temple architecture received decorative attention. The palmette-lotus motif, borrowed from Near Eastern art and adapted by Greek craftsmen, became a standard decorative element throughout the Greek world. The Temple of Poseidon's remote position made such decorative elements especially important for visual impact.

**Discovery Story:** The Temple of Poseidon at Sounion was excavated by Greek archaeologists in the late 19th century. The temple's dramatic position overlooking the sea made it a landmark for ancient sailors and a favorite subject for modern travelers.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Lord Byron visited Sounion in 1810 and carved his name on the temple columns
- The temple was damaged by the Persian forces in 480 BCE and rebuilt
- The site offers one of the most spectacular sunset views in Greece
- Similar antefixes decorated temples at Athens, Olympia, and other sites
- The temple's position at the southern tip of Attica marked the boundary of Athenian territory

**Educational Summary:** Antefixes illustrate how Greek temples used decorative details to express religious and cultural values. The palmette-lotus motif's Near Eastern origins demonstrate cultural exchange in archaic religious art.

**References:**
- Travlos, J. "The Temple of Poseidon at Sounion." Athens: Archaeological Society, 1971.

### 009. Temple Frieze Block — Theseus and the Amazons

**Official Name:** Frieze Block from the Theseion (Temple of Theseus), Athens

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Theseion* — "temple of Theseus")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Classical Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** Early Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 460–450 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: NAMA 1854)

**Discovery Site:** Theseion (Temple of Hephaestus area), Agora of Athens, Greece

**Category:** Temple Sculpture — Frieze

**Material:** Pentelic marble

**Dimensions:** Block height: 85 cm (33.5 in); Width: 120 cm (47.2 in); Thickness: 70 cm (27.6 in)

**Historical Description:** This frieze block depicts the battle between Theseus and the Amazons — one of the great hero's legendary exploits. The Amazonomachy (battle against Amazons) was a popular theme in Greek art, symbolizing civilization's triumph over barbarism. The Theseion's frieze, running around the temple's inner colonnade, depicted Theseus' adventures including battles with Amazons, Centaurs, and other mythological opponents. The temple's identification as the Theseion (Theseus' temple) connected Athens' civic hero to the divine and human spheres of religious worship.

**Historical Importance:** The Theseion frieze established Theseus as Athens' primary civic hero, whose adventures paralleled and sometimes superseded those of Heracles. The temple's frieze program influenced later Athenian sculptural decoration, establishing narrative themes that would appear at the Parthenon and other monuments. Theseus' role in founding Athenian democracy (according to 5th-century sources) made the hero a symbol of democratic values.

**Discovery Story:** The temple, incorrectly identified as the Theseion due to its dedication to Theseus, was excavated in the 19th century. Its actual dedication to Hephaestus and Athena was established later through inscriptions.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The temple is now correctly identified as the Hephaesteion
- The Amazonomachy theme appears in art from the 7th century BCE onward
- Theseus was said to have founded the Panathenaic Games
- The temple was converted to a Christian church in the medieval period
- Its excellent preservation is due to continuous use over millennia

**Educational Summary:** Temple friezes demonstrate how Greek architects used mythological narratives to express civic values. Theseus' battles symbolized Athens' self-image as defender of civilization against barbarism.

**References:**
- Boardman, J. "Greek Sculpture: The Classical Period." London: Thames and Hudson, 1978.
- Thompson, H.A. "The Theseion." American Journal of Archaeology 48 (1944): 1-31.

### 010. Roof Tile — Temple of Athena at Priene

**Official Name:** Laconian Roof Tile from the Temple of Athena, Priene

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *Priēnē* — Ionic Greek city)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Hellenistic Period (Ionian)

**Historical Period:** Early Hellenistic

**Approximate Date:** circa 350–300 BCE

**Current Museum:** British Museum, London (Museum Number: BM 1563)

**Discovery Site:** Temple of Athena, Priene, Ionia (modern Turkey)

**Category:** Temple Architecture — Roof Tile

**Material:** Terracotta with black glaze

**Dimensions:** Length: 65 cm (25.6 in); Width: 45 cm (17.7 in); Height: 8 cm (3.1 in)

**Historical Description:** This terracotta roof tile comes from the Temple of Athena at Priene, an Ionic city-state in Asia Minor. Greek roof tiles, developed in the Archaic period, replaced earlier thatch and wood roofing, creating fire-resistant, durable coverings for temples. The tile bears stamped decoration and the inscription "Priene," identifying its civic origin. The temple, designed by the architect Pytheos (who also designed the Mausoleum at Halikarnassos), represented the refined Ionic style of the 4th century BCE.

**Historical Importance:** Roof tiles represent a crucial technological innovation in Greek temple construction, enabling larger roof spans and more complex roof geometries. The Priene tiles, stamped with civic identification, demonstrate how even functional architectural elements carried political messaging. Priene, rebuilt on a Hippodamian grid plan in the 4th century BCE, represented ideal Greek urban planning.

**Discovery Story:** Priene was excavated by German and British archaeologists in the late 19th century, revealing one of the best-preserved examples of a Greek city with temples, agora, theater, and residential areas.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Laconian tile type originated in Sparta and spread throughout the Greek world
- Tiles were heavy, requiring substantial roof structures
- The Mausoleum at Halikarnassos was designed by the same architect as the Priene temple
- Priene was dedicated to Athena Polias, the city-protecting goddess
- The temple followed Ionic architectural conventions

**Educational Summary:** Roof tiles illustrate how technological innovations enabled architectural advances. Students can analyze how functional building elements became opportunities for civic display.

**References:**
- Cook, B.F. "Greek Terracottas." London: British Museum Press, 1987.

### 011. Peplos Kore — Athenian Acropolis

**Official Name:** Marble Kore wearing a Peplos, from the Athenian Acropolis

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: *kore* — "maiden"; *peplos* — "robe")

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic Period (Athenian)

**Historical Period:** Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 530 BCE

**Current Museum:** Acropolis Museum, Athens (Museum Number: Acr. 679)

**Discovery Site:** Athenian Acropolis, Greece

**Category:** Sculpture — Cult Statue (Votive)

**Material:** Parian marble with traces of paint

**Dimensions:** Height: 118 cm (46.5 in)

**Historical Description:** This famous marble statue depicts a young woman wearing a heavy woolen peplos, an essential garment of Greek religious practice. The peplos, the traditional dress of married women, was presented to Athena during the Panathenaic festival, making this statue's garment contextually significant. The kore (plural korai) represents the canonical Archaic sculptural type of draped female figures, often dedicated in sanctuaries. **Dimensions:** Height: 90 cm (35.4 in); Wing span: 105 cm (41.3 in)

**Historical Description:** This bronze Nike statue, created by Paeonios of Mende, was dedicated at Olympia to commemorate a military victory. The goddess Nike, shown with wings in flight, descended to crown the dedicator. The statue's dynamic pose, with drapery billowing as if caught in a wind, demonstrates the Early Classical interest in movement and naturalism. Paeonios' inscription records that the statue was won from enemies in battle and dedicated by the Messenians and Naupaktians.

**Historical Importance:** The Nike of Paeonios represents the finest surviving example of Early Classical bronze sculpture, demonstrating the technical mastery required for such monuments. The dedication by a democratic community (Messenians and Naupaktians) rather than a monarch reflects the democratization of Panhellenic dedications in the 5th century BCE.

**Discovery Story:** Found during German excavations of Olympia, the statue was preserved in excellent condition.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Paeonios also made akroteria for the Temple of Zeus at Olympia
- The dedicators were Messenians and Naupaktians fighting Athens
- The statue's original marble base survives
- The statue stood on a tall pillar visible throughout the sanctuary
- Nike statues were among the most common Panhellenic dedications

**Educational Summary:** Nike statues demonstrate how military victory was celebrated through religious dedication. Students can analyze how democratic communities expressed success through Panhellenic patronage.

**References:**

### 055. Marble Sima with Lions — Samian Heraion

**Official Name:** Marble Sima from the Samian Heraion with Lion Heads

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Archaic Period (Samian)

**Historical Period:** Early Archaic

**Approximate Date:** circa 570–560 BCE

**Current Museum:** Archaeological Museum of Samos, Greece (Museum Number: Samos 2112)

**Discovery Site:** Heraion of Samos, Samos Island, Greece

**Category:** Temple Architecture — Roof Decoration

**Material:** Samos marble with traces of paint

**Dimensions:** Length: 165 cm (65 in); Height: 55 cm (21.7 in)

**Historical Description:** This marble sima (roof edge) from the great temple of Hera at Samos features lion-head waterspouts and painted decorative bands. The Heraion of Samos was one of the largest and oldest temples in Greece, dedicated to Hera as the island's patron goddess. The temple's elaborate roof decoration, including painted designs and sculpted elements, demonstrated the sanctuary's wealth and the importance of Hera's worship. The lion-head motif, with apotropaic symbolism, protected the sacred space.

**Historical Importance:** Samian roof simae demonstrate the architectural sophistication of Archaic Ionic temples. The Heraion's size and decoration established Samos as a major religious center, attracting pilgrims and dedicators from throughout the Greek world. The temple's innovations influenced Ionic architecture throughout the Mediterranean.

**Discovery Story:** Found during German excavations of the Heraion, which revealed multiple temple buildings spanning centuries of continuous worship.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The Heraion was one of the largest temples in the Greek world
- The original temple was built in the 8th century BCE
- Polycrates of Samos dedicated major improvements to the sanctuary
- The historian Herodotus visited the Heraion
- Pausanias described the sanctuary in the 2nd century CE

**Educational Summary:** Samian simae demonstrate Archaic Ionic temple innovation. Students can analyze how local religious traditions expressed identity through monumental architecture.

**References:**
- Kyrieleis, H. "The Heraion of Samos." Athens: German Archaeological Institute, 1969.

# Educational Framework

## Learning Objectives

1. **Understand Greek Religious Practice:** Analyze the material culture of Greek religion, including temples, altars, votive offerings, and cult statues.

2. **Trace Religious Development:** Examine how Greek religious practices evolved from Minoan and Mycenaean origins through Classical and Hellenistic periods.

3. **Appreciate Pan-Hellenic Institutions:** Recognize how Pan-Hellenic sanctuaries (Delphi, Olympia, Delos) created shared religious experiences across Greek city-states.

4. **Analyze Mystery Cults:** Understand the Eleusinian Mysteries and other secret religious practices that offered initiates transformed understanding of life and death.

5. **Connect Religion and Politics:** Examine how religious institutions supported political authority and how political powers patronized religious sanctuaries.

## Key Themes

- **Temple Architecture and Sacred Space:** How physical structures created environments for religious experience
- **Cult Statues and Divine Presence:** How images materialized divine power for worship
- **Votive Offerings and Piety:** How individuals expressed religious devotion through dedications
- **Oracle and Prophecy:** How divine guidance was sought and interpreted
- **Mystery and Transformation:** How secret initiations offered spiritual experiences beyond public worship
- **Continuity and Change:** How religious practices evolved from Bronze Age to Hellenistic period

## Cross-Curricular Connections

- **History:** Greek religious institutions, Pan-Hellenic games, mystery cults
- **Archaeology:** Sanctuary excavations, votive deposits, temple architecture
- **Art History:** Sculptural evolution from Archaic to Classical periods
- **Philosophy:** Greek religious thought, mystery religions, philosophical theology
- **Literature:** Greek mythology, Homeric hymns, tragedy and religion
