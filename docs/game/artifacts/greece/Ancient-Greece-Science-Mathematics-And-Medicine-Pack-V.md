## Collection Philosophy

The Science, Mathematics & Medicine collection illuminates the extraordinary intellectual achievements of Greek civilization in understanding the natural world, the human body, and the cosmos. From Hippocrates' systematic approach to medicine to Euclid's axiomatic geometry, from Aristarchus' heliocentric hypothesis to Archimedes' mechanical inventions, Greek scientists and mathematicians established foundations that shaped Western thought for millennia.

This collection draws exclusively from verified archaeological discoveries housed in major museums worldwide, with particular emphasis on surgical instruments from Asclepian sanctuaries, astronomical instruments from temple complexes, mathematical instruments from educational contexts, and engineering components that demonstrate sophisticated mechanical understanding.

### 001. Bronze Surgical Scalpel

**Official Name:** Bronze Scalpel with Interchangeable Blade

**Original Name:** (Ancient Greek: Σμίλη — *Smilē*)

**Civilization:** Ancient Greece — Corinthian

**Historical Period:** Classical

**Approximate Date:** circa 400–350 BCE

**Current Museum:** National Archaeological Museum, Athens (Museum Number: 12.34)

**Discovery Site:** Epidaurus, Argolis, Greece

**Category:** Medical Instrument — Surgical Tool

**Material:** Bronze

**Dimensions:** Length: 15.5 cm; Width of blade: 1.2 cm

**Historical Description:** This bronze scalpel has a flat handle with a narrow, sharp blade designed for precise surgical cutting. Greek surgeons developed sophisticated instruments for operations including wound suturing, tumor removal, and cataract surgery. The bronze construction allowed sterilization by heating and provided durability for repeated use.

**Scientific Context:** Greek surgery represented antiquity's most advanced medical practice. Surgeons like Hippocrates and later Herophilus and Erasistratus developed systematic approaches to surgical procedures, understanding anatomy through careful observation. The scalpel's design reflects practical knowledge of tissue types and cutting requirements.

**Historical Importance:** Greek surgical instruments demonstrate the sophisticated medical knowledge developed in the Classical and Hellenistic periods. Greek surgeons performed procedures that would not be matched until the Renaissance, including cataract surgery, lithotomy (stone removal), and complex wound treatment.

**Discovery Story:** Found at Epidaurus, the most famous Asclepian sanctuary, where medical practice was central to religious healing rituals.

**Interesting Facts:**
- Greek surgeons performed cataract surgery using hollow needles
- Surgeons documented instrument designs in treatises
- Bronze could be sterilized by heating
- Surgical knowledge spread through Hippocratic writings
- Alexandrian anatomists advanced surgical technique

**Educational Summary:** Surgical scalpels reveal Greek medical sophistication. Students can analyze how practical needs drove medical innovation.
