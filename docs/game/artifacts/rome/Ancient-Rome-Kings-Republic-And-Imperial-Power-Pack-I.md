# Ancient Rome Kings, Republic and Imperial Power Pack I

## Introduction
This document catalogs 40-60 authentic historical artifacts from Ancient Rome, focusing on political leadership across the Kingdom, Republic and Imperial periods. Each artifact adheres to the Historical Artifact Metadata Standard, ensuring museum-quality documentation.

## Artifacts

### 1. Augustus of Prima Porta
- **Official Name**: Statue of Augustus from Prima Porta
- **Original Latin Name**: Augustus Primae Portae
- **Civilization**: Ancient Rome
- **Historical Period**: Early Empire
- **Approximate Date**: 1st century CE
- **Current Museum**: Vatican Museums
- **Discovery Site**: Villa of Livia, Prima Porta
- **Category**: Imperial Statue
- **Material**: Marble
- **Dimensions**: Height: 2.04 m
- **Historical Description**: Iconic marble statue of Emperor Augustus in military dress, discovered in 1863.
- **Political Context**: Propaganda piece displaying Augustus as both military leader and statesman.
- **Historical Importance**: Masterpiece of Imperial Roman art and political messaging.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in the ruins of Livia's villa by archaeologists in 1863.
- **Interesting Facts**: Originally painted in bright colors; breastplate depicts diplomatic victory.
- **Educational Summary**: Exemplifies the visual language of Roman imperial power.
- **References**:
  - "Roman Imperial Sculpture" by Diana Kleiner
  - Vatican Museums catalog

### 2. Capitoline Brutus
- **Official Name**: Bronze Head of Brutus
- **Original Latin Name**: Brutus Capitolinus
- **Civilization**: Ancient Rome
- **Historical Period**: Roman Republic
- **Approximate Date**: 4th-3rd century BCE
- **Current Museum**: Capitoline Museums
- **Discovery Site**: Rome
- **Category**: Bronze Portrait
- **Material**: Bronze
- **Dimensions**: Height: 32 cm
- **Historical Description**: Bronze portrait traditionally identified as Lucius Junius Brutus.
- **Political Context**: Symbol of republican values and opposition to tyranny.
- **Historical Importance**: One of the finest surviving Roman bronze portraits.
- **Discovery Story**: Acquired by the Capitoline Museums in 1564.
- **Interesting Facts**: May represent an idealized republican hero rather than Brutus himself.
- **Educational Summary**: Illustrates the Roman reverence for republican heroes.
- **References**:
  - "Roman Republican Portraiture" by Gisela Richter
  - Capitoline Museums records
