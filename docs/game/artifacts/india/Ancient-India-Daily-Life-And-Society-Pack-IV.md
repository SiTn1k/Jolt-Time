# Ancient India Daily Life & Society Pack IV

## Introduction
This artifact pack documents 40–60 historically verified artifacts from Ancient India, focusing on themes of daily life, domestic activities, craftsmanship, and social organization. Each artifact adheres to the Historical Artifact Metadata Standard, ensuring museum-quality documentation.

## Artifacts

### 1. Terracotta Bull Cart Toy
- **Original Sanskrit/Prakrit Name**: बालक्रीडा शकट (Balakrīḍā Śakaṭa)
- **Civilization**: Ancient India
- **Historical Period**: Indus Valley Civilization
- **Approximate Date**: 2500–1900 BCE
- **Current Museum**: National Museum, New Delhi
- **Discovery Site**: Mohenjo-Daro
- **Category**: Terracotta Toys
- **Material**: Terracotta
- **Dimensions**: 8 cm long
- **Historical Description**: Miniature bull cart with movable wheels.
- **Daily Life Context**: Children's toy showing importance of bullock carts in transportation.
- **Historical Importance**: Earliest evidence of wheeled toys in South Asia.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in residential area excavations.
- **Interesting Facts**: Wheels were made separately and attached with string.
- **Educational Summary**: Illustrates childhood in ancient urban societies.
- **References**:
  - National Museum Catalogue
  - "Toys of the Indus Valley" by J.M. Kenoyer

### 2. Indus Dancing Girl
- **Original Name**: नर्तकी मूर्ति (Nartakī Mūrti)
- **Civilization**: Ancient India
- **Historical Period**: Indus Valley Civilization
- **Approximate Date**: 2500–1900 BCE
- **Current Museum**: National Museum, New Delhi
- **Discovery Site**: Mohenjo-Daro
- **Category**: Bronze Figurines
- **Material**: Bronze
- **Dimensions**: 10.5 cm tall
- **Historical Description**: Bronze statue of a dancing girl with bangles and elaborate hairstyle.
- **Daily Life Context**: May represent a professional dancer or ritual performer.
- **Historical Importance**: Masterpiece of Indus Valley metalwork.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in 1926 by Ernest Mackay.
- **Interesting Facts**: Made using lost-wax casting technique.
- **Educational Summary**: Shows artistic sophistication and social roles.
- **References**:
  - National Museum Catalogue
  - "The Indus Civilization" by G. Possehl

### 3. Painted Grey Ware Bowl
- **Original Sanskrit Name**: धूसर रंगित भाजन (Dhūsara Raṅgita Bhājana)
- **Civilization**: Ancient India
- **Historical Period**: Vedic Period
- **Approximate Date**: 1000–600 BCE
- **Current Museum**: Mathura Museum
- **Discovery Site**: Hastinapura
- **Category**: Ceramic Bowls
- **Material**: Ceramic
- **Dimensions**: 15 cm diameter
- **Historical Description**: Thin-walled grey bowl with black painted geometric designs.
- **Daily Life Context**: Used for serving food in elite households.
- **Historical Importance**: Characteristic pottery of later Vedic period.
- **Discovery Story**: Found in stratified excavations at Hastinapura.
- **Interesting Facts**: Associated with early iron-using cultures.
- **Educational Summary**: Reflects changing food habits and social stratification.
- **References**:
