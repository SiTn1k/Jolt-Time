# 1. Artifact Philosophy

**Artifacts are not loot. Artifacts are history. Every artifact tells a story. Every artifact has value.**

---

## 1.1 The Nature of Artifacts

Artifacts are not disposable items. They are not currency. They are not fuel. They are windows into human history — tangible connections to real people, real events, and real achievements that shaped our world.

**Every artifact in Jolt Time represents a moment when a human being created something extraordinary.** A potter shaping clay into beauty. A blacksmith forging bronze into tools. A scribe carving knowledge into stone. A scientist unlocking the secrets of the universe. These are not random drops. These are humanity's finest moments, preserved in time.

---

## 1.2 The Three Truths

Every artifact embodies three fundamental truths:

**Historical Truth**  
Every artifact existed. It was made by real people, for real purposes, in a real time and place. We do not create fiction — we rediscover reality.

**Cultural Truth**  
Every artifact carries the weight of its civilization's values, beliefs, and achievements. It tells us who these people were, what they loved, what they feared, what they hoped.

**Human Truth**  
Every artifact is a testament to human creativity, ingenuity, and persistence. It represents the unquenchable human drive to create, to understand, to leave something behind.

---

## 1.3 The Promise We Make

When a player acquires an artifact in Jolt Time, we make a promise:

**This artifact is real. Its story is true. Its place in history is significant.**

We never break this promise. Every artifact that enters a player's collection has been verified, researched, and authenticated. It belongs in a museum because it belongs in history.

---

## 1.4 Why This Philosophy Matters

The artifact philosophy is not decorative — it is foundational.

**It drives respect.** Players treat artifacts differently when they understand their real significance. They do not discard, consume, or trade them casually.

**It creates meaning.** An artifact is not just an image with stats — it is a story waiting to be discovered.

**It enables education.** Learning happens naturally when artifacts carry real historical weight.

**It ensures quality.** If every artifact must tell a true story, we cannot flood the game with meaningless content.

---

# 2. Artifact Purpose

**Why artifacts exist. How they drive progression. How they educate. How they motivate.**

---

## 2.1 Primary Purpose: Historical Connection

Artifacts exist to create a tangible connection between players and human history.

Every artifact is a bridge — a way for players to touch, hold, and understand the past. When a player discovers an artifact, they are not just adding an item to a collection. They are establishing a personal relationship with a moment in history that changed the world.

**The artifact transforms abstract history into concrete experience.**

---

## 2.2 Progression Driver

Artifacts are the primary axis of progression in Jolt Time.

**Collection Progression**  
Completing artifact collections unlocks new museums, new eras, and new content. Collections are the primary long-term goal structure.

**Museum Progression**  
Displaying artifacts in museums generates passive income, research points, and prestige. The museum grows as the collection grows.

**Research Progression**  
Artifacts fuel research projects that unlock new gameplay features, bonuses, and story content.

**Prestige Progression**  
Complete collections, full museums, and rare artifacts determine player rank and recognition.

---

## 2.3 Educational Engine

Artifacts are the primary vehicle for historical education in Jolt Time.

**Contextual Learning**  
Each artifact comes with historical context that teaches players about the era, civilization, and significance of the item.

**Discovery Learning**  
Players learn by discovering. The excitement of finding an artifact motivates engagement with its story.

**Connected Learning**  
Artifacts connect to each other. A player who discovers a Greek amphora becomes curious about Greek pottery, which leads to Greek trade, which leads to the Roman Empire.

**Expert Verification**  
All artifact information is verified by historians and archaeologists. Players learn accurate, valuable information.

---

## 2.4 Motivation Architecture

Artifacts motivate through multiple psychological drivers:

**Curiosity**  
What will I discover next? The unknown pulls players forward.

**Completion**  
I must complete this collection. The missing piece creates tension and drive.

**Pride**  
Look what I found. Rarity and uniqueness create status.

**Knowledge**  
I understand history now. Learning satisfies intellectual curiosity.

**Legacy**  
My museum tells a story. Collection becomes personal expression.

**Community**  
Can you help me find this? Collaboration and trading create social bonds.

# 3. Artifact Categories

**Define high-level categories that organize all artifacts in Jolt Time.**

---

Every artifact belongs to exactly one primary category. Categories define what an artifact IS, not what it looks like or where it came from.

| Category | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|

| **Armor** | Protective equipment | Cuirasses, greaves, gauntlets, chainmail, helmets |
| **Jewelry** | Personal adornments | Rings, necklaces, bracelets, earrings, crowns |

| **Scrolls** | Documents and records | Legal documents, letters, certificates, maps |
| **Paintings** | Visual art and depiction | Frescoes, portraits, murals, miniatures |
| **Statues** | Sculpture and three-dimensional art | Figurines, monuments, reliefs, busts |

| **Music** | Instruments and compositions | Lyres, drums, flutes, bells, tuning forks |
| **Religion** | Spiritual and ceremonial items | Altars, amulets, reliquaries, shrine elements |
| **Military** | Warfare and strategy items | Flags, banners, strategy maps, medals |
| **Daily Life** | Common household items | Pottery, tools, textiles, furniture, utensils |

| **Technology** | Engineering and innovation | Gadgets, machines, devices, prototypes |
| **Transportation** | Movement and travel | Chariots, carts, ship models, harness, stirrups |

---

## 3.2 Category Design Principles

**Mutually Exclusive**  
Each artifact has one primary category. A golden sword is a weapon, not jewelry, even if it has decorative elements.

**Comprehensive**  
All historical artifacts must fit into a category. Categories are exhaustive.

**Meaningful**  
Categories have gameplay implications. Players may specialize in certain categories.

**Discoverable**  
Players can browse by category to understand artifact types.

---

## 3.3 Cross-Category Collections

While artifacts have one primary category, collections often span multiple categories.

**Example: "Tools of the Egyptian Scribe" Collection**
- Primary category: Tools (the writing equipment)
- Includes: Scrolls (the documents), Jewelry (the amulets scribes wore), Daily Life (the ink pots)

This cross-pollination creates richer storytelling and more interesting collection challenges.

# 4. Artifact Hierarchy

**How artifacts are organized from broad categories to specific variations.**

---

## 4.1 The Four-Level Hierarchy

```
Category
    ↓
Collection
    ↓
Artifact
    ↓
Variation
```

---

## 4.2 Level 1: Category

**The broadest organizational unit.**

Categories group artifacts by type and function. Players can browse categories to understand the full scope of human creative achievement.

**Example Categories:**
- Weapons
- Armor
- Jewelry
- Scientific Instruments

---

## 4.3 Level 2: Collection

**Artifacts grouped by theme, story, or historical context.**

Collections are the primary gameplay unit. Players complete collections to progress. Collections tell coherent stories.

**Example Collection: "The Library of Alexandria"**
- Includes artifacts related to the ancient world's greatest repository of knowledge
- Contains: Books, Scrolls, Scientific Instruments, Maps, Statues
- Spans categories but tells one story

**Example Collection: "Weapons of the Samurai"**
- Focused theme: martial tradition of feudal Japan
- Contains: Weapons, Armor, Ceremonial Items
- Tells a complete story of warrior culture

---

## 4.4 Level 3: Artifact

**A specific historical object with a unique identity.**

Each artifact is a unique entry in the catalog. It has a specific name, specific history, and specific characteristics.

**Example Artifact: "Rosetta Stone"**
- Collection: "The Decoding of Hieroglyphics"
- Category: Documents
- Civilization: Ancient Egypt
- Period: 196 BCE
- One unique entry in the game

---

## 4.5 Level 4: Variation

**Different representations of the same artifact.**

Variations represent different versions, conditions, or presentations of the same core artifact.

**Variation Types:**

**Condition Variation**  
Different states of preservation or completeness:
- Pristine (perfect condition)
- Excellent (minor wear)
- Good (moderate wear)
- Fair (significant damage)
- Fragmentary (partial survival)

**Representation Variation**  
Different visual or contextual representations:
- Original scale replica
- Detailed close-up
- Contextual display
- Interactive 3D model

**Source Variation**  
Different discoveries or provenances:
- Original find location A
- Original find location B
- Museum specimen
- Private collection

---

## 4.6 Hierarchy Summary

| Level | Unit | Purpose | Example |
|-------|------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Category | Browse by type | Weapons |
| 2 | Collection | Complete sets, tell stories | Weapons of the Samurai |
| 3 | Artifact | Unique historical object | Katana of Tokugawa Ieyasu |
| 4 | Variation | Different representations | Pristine katana, Fragmentary katana |

# 5. Artifact Rarity Philosophy

**Define rarity tiers and when each should appear. No percentages.**

---

## 5.1 Rarity is Meaning, Not Probability

**Rarity in Jolt Time is not a random number generator.** It is a statement about significance.

A Legendary artifact is not legendary because it has a 1% drop rate. It is legendary because it represents one of the most significant discoveries in human history. The rarity reflects historical importance, not artificial scarcity.

---

## 5.2 The Six Tiers

### Common

**What it represents:** Everyday objects that existed in abundance. The tools, utensils, and items that ordinary people used.

**Historical example:** A Roman terra sigillata bowl. Common because the Romans made millions. Significant because they tell us about daily life.

**When players encounter Common artifacts:**
- Early expeditions in any era
- Foundation pieces for beginning collections
- Learning tools for new players
- Building blocks of larger sets

**Design principle:** Common does not mean worthless. Common artifacts tell important stories about ordinary life.

---

### Uncommon

**What it represents:** Specialized items used by craftspeople, merchants, and skilled workers. Objects that required training or expertise to create.

**Historical example:** A medieval blacksmith's hammer with distinctive decoration. Not everyone could afford one. Surviving examples are relatively common because they were well-made.

**When players encounter Uncommon artifacts:**
- After establishing basic collections
- In mid-tier expeditions
- As completion rewards for Common-heavy sets
- In specialized profession collections

**Design principle:** Uncommon artifacts show the diversity of human skill and specialization.

---

### Rare

**What it represents:** Objects of significant value, importance, or scarcity. Items owned by the wealthy, powerful, or specially honored.

**Historical example:** A Ming Dynasty porcelain vase with imperial markings. Created for the emperor. Surviving examples are genuinely scarce.

**When players encounter Rare artifacts:**
- As rewards for challenging expeditions
- As collection completion milestones
- In era-specific premium collections
- As trading currency among collectors

**Design principle:** Rare artifacts mark genuine achievement. Finding one is a moment of excitement.

---

### Epic

**What it represents:** Objects of extraordinary significance. Items associated with famous figures, major events, or remarkable craftsmanship.

**Historical example:** The death mask of Tutankhamun. Associated with one of history's most dramatic discoveries. Recognized worldwide.

**When players encounter Epic artifacts:**
- As rewards for master-level expeditions
- As completion rewards for major collections
- As milestones in museum development
- As prestige markers

**Design principle:** Epic artifacts are celebrated. They appear rarely but memorably.

---

### Legendary

**What it represents:** Objects that changed history. Items so significant that their discovery rewrote our understanding of human civilization.

**Historical example:** The Cyrus Cylinder. The first declaration of human rights. Studied by scholars worldwide.

**When players encounter Legendary artifacts:**
- As ultimate rewards for dedicated players
- As civilization-defining collection completions
- As museum centerpiece pieces
- As multi-year collection achievements

**Design principle:** Legendary artifacts are the crown jewels. Players aspire to them for years.

---

### Mythic

**What it represents:** Objects of almost supernatural significance. Items that embody humanity's highest ideals, greatest achievements, or most enduring mysteries.

**Historical example:** The Antikythera mechanism. A device so advanced that its discovery seemed impossible. Still not fully understood.

**When players encounter Mythic artifacts:**
- As once-in-a-lifetime discoveries
- As ultimate museum centerpieces
- As civilization-defining achievements
- As community milestones (shared discoveries)

**Design principle:** Mythic artifacts are sacred. They represent the pinnacle of human achievement.

---

## 5.3 Rarity Distribution Guidelines

**No Percentages.** The design team does not assign drop rates. Instead:

**Discovery-Based Acquisition**  
Players find artifacts through expeditions, quests, and achievements. What they find depends on where they go, what they research, and when they explore.

**Era-Appropriate Distribution**  
Some eras had more surviving artifacts than others. Ancient Egypt left more than ancient Persia. This historical reality is reflected in availability.

**Collection Completeness**  
Completing collections increases the rarity of subsequent discoveries in that collection line.

**Player Progression**  
Higher-level players access more significant expeditions where rarer artifacts are more common.

---

## 5.4 Rarity Integrity

**Once an artifact's rarity is set, it never changes.**

We do not inflate rarities. We do not reclassify Legendary artifacts as Mythic to create excitement. The rarity system is based on historical significance, and significance does not change.

**Exception:** If scholarly consensus changes the importance of an artifact, the rarity may be re-evaluated. This is rare and requires design team approval.

# 6. Historical Authenticity Rules

**Every artifact must be historically valid. Real. Referenced. Educational.**

---

## 6.1 The Authenticity Mandate

**No fictional artifacts. No fantasy creations. No imaginary historical objects.**

Every artifact in Jolt Time must be a real object that existed, was discovered, and is documented in the historical record.

This is not a suggestion. It is a foundational principle that separates Jolt Time from fantasy games and ensures our educational mission.

---

## 6.2 Verification Requirements

Before any artifact enters the game, it must meet these requirements:

**Documented Existence**  
The artifact must be documented in credible historical sources. Peer-reviewed papers, museum catalogs, and archaeological reports are acceptable sources. Wikipedia alone is not sufficient.

**Known Provenance**  
We know (or can reasonably infer) where the artifact came from, when it was created, and how it survived to be discovered.

**Physical Reality**  
The artifact is a physical object, not a conceptual or mythological entity. We do not include unicorns, dragons, or mythological beings as artifacts.

**Scholarly Agreement**  
The artifact's authenticity is not disputed by credible scholars. Contested or likely-fake items are excluded.

---

## 6.3 Source Quality Tiers

| Tier | Source Type | Examples | Use |
|------|-------------|----------|-----|
| **Primary** | Museum collections | British Museum, Smithsonian, Louvre | Core artifact data |
| **Secondary** | Academic publications | Peer-reviewed journals, university press | Historical context |
| **Tertiary** | Authoritative databases | Getty Vocabularies, UNESCO lists | Classification and metadata |

---

## 6.4 Authenticity in Practice

**Real Example: The Bimaran Casket**

*Status:* Real artifact  
*Discovery:* Found in Bimaran monastery, Afghanistan  
*Current location:* British Museum  
*Verification:* Multiple scholarly sources, widely accepted authenticity  
*Entry into game:* Approved

**Rejected Example: The Holy Grail**

*Status:* Mythological object  
*Reason:* No physical artifact exists. Mentioned in texts but never found. Cannot be verified.  
*Alternative:* We include documented chalices from actual archaeological finds.

**Rejected Example: Excalibur**

*Status:* Legendary weapon  
*Reason:* No physical evidence exists. King Arthur is mythology, not history.  
*Alternative:* We include documented Celtic and early medieval swords with historical significance.

---

## 6.5 The Educational Responsibility

Historical authenticity is not just a design principle — it is an educational responsibility.

**Players trust us to teach them truth.** When we say an artifact is real, we are making a promise. Breaking that promise would undermine our entire educational mission.

**Real history is more interesting than fiction.** The truth about the Antikythera mechanism is more fascinating than any fictional device. The real story of the Rosetta Stone is more dramatic than any myth.

**Accuracy enables deeper learning.** When artifacts are real, players can research them further, visit museums, and deepen their knowledge beyond the game.

# 7. Artifact Narrative

**Each artifact contains rich narrative information that tells its story.**

---

## 7.1 Required Narrative Fields

Every artifact includes these narrative elements:

### Historical Name

**What it is called in the historical record.**

The name by which the artifact is known in academic and museum contexts.

*Example:* "Rosetta Stone"

---

### Original Name

**What the people who made it called it.**

The name the artifact's creators would have used, in their language and script.

*Example:* "Stone of the Decree" (Ancient Egyptian: ዓለም የሚሽር)

---

### Civilization

**The culture that created the artifact.**

The specific civilization, empire, or culture responsible for the artifact's creation.

*Example:* Ancient Egypt, Roman Empire, Han Dynasty

---

### Period

**When the artifact was created.**

The historical time period, including century and, where known, exact dates.

*Example:* 196 BCE, Ptolemaic Period

---

### Story

**The artifact's place in history.**

A compelling narrative that explains what this artifact means, why it matters, and what it tells us about the people who made it.

*Example:* "The Rosetta Stone is a granodiorite stele inscribed with three versions of a decree issued in Memphis, Egypt in 196 BCE during the Ptolemaic dynasty. The decree appears to re-confirm the royal cult of the new king Ptolemy V. The stone is famous because it was the key to deciphering Egyptian hieroglyphs, unlocking thousands of years of recorded history."

---

### Importance

**Why this artifact is significant.**

A clear explanation of the artifact's historical, cultural, or scholarly importance.

*Example:* "Without the Rosetta Stone, we would not be able to read any ancient Egyptian hieroglyphic texts. It is the single most important artifact for understanding ancient Egypt."

---

### Discovery

**How we found it.**

The story of the artifact's rediscovery by modern archaeology.

*Example:* "Found in 1799 by soldiers of Napoleon's expedition near the town of Rashid (Rosetta) in the Nile Delta. After Napoleon's defeat, the stone was transferred to British possession in 1801 and has been housed in the British Museum since 1802."

---

### Trivia

**Interesting facts that delight.**

Lesser-known facts that make the artifact more memorable and engaging.

*Example:* "The Rosetta Stone was actually part of a larger stele. The top and sides are broken off. Scholars estimate the complete stone would have been about twice the size of the surviving fragment."

---

## 7.2 Narrative Quality Standards

**Accuracy**  
All narratives are fact-checked by subject matter experts. No embellishment, no fabrication.

**Engagement**  
Narratives are written to be read. They are not dry academic descriptions. They tell stories.

**Length**  
Narratives are substantial but not overwhelming. 100-300 words for the Story field. Players should want to read them.

**Spoiler Control**  
Discovery narratives can be unlocked through gameplay, not revealed immediately. Players earn the full story.

---

## 7.3 Progressive Disclosure

Narrative information is revealed progressively:

**Immediate (on discovery):**  
Historical name, civilization, period, thumbnail image

**After basic research:**  
Original name, importance summary, discovery story

**After full research:**  
Complete narrative, trivia, related artifacts, further reading

This creates ongoing engagement and rewards continued attention to individual artifacts.

# 8. Collection Philosophy

**Artifacts belong to collections. Collections belong to museums. Collections tell complete stories.**

---

## 8.1 Collections as Stories

Every collection in Jolt Time tells a coherent story. It is not a random grouping of artifacts — it is a curated narrative about a specific theme, civilization, era, or aspect of human history.

**Example: "The Seven Wonders of the Ancient World"**
- Collection story: An exploration of the most remarkable achievements of the ancient Mediterranean world
- Artifacts: Models, plans, and recovered fragments of each wonder
- Completion message: "You have explored humanity's first greatest hits"

**Example: "Daily Life in Pompeii"**
- Collection story: A snapshot of ordinary Roman life, frozen in time
- Artifacts: Items recovered from the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 CE
- Completion message: "You have touched the ordinary moments that became extraordinary"

---

## 8.2 Collection Size Guidelines

| Collection Type | Artifact Count | Design Purpose |
|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Starter** | 3-5 artifacts | Tutorial, first collection |
| **Standard** | 7-12 artifacts | Core gameplay, moderate challenge |
| **Extended** | 15-25 artifacts | Deep dives, dedicated interest |
| **Master** | 30+ artifacts | Ultimate challenges, years to complete |

---

## 8.3 Collection Completion Rewards

Completing a collection provides:

**Immediate Rewards:**
- Research points
- Museum display space unlock
- Collection-specific bonus
- Narrative completion achievement

**Deferred Rewards:**
- Access to next collection tier
- Era progression advancement
- Prestige points

---

## 8.4 Museum Integration

Collections are not standalone — they belong to museums.

**Each museum specializes in specific collection types.**

- **The British Museum Collection:** Artifacts from the British Museum's global holdings
- **The Archaeological Collection:** Excavated artifacts with discovery narratives
- **The Artistic Collection:** Paintings, sculptures, and decorative arts
- **The Scientific Collection:** Instruments, inventions, and technological achievements

This integration creates deeper organizational logic and rewards museum development.

---

# 9. Completion Philosophy

**Players should want to complete sets, museums, civilizations, and eras.**

---

## 9.1 The Completion Hierarchy

Players pursue completion at multiple levels simultaneously:

**Artifact Completion**  
Collect one of every variation of a specific artifact.

**Collection Completion**  
Complete every artifact in a collection.

**Museum Completion**  
Fill every display slot in a museum.

**Civilization Completion**  
Complete every collection from a single civilization.

**Era Completion**  
Complete every collection from a historical period.

**Global Completion**  
The ultimate achievement: complete everything.

---

## 9.2 Why Completion Matters

Completion creates:

**Direction**  
Players always know what to pursue next. The completion hierarchy provides constant goals.

**Meaningful Progress**  
Every artifact contributes to a larger whole. Nothing is wasted.

**Pride and Recognition**  
Complete collectors earn badges, titles, and recognition.

**Community Connection**  
Players help each other complete rare pieces through trading and gifting.

---

## 9.3 Completion Incentives

**Soft Incentives (always available):**
- Completion badges and titles
- Leaderboard rankings by completion percentage
- Research point bonuses for complete collections

**Hard Incentives (gate content):**
- New eras unlocked only at collection completion
- Special expeditions available only to complete museums
- Elite PvP leagues require civilization completion

---

## 9.4 Anti-Frustration Measures

Completion should be challenging, not frustrating.

**Bad Luck Protection**  
No artifact has infinite drop rarity. Every artifact is eventually findable.

**Trading System**  
Missing pieces can be traded with other players.

**Discovery Reset**  
After a cooldown period, previously discovered artifacts become "re-discoverable" for players who want additional copies.

**Completion Assistance**  
Dedicated players can help newer players, creating community bonds.

---

# 10. Educational Philosophy

**Artifacts teach naturally. No boring text. History through gameplay.**

---

## 10.1 Learning Without Realizing

The best education is invisible. Players do not feel like they are studying — they feel like they are exploring, discovering, and collecting.

**Design principle:** Every interaction with an artifact teaches something. Players absorb history through repeated, engaging exposure.

---

## 10.2 Learning Pathways

Artifacts create natural learning pathways:

**Linear Discovery**  
Player discovers Artifact A → learns about topic → becomes curious → discovers Artifact B → learns more → becomes invested.

**Comparative Learning**  
Player notices similarities between artifacts from different eras → asks questions → discovers connections → understands broader patterns.

**Deep Dive**  
Player becomes fascinated by one artifact → reads full narrative → researches further → becomes local expert → teaches others.

---

## 10.3 Contextual Information Architecture

**Surface Level (always visible):**  
- Artifact name
- Civilization and period
- Category
- Rarity

**Detail Level (on inspection):**  
- Original name
- Basic significance
- Thumbnail image

**Research Level (unlocked through gameplay):**  
- Full narrative
- Discovery story
- Related artifacts
- Museum location

**Expert Level (for dedicated learners):**  
- Scholarly sources
- Academic debates
- Ongoing research
- unanswered questions

---

## 10.4 Gamification of Learning

Learning itself is gamified:

**Research Projects**  
Unlocking artifact narratives requires completing research missions, not just clicking.

**Knowledge Challenges**  
Players test their historical knowledge and earn rewards for correct answers.

**Expert Titles**  
Players who master specific eras earn titles and recognition.

**Teaching Mechanics**  
Players can share artifact knowledge with friends, creating teaching moments.

# 12. Artifact Progression

**The lifecycle of an artifact from discovery to mastery.**

---

## 12.1 The Five Stages

```
Discovery → Research → Restoration → Exhibition → Mastery
```

---

## 12.2 Stage 1: Discovery

**The artifact enters the player's possession.**

**What happens:**
- Player finds the artifact during an expedition
- Basic information is revealed (name, category, civilization)
- Artifact enters inventory
- Player can view the artifact but not its story

**Player actions:**
- Decide to keep or trade
- Begin research
- Add to wish list

**Design intent:** Discovery creates excitement. The artifact is new, unknown, full of potential.

---

## 12.3 Stage 2: Research

**The player uncovers the artifact's story.**

**What happens:**
- Player spends research points or time
- Narrative information is progressively revealed
- Related artifacts are suggested
- Collection connections are identified

**Player actions:**
- Invest research resources
- Choose what to learn first
- Share discoveries with friends

**Design intent:** Research transforms an object into a story. Players earn knowledge.

---

## 12.4 Stage 3: Restoration

**The artifact is prepared for display.**

**What happens:**
- Player invests resources in restoration
- Artifact visual improves (condition upgrade)
- Display value increases
- Restoration narrative is unlocked

**Player actions:**
- Choose restoration level
- Decide on display context
- Complete restoration challenges

**Design intent:** Restoration creates ownership. Players invest in their artifacts.

---

## 12.5 Stage 4: Exhibition

**The artifact is displayed in the museum.**

**What happens:**
- Artifact is placed in museum display
- Passive rewards are generated
- Collection progress advances
- Display bonuses are applied

**Player actions:**
- Choose exhibition location
- Curate display theme
- Optimize placement for bonuses

**Design intent:** Exhibition creates legacy. The museum becomes a personal expression.

---

## 12.6 Stage 5: Mastery

**The player becomes an expert on this artifact.**

**What happens:**
- All narrative content is unlocked
- Special mastery bonuses are earned
- Related achievements are granted
- Expert title is awarded

**Player actions:**
- Showcase expertise
- Help others learn
- Pursue mastery of related artifacts

**Design intent:** Mastery creates prestige. Expert collectors are recognized.

---

# 13. Artifact Upgrades

**High-level only. Research. Restoration. Knowledge. Preservation. Never power creep.**

---

## 13.1 Upgrade Philosophy

Artifacts in Jolt Time do not become stronger. They become more understood.

**No power creep.** An artifact from 196 BCE does not become more powerful as you upgrade it. Instead, it becomes more valuable as a historical piece.

**Upgrades are knowledge-based.** Upgrading an artifact means understanding it better, not making it better.

---

## 13.2 The Four Upgrade Types

### Research

**Unlocking narrative and information.**

Research upgrades reveal more about the artifact's history, significance, and story.

**Upgrade progression:**
1. Basic identification (name, category)
2. Historical context (period, civilization)
3. Original name and native context
4. Full narrative and discovery story
5. Expert knowledge and scholarly debates

**What changes:** Information availability, not artifact stats.

---

### Restoration

**Improving condition and presentation.**

Restoration upgrades improve the artifact's physical condition and visual presentation.

**Upgrade progression:**
1. Fragmentary (as found)
2. Fair (partially restored)
3. Good (major pieces joined)
4. Excellent (near complete)
5. Pristine (museum quality)

**What changes:** Visual presentation, display value, collection completion bonus.

---

### Knowledge

**Understanding significance.**

Knowledge upgrades represent the player's growing understanding of the artifact's place in history.

**Upgrade progression:**
1. Novice (surface knowledge)
2. Apprentice (basic understanding)
3. Journeyman (solid comprehension)
4. Expert (deep knowledge)
5. Master (scholarly expertise)

**What changes:** Knowledge-based achievements, teaching ability, bonus research speed.

---

### Preservation

**Ensuring long-term survival.**

Preservation upgrades represent care and maintenance of the physical artifact.

**Upgrade progression:**
1. At risk (damaged, unstable)
2. Vulnerable (needs attention)
3. Stable (maintained)
4. Protected (well cared for)
5. Preserved (museum quality care)

**What changes:** Passive preservation bonuses, museum stability bonuses.

---

## 13.3 No Power Creep Guarantee

**Artifacts do not provide combat bonuses.**  
Artifacts do not increase player stats.  
Artifacts do not grant gameplay advantages.  
Artifacts do not affect PvP outcomes.

**Artifacts provide:**
- Collection completion progress
- Museum display value
- Research point generation
- Prestige and recognition
- Educational satisfaction

This guarantee is absolute. No update, no expansion, no feature may introduce power creep through artifacts.

---

# 14. Artifact Economy

**Artifacts are collected, researched, displayed, and shared. They are never consumed.**

---

## 14.1 The Four Pillars

```
Collect → Research → Display → Share
```

---

## 14.2 Collection

**Artifacts are collected, not acquired.**

The language matters. Players do not "get" artifacts — they collect them. Collection implies permanence, curation, and value.

**Collection principles:**
- Every artifact has permanent value
- Duplicate artifacts have reduced but nonzero value
- Collection progress is never reversed
- Complete collections unlock permanent rewards

---

## 14.3 Research

**Artifacts fuel knowledge creation.**

Research transforms artifacts into understanding. The artifact is not consumed — it remains in the collection while generating research points.

**Research principles:**
- Research generates persistent rewards
- Research speed scales with collection mastery
- Research unlocks narrative content
- Research connects artifacts to each other

---

## 14.4 Display

**Artifacts are displayed, not stored.**

The museum is not a warehouse — it is a gallery. Players display artifacts to generate value, not hide them in inventory.

**Display principles:**
- Display slots must be earned through progression
- Display quality affects rewards
- Curated displays generate bonuses
- Full museums are the ultimate goal

---

## 14.5 Sharing

**Artifacts connect players to each other.**

Players share artifacts through trading, gifting, and community displays. No artifact is孤.

**Sharing principles:**
- Trading is balanced to prevent exploitation
- Gifting creates community bonds
- Community museums showcase collective achievement
- Shared discoveries create shared memories

---

## 14.6 What Artifacts Are NOT

Artifacts are not:

**Currency**  
Artifacts cannot be sold for gold or premium currency. They have collection value, not economic value.

**Consumables**  
Artifacts are never used up. Research does not destroy them. Display does not damage them.

**Weapons**  
Artifacts do not provide combat stats. They are historical objects, not power sources.

**Trade goods**  
Artifacts cannot be converted into other resources. Their value is intrinsic.

---

# 15. Artifact Ownership

**Players own history. Players own knowledge. Players own collections. Players own museums. Players do not own disposable items.**

---

## 15.1 What Players Own

**Their Collection**  
Every artifact a player has discovered belongs to them permanently. Even if they trade it away, their discovery record remains.

**Their Knowledge**  
Research progress is personal. Knowledge about artifacts cannot be taken away.

**Their Museum**  
The museum a player builds is uniquely theirs. No reset, no wipe, no loss.

**Their Progress**  
Collection completion, era mastery, and prestige are permanent achievements.

---

## 15.2 What Players Do Not Own

**The Historical Record**  
History belongs to everyone. Artifacts are discoveries, not creations.

**Unique Artifacts**  
When a player trades away an artifact, they lose access to it. But the artifact's information remains in the global knowledge base.

**Other Players' Collections**  
Respect for other players' property is absolute. No theft, no griefing, no theft of progress.

---

## 15.3 Ownership Guarantees

Jolt Time makes these promises:

**Permanent Ownership**  
No update, no change, no emergency will erase player collections. If a player has it today, they have it tomorrow.

**Secure Ownership**  
Accounts are secure. Collections cannot be stolen through glitches or exploits.

**Transferable Ownership**  
Players can trade artifacts with each other. Ownership transfers completely.

**Legacy Ownership**  
If a player stops playing, their collection remains viewable. They can return at any time.

---

# 16. Future Expansion Rules

**Adding new civilizations, museums, collections, and artifact types without breaking previous content.**

---

## 16.1 Expansion Principles

**Backward Compatibility**  
Every expansion works with existing content. New artifacts fit the existing framework.

**Forward Compatibility**  
New content never invalidates old content. Completed collections remain complete.

**Incremental Value**  
New expansions add to existing value, not replace it.

---

## 16.2 New Civilizations

When adding a new civilization:

**Classification First**  
Define the civilization's category, period, and geographic region before adding artifacts.

**Era Integration**  
Place the civilization in historical context. Connect it to adjacent eras.

**Museum Assignment**  
Determine which museums will house the civilization's collections.

**Collection Design**  
Create collections that tell the civilization's story coherently.

**Verification**  
All artifacts must meet Historical Authenticity Rules before inclusion.

---

## 16.3 New Museums

When adding a new museum:

**Theme Clarity**  
Define the museum's unique focus and purpose.

**Collection Assignment**  
Determine which collections belong to this museum.

**Integration Points**  
Define how this museum connects to existing museums.

**Display Requirements**  
Determine display slot costs and progression.

---

## 16.4 New Collections

When adding a new collection:

**Story Coherence**  
The collection must tell a clear, compelling story.

**Completion Feasibility**  
All artifacts must be obtainable within reasonable time.

**Reward Structure**  
Completion rewards must be defined before release.

**Integration**  
Collections must connect to museums and eras.

---

## 16.5 New Artifact Types

When adding new artifact categories:

**Framework Compatibility**  
The new category must work with existing metadata schema.

**Hierarchy Integration**  
The category must fit the Category → Collection → Artifact → Variation structure.

**Narrative Requirements**  
All required narrative fields must be applicable.

**Rarity Classification**  
New categories must follow existing rarity principles.

---

## 16.6 Content Longevity

**Existing content never becomes obsolete.** When new eras are added, old eras remain valuable. When new collections appear, old collections remain completable and rewarding.

**Seasonal content complements permanent content.** Events and seasons add variety without replacing core progression.

**Player investment is protected.** Every hour spent, every artifact collected, every museum built remains valuable.

---

# 17. Things We NEVER Do

**These rules are absolute. They are never crossed.**

---

## 17.1 No Fake Artifacts

**We never create artifacts that did not exist.**

Every artifact in Jolt Time is a real object documented in the historical record. No exceptions. No "inspired by real history" — only actual history.

---

## 17.2 No Fantasy Artifacts

**We never include mythical, fictional, or legendary objects as functional artifacts.**

Excalibur is not in the game. The Holy Grail is not in the game. The Ark of the Covenant is not in the game. We include real artifacts that scholars can study.

---

## 17.3 No Fictional History

**We never create fake backstories, fake discoveries, or fake significance.**

All narratives are based on documented history. We do not invent dramatic stories to make artifacts more interesting. Real history is more interesting than fiction.

---

## 17.4 No Random Rarity Inflation

**We never increase artifact rarity to create artificial excitement.**

When we classify an artifact as Legendary, it remains Legendary. We do not reclassify artifacts to manipulate player behavior.

---

## 17.5 No Meaningless Duplicates

**We never create duplicate artifacts just to extend gameplay time.**

If an artifact exists, one instance is sufficient. We do not create multiple versions of the same artifact to force longer collection times.

---

## 17.6 No Pay-to-Obtain Artifacts

**We never put artifacts behind paywalls.**

Artifacts can only be obtained through gameplay. No purchasing artifacts directly. No buying collection completion. Ever.

---

## 17.7 No Power Through Artifacts

**We never let artifacts affect gameplay power.**

Artifacts do not make players stronger in combat, in competition, or in any measurable gameplay metric. Artifacts are for collection, display, and education only.

---

## 17.8 No Artifact Consumption

**We never allow artifacts to be consumed, destroyed, or converted.**

Artifacts are permanent. Research does not destroy them. Upgrades do not diminish them. They cannot be traded away without consent.

---
